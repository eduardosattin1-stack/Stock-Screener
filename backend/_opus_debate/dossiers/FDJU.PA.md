# FDJ United (FDJU.PA) — A Deep Dive on Management and the Setup

*Forensic dossier — three consecutive results calls (2024 full-year on 2025-03-06; 2025 half-year on 2025-07-30; 2025 full-year on 2026-02-19) cross-referenced against the screener's hard financials.*

## 1. Narrative Arc

The story FDJ United tells about itself changed shape completely over these three calls, and the change is the whole point. In March 2025, presenting 2024 results, the message was triumphant and offensive: a French national-lottery champion that had just bought Kindred (a pan-European online betting and gaming group), rebranded as "FDJ United," and was now "a balanced group, more diversified both geographically, in terms of activities, in terms of channels." Management led with the acquisition, the Olympic sponsorship, a 15% dividend increase, and a clean resolution of the long-running European Commission state-aid investigation (no state aid found; a one-time €97 million additional payment booked). The tone was that of a company that had just leveled up.

By the July 2025 half-year call the language had quietly turned defensive. The headline number — revenue "down 2% on a restated basis" — was immediately wrapped in excuses: a "tough comparable" against Kindred's "best quarter ever" during the Euro 2024 football tournament. The new "Play Forward 2028" plan was introduced, and with it a pivot in vocabulary from growth to *efficiency*: a €20 million cost plan, margin defense, and repeated reassurance that 2025 was "obviously a transition year."

By February 2026, presenting 2025 results, the defensive posture was fully entrenched. Chairwoman Stéphane Pallez opened by conceding that "our performance, our results were significantly impacted by major adverse factors" — gaming-tax hikes in France, the Netherlands and Romania (over €50 million of combined revenue-and-profit hit) and "ill-considered" Dutch regulation that shrank the legal market "by around 25%." The growth story had been replaced by a survival-and-self-help story: the cost plan was raised from €120 million to €150 million by 2028 (and pulled a year ahead), and the medium-term 5% revenue growth target was pushed out to a *2028* exit rate. In two years the narrative went from "European champion expanding" to "resilient operator absorbing a tax-and-regulation storm while cutting costs."

The central forensic finding: revenue and free cash flow tell a stable-to-growing story, but *earnings* tell a sharp-decline story. The screener flags it directly — EPS down 56% year-on-year, a five-year EPS compound annual decline of about 11%, even as revenue compounded ~14%. That divergence is the Kindred deal: it bought top-line scale and cash flow but loaded the income statement with financing cost, purchase-price amortization, and exposure to a higher-tax, more-regulated online segment.

## 3. Tone & Confidence Trajectory

**Trajectory: Declining (from offensive to defensive).**

- **Mar-2025 — confident, offensive.** "We want to be a European champion in the gaming industry." Heavy emphasis on expansion, the acquisition, the Olympics, dividend growth.
- **Jul-2025 — guarded, transitional.** "2025 is obviously a transition year." Revenue contraction reframed as a comparison artifact; the cost plan introduced; "we are definitely confident in our capacity to achieve our full-year guidance" — the word "confident" now attached to *guidance defense* rather than growth.
- **Feb-2026 — defensive, self-help.** "It is fair to recognize that in '25, our performance… [was] significantly impacted by major adverse factors." Language dominated by cost efficiency, regulation, "preserve its fundamentals," "meet our financial commitments." The shift from investing-for-growth to protecting-the-margin is complete.

Specificity actually *improved* over time on costs (precise tax breakdowns by jurisdiction, a quantified €150 million plan), which reads as credible candor — but it improved precisely because the news got worse and management had to show its homework.

## 4. Guidance Credibility

**Reliability: Medium-High.** Management has been consistent and has delivered against its own (lowered) bars. The €20 million 2025 cost target was not just met but "significantly exceeded, reaching approximately €50 million," and the multi-year plan was raised and accelerated — over-delivery on the self-help leg. They flagged the 2025 tax and regulatory hits early and the full-year results landed "in line with our expected statutory." The screener's beat rate of 3 of 4 quarters (75%) corroborates a generally reliable team. The credibility ding is on the *original* growth framing: the 5% medium-term growth target has effectively become a 2028-exit-rate aspiration rather than an average, and 2026 is guided only to "slight growth in revenue" against a further ~€90 million tax headwind. They deliver what they promise, but they have repeatedly had to lower what they promise.

## 5. Analyst Pressure Points

- **Where does growth come from?** (Christian Devismes, CIC, Mar-25): the 2-3 point gap between 2025 growth and the long-term 4-5% target. Management attributed it to tax and regulatory drag — which proved accurate, and recurred.
- **When do the cost savings actually show up?** (Sabrina Blanc, Bernstein, Jul-25): management repeatedly pushed the real benefit to "2026 and more heavily in 2027," tied to the in-house sports-betting platform (KSP) rollout and ending the Kambi licensing cost "by the end of 2026." This is a recurring deflection — the payoff is always one to two years out.
- **UK and Netherlands regulation** (Ed Young, Morgan Stanley; others): management could only say the Netherlands is "stable" with "no news," and that the UK voluntary code application was a positive — while a fresh ~€30 million UK tax hit (rising to €40 million in 2027) was later confirmed. Analysts pushed on regulation; regulation kept turning into bad news.
- **Online casino in France** (Ed Young, Jul-25): management dampened hopes — "very low probability of consultations to come back… because of the political environment." A potential upside optionality quietly taken off the table.

## 6. Red Flags / Green Flags

🔴 Net income halved in 2025 (€399M → €176M; EPS €2.16 → €0.95) — the earnings base genuinely shrank, not just optically.
🔴 Serial regulatory/tax escalation: France, Netherlands, Romania in 2025; UK and France-annualization adding ~€90M more in 2026. This is a recurring, not one-off, headwind in the online segment.
🔴 The growth target keeps receding — 4-5% "long-term" became a 2028 exit rate; 2026 is only "slight growth."
🔴 Cost-plan payoff perpetually "one to two years out" (2026/2027) — execution risk on the KSP platform migration is the load-bearing assumption.
🔴 Gross margin contracting; the higher-tax, lower-margin online segment is a larger share of the mix post-Kindred.

🟢 Free cash flow is robust and real — TTM €923M, P/FCF 7.4x, ~80%+ EBITDA-to-cash conversion. The cash engine is intact.
🟢 The French lottery concession (LSF) is a genuine quasi-monopoly: +5% revenue growth, ~36% EBITDA margin, iLottery up 16% — a true wide-moat franchise.
🟢 Cost self-help over-delivered (€50M vs €20M target) and was raised to €150M by 2028 — management is executing the controllable lever.
🟢 The European Commission state-aid overhang was resolved cleanly (no state aid; €97M paid and booked) — a real uncertainty removed.
🟢 Deleveraging on track, leverage under 2x, investment-grade (Moody's Baa1 stable).

## 7. Hidden Signals

The most telling terminology shift is from "champion / expansion / diversification" toward "mutualization, automatization, internalization, performance plan" — the vocabulary of a company squeezing an acquired asset, not growing a franchise. Management now openly distinguishes the two halves of the business: the French exclusive-rights lottery (LSF) is described as "the cash generator," while the online open-to-competition segment (OBG) is framed entirely around tech migration, brand consolidation, and cost — never around growth. That internal framing is, in effect, management telling you the parts are worth very different things. Note also the emphasis on AI as a *cost* tool ("70% of direct marketing campaigns automated," customer-service costs down 10% on 20% more interactions) rather than a revenue driver — consistent with a defensive posture. On capital allocation, management explicitly de-prioritized mergers and acquisitions ("not a significant priority for OBG"), signaling they intend to digest Kindred and pay the dividend rather than expand further.

## 8. Capital-Allocation Verdict (for the director)

**Classification: PIVOTING (from growth-by-acquisition to integration-and-cost-discipline).**

The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: **this is two businesses bolted together, and they deserve two very different valuations.** The French lottery (LSF) is a regulatory-license, quasi-utility crown jewel — wide moat, ~36% margins, mid-single-digit growth, the source of three-quarters of revenue and the bulk of profit. The online betting-and-gaming arm (OBG, essentially Kindred) is a lower-margin, competition-exposed, tax-and-regulation-battered business whose terminal economics are genuinely uncertain. The market is pricing the whole company near a 52-week low and at 7.4x cash flow as if the online segment's troubles contaminate the lottery — which they do not. The risk is that the cost plan disappoints or that online tax/regulation keeps escalating; the opportunity is that the lottery alone may be worth close to the entire current price.

**Moat:** The consolidated business is NARROW — the lottery is WIDE (an exclusive French concession, the textbook regulatory-license moat), but it is blended with an online segment that has little durable moat and is exposed to competition and regulatory whim. **Moat trend: ERODING** at the consolidated level — ROIC fell from quasi-utility heights toward 16.7% and gross margin is contracting as the lower-quality online mix grows and taxes rise. **Secular threat: manageable.** This is not a structurally dying base — lottery demand is durable and the online tax pressure, while real and recurring, is a margin-and-regulation problem, not a terminal-demand collapse. The cheapness is closer to a fear-print on a mixed-quality conglomerate than a value trap on a melting franchise, *provided* the lottery's economics hold.
