# Forensic Dossier — CECONOMY AG (CEC.DE)

*MediaMarkt / Saturn — Europe's largest big-box consumer-electronics retailer, holding company listed in Germany. Three earnings calls cover the run-up to and the early stages of JD.com's cash takeover (December 2025 full-year results, February 2026 Q1, May 2026 Q2/half-year).*

**The one fact that overrides everything below:** CECONOMY is no longer a free-floating equity to be valued on its fundamentals. JD.com (the Chinese e-commerce group) has launched a binding all-cash takeover at €4.60 per share, and together with the founding-family vehicle Convergenta it already controls 85.2% of the company. The stock trades a few cents under the offer (~€4.40-4.45) as a near-completed merger-arbitrage — that is, a bet on the deal closing, not on the retailer's earnings power. The screener's "deep value" tags (price 3.7, price-to-free-cash-flow of 2.4x, a discounted-cash-flow fair value of 21.1) are stale artifacts that ignore the €4.60 cap. Read the rest of this dossier as background on deal-break risk, not as a thesis for owning the business outright.

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

Across the three calls the operating story is strikingly consistent — almost rehearsed — because management's real job in this window is to keep the business stable while the takeover clears. The refrain never changes: a three-year transformation from "classical retailer" into an "omnichannel service platform," "experience electronics," and a now-roughly-€1-billion bundle of higher-margin "growth businesses" (Services & Solutions, Marketplace, Retail Media, Private Label, Space-as-a-Service). The boast repeated every quarter is consecutive adjusted-operating-profit growth — "11 quarters" in December, "12th quarter in a row" in February, still climbing in May.

What actually evolves is the JD.com thread. In December the deal is "signed" with 85.2% secured and closing guided to "the first half of 2026." By February management is fielding pointed questions on the specific regulatory gates (merger control done; foreign-direct-investment reviews and the EU foreign-subsidies review still open) and reaffirming "first half of 2026." By May the language softens to "second half of the calendar year… but obviously before November 10, which is the long-stop date" — a quiet slip of roughly half a year in the expected close, plus a fresh detail that delisting would follow "between 3 and 6 months after closing." The CEO also announced his own departure in May ("my last earnings call personally"), with the CFO named successor — normal for a company being absorbed, but worth noting.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

The gap between the upbeat call narrative and the screener's reported financials is wide, and it is mostly explained by one word: *adjusted*.

| Management claim (call) | What the financials show | Read |
|---|---|---|
| "Sales reached €23.1 billion… growth of 5.7%" (Dec) | Revenue €23.07B, up ~2.8% reported | Currency/portfolio-adjusted growth is higher than the headline reported figure; both are low-single-digit. Honest, if flattered. |
| "We grew EBIT by 24% to €378 million" and "12 quarters of profit growth" | Reported net income **-€35M in FY2025** (a loss), net margin -0.2%, latest EPS -€0.0721; trailing-twelve-month EPS -€0.13 | The "EBIT" they tout is *adjusted* operating profit. Reported earnings are negative, dragged by JD-related one-offs (€32M) and Poland restructuring. The "12 quarters of growth" story is real but sits on top of a company that still loses money on a reported basis. |
| "We increased free cash flow by 180%… €337 million" (Dec); "free cash flow… €1.4 billion" (Q1) | Trailing-twelve-month free cash flow €739M; the €1.4B Q1 figure is a seasonal working-capital swing, not run-rate | Free cash flow is genuinely the brightest number here, but the eye-catching quarterly figures are seasonal (peak-season payables) and reverse out — Q2 free cash flow was *-€165M*. |
| "Around €500 million in adjusted EBIT" target for FY25-26 | Q1 already delivered €311M (64% of the year, by their own seasonality math) | The target looks achievable, but it is an *adjusted* target on a business whose reported bottom line is still around break-even. |
| Returns / quality | Return on invested capital just **1.8%**, return on equity 10.6%, Piotroski score 4/9, Altman-Z solvency 2.2, net debt ~€1.6B | This is the tell. Even on the company's best framing, capital returns barely clear — and likely do not clear — the cost of capital. Five-year book value per share has *shrunk* ~15.7% a year. |

Bottom line: the operating narrative is richer than the reported numbers justify, because it leans entirely on adjusted operating profit and seasonal cash peaks while the reported business hovers around break-even with sub-cost-of-capital returns. None of this matters much to the *price*, which is pinned to €4.60 — but it matters enormously to deal-break risk, because a thin, low-return retailer is exactly the kind of asset that re-rates sharply *lower* if the bid were to fall away.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Stable-to-rising on operations, deliberately careful on the deal.** The operating tone is uniformly confident and on-message every quarter — "we've delivered," "stress test… we have successfully completed," "on the finishing stretch." This is not a management team under visible strain on the business.

On the takeover, the tone is studiously controlled and, by May, faintly more guarded: in February the CEO says "I cannot give you any color" on the Austrian foreign-investment review and the foreign-subsidies process; in May he repeats "these are very sensitive discussions, the confidentiality of which we respect 100%." The shift from "first half of 2026" (Dec/Feb) to "second half of the calendar year… before November 10" (May) is the single most important tonal datapoint in the file — a timeline slip delivered calmly but unmistakably.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Medium-to-High on operations; the one slip is on deal timing.** On the business, management set a €500M adjusted-operating-profit target at its 2023 strategy day and has "repeated that number precisely to the last decimal since," and Q1 tracked to it. They held the line on a stable ~€200M annual cash-flow guide. The screener's earnings-beat rate is weak (1 of 3 quarters, 33%), which tempers this, but the multi-year adjusted-profit trend has gone the way management said it would.

Where credibility frays is deal timing: closing guidance slipped from "first half of 2026" to "before the November 10 long-stop." That is the kind of slow drift that, in merger-arbitrage, eats the annualized return even when the deal ultimately closes.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Analysts pushed hardest on exactly the right things. The recurring, repeatedly-deflected topic is the **regulatory path of the JD.com deal** — Verition Fund and others probing the Austrian foreign-investment review, possible remedies, and the EU foreign-subsidies process; management declines specifics each time. The second pressure point is **soft demand in the core German/Austrian (DACH) market**, which management concedes ("softer demand in Germany and Austria") and answers with a cost program and a "top-200 products" focus rather than a demand recovery. Third, analysts probe **reported versus adjusted operating profit** and whether €500M is a ceiling; management dodges ("I cannot preempt what will be said in July"). None of these has yet "turned into bad news," but the regulatory-timeline questions are the live risk.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 Reported earnings are negative (FY2025 net loss of ~€35M; trailing EPS -€0.13) while the narrative trumpets adjusted-profit growth — a persistent reported-vs-adjusted gap.
🔴 Return on invested capital of 1.8% and five-year book-value-per-share erosion of ~15.7% a year: the underlying franchise barely earns its keep.
🔴 Deal-close timeline slipped roughly six months across two quarters (H1 2026 → before Nov 10, 2026), with the foreign-investment and foreign-subsidies reviews still open as of the last call.
🔴 ~€1.6B net debt on a thin-margin retailer — a real liability that any standalone valuation must subtract.
🟢 A binding €4.60 all-cash offer with 85.2% already locked up and merger-control clearance in hand across all relevant jurisdictions — the dominant fact, and a strong floor under the price barring a deal break.
🟢 Free cash flow is genuinely positive and improving (~€739M trailing twelve months), and the higher-margin "growth businesses" (~40% of gross profit, up from ~31% three years ago) are a real, if slow, mix shift.
🟢 The €500M adjusted-operating-profit target has been held "to the last decimal" and is tracking.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The most telling subtle shifts are in the deal language: the move from "signed" and "first half of 2026" to hedged "constructive discussions" and a long-stop-date framing signals the regulators are taking their time, even if a break looks unlikely. The CEO's departure with the CFO stepping up is a classic pre-absorption tidying. And the repeated invocation of JD.com's logistics/technology "acceleration" is forward-looking color for a future under private ownership — i.e., management is already speaking to a post-listing audience, not to public shareholders.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Trajectory: STABLE — but the classification is almost beside the point, because this is a near-closed cash takeover, not a going-concern equity decision.** The single most important thing the director must weigh before sizing is *deal-break risk versus the arbitrage spread*: the upside is capped at €4.60 (a few percent from the current ~€4.40-4.45), the catalyst is the closing of a binding offer that already has 85.2% acceptance and antitrust clearance, and the only real downside is a regulatory break (foreign-investment / foreign-subsidies review) that would drop the stock back toward its un-bid fundamentals — a thin-margin, sub-cost-of-capital, net-debt-laden retailer worth far less than €4.60. This is a tight, capped merger-arb with a modest timeline-slip risk, not a deep-value re-rating.

**Economic moat: NARROW and ERODING.** The asset type is intangible-brand plus regional scale in big-box consumer-electronics retail — a category under structural pressure from e-commerce (Amazon and online channels). The returns evidence confirms erosion, not width: return on invested capital of 1.8%, a five-year book-value-per-share decline, and gross margin described as "contracting." The omnichannel/growth-business mix shift is a credible defense but has not arrested the decline in returns. **Secular threat: material** — retail channel-shift to e-commerce is a genuine long-run headwind that caps the terminal value of the physical-store base. The cheapness on the screener is therefore the *value-trap* kind (a low multiple on a structurally-shrinking, low-return base) — which is precisely why a strategic acquirer at a fixed €4.60, rather than the open market, is the only reason to own this name today.
