# Forensic Dossier — Crédit Agricole S.A. (ACA.PA)

*A deep dive on management and the setup, built from four consecutive quarterly earnings calls (Q2 2025 through Q4/full-year 2025) cross-checked against the screener's hard financials.*

Crédit Agricole S.A. (ticker ACA.PA on Euronext Paris) is the listed holding company of one of France's three largest banking groups — a universal bank spanning French and Italian retail, a top-tier insurer (Crédit Agricole Assurances), the Amundi asset-management arm, a global corporate-and-investment bank (CACIB), consumer finance (CAPFM/Agos), and the CACEIS asset-servicing business. A note on the structure that matters for everything below: "CASA" (Crédit Agricole S.A., the listed entity) is a sub-set of the much larger mutual "Group Crédit Agricole," which includes the unlisted regional banks. Many of the headline numbers in the calls are quoted at both levels, and the listed shareholder only owns the CASA layer.

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story across these four calls is strikingly consistent — almost monotonously so — which for a bank is itself a signal. Across Q2, Q3 and the full-year 2025 calls, management (Deputy GM Clotilde L'Angevin, joined by new CEO Olivier Gavalda for the full-year call) hammered one message: a "diversified, regular, compounding" model that has grown revenue at roughly 5% a year for a decade and cut its cost-to-income ratio (operating costs as a share of revenue) by about 15 percentage points over five years.

What evolved was the *acquisition cadence*, not the thesis. Each quarter added another bolt-on or minority stake: Santander's 30.5% of CACEIS bought back, the Banco BPM stake stepped up from 9.9% to 19.8% and then consolidated, Victory Capital (the Amundi US swap for a 26% stake plus a 15-year distribution deal), Degroof Petercam, Banque Thaler in Switzerland, Milleis, Crelan, and a private-assets partnership with ICG. The through-line management itself names is "stake-building that reinforces industrial partnerships" — when an analyst (Goldman's Chris Hallam, Q2) asked whether the Banco BPM playbook would be replicated, L'Angevin confirmed it as a deliberate template.

The single biggest forward event was telegraphed in every call: the November 18, 2025 Capital Markets Day unveiling the "ACT 2028" medium-term plan. The Q2 and Q3 calls were openly holding back detail ("you're very excited to have the insights we will provide on the 18th of November"). That plan has since been delivered, and its targets — net income above €8.5bn in 2028, return on tangible equity above 14%, cost-to-income below 55%, revenue growth above 3.5% a year — are evolutionary, not transformational, extensions of the existing trajectory.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

This is where the listed-share investor must read carefully, because the screener's financials are dominated by the *insurance* accounting (gross written premiums and investment assets flow through revenue and the balance sheet), which makes the raw line items look very different from a normal bank.

| Management claim | The hard numbers | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Record net income… stable for CASA in 2025 despite a €147m tax surcharge" (FY call) | Net income 2025 €7.07bn vs €7.09bn 2024; EPS €2.18 vs €2.11. Multi-year: NI €5.84bn (2021) → €7.07bn (2025) | **Matches.** Genuinely stable, slow-growing earnings. EPS 5-yr CAGR ~4.3%. |
| "Revenue growth strong, +3.3% CASA / +3.9% Group in 2025" | Screener revenue +7.0% YoY, 3-yr CAGR +35.1% | **Diverges — and the screener is misleading.** The +35% CAGR is an artifact of insurance-premium and consolidation accounting (2022 rev €45.6bn → 2025 €112.3bn), not organic banking growth. Management's ~3-4% is the real figure. |
| "Very strong ROTE of 13.5% (FY), 16.6% (H1)" | Screener ROE 9.7%, **ROIC 0.3%** | **Both true, measuring different things.** ROTE (return on tangible equity) is the right bank metric; 13.5% is solid for a European bank. The screener's ROIC of 0.3% is a meaningless artifact for a bank (it has no "invested capital" in the industrial sense) and should be ignored. |
| "Banco BPM will contribute ~€100m per quarter going forward" | Q4 hit by €607m first-consolidation charge | **Credible but front-loaded with pain.** The €607m one-time charge cut Q4 CASA net income by 39.3%; the recurring €100m/quarter is the payoff. |
| "Cost-to-income at a competitive 53.9% (H1) rising to 55.7% (FY)" | Net margin 6.3%; gross margin 62.6% | **Matches the narrative of efficiency, but the trend ticked the wrong way in H2** — the FY ratio (55.7%) is worse than H1 (53.9%) on IT investment and integration costs. CEO Gavalda explicitly called FY 2025 "a peak point" he expects to fall. |
| "Net debt -48.21B" (screener) | **This is wrong for a bank.** The live correction recomputes net debt at €351.68bn (total debt €519bn minus cash €167bn) | **The screener's "-48B net cash" is a data artifact.** A bank's balance sheet is debt-funded by design; neither figure is a usable "net cash" overlay. The Altman-Z of 0.1 and the FCF figures (TTM FCF -€11bn) are likewise non-meaningful for a bank and must not drive the valuation. |

The crucial forensic takeaway: **most of the alarming screener metrics (ROIC 0.3%, Altman-Z 0.1, negative TTM free cash flow, P/FCF 2.7x, the giant CAGR) are bank-accounting noise and carry no signal.** The legitimate read is a stable bank earning ~€7bn, growing low-single-digit, at a low double-digit return on tangible equity, paying a rising dividend (€1.13/share for 2025, +3%).

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Stable-to-rising, and notably scripted.** L'Angevin's delivery is consistent and confident across all four calls; the tone genuinely lifts in the full-year call once Gavalda takes over and frames 2026 with "I'm very confident [the cost ratio] should drop" and "we are truly off to a running start." There is no defensive pivot to cost-cutting language — the posture stays offensive (customer capture, new geographies, Germany platform, tokenized finance). The one note of guarded caution is on French net interest income, where management repeatedly "leans on the side of caution" and guides to merely *stable* LCL net interest income for 2025 before a "favorable" 2026.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**High.** The most testable promise — that 9-month CASA net income of €6.0bn would equal the full-year 2025 medium-term-plan target set back in 2022 — was explicitly flagged in Q3 ("which you recall was our 2025 MTP target") and the company hit its 2025 goals. They also delivered the "2025 net income equal to last year excluding the corporate-tax surcharge" pledge made in Q2. The screener's 83% earnings-beat rate (5 of 6 quarters) corroborates a reliable, mildly-sandbagging management. Reliability: **High.**

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Three recurring themes analysts pushed on:
1. **"Why do you underperform European bank peers despite delivering above-guidance returns?"** (Deutsche Bank, Q3). L'Angevin deflected to the strength/regularity of the model rather than addressing the persistent valuation discount head-on — the single most important unanswered question for a value buyer.
2. **Italy / Banco BPM strategy.** Analysts probed repeatedly whether CASA would sell Crédit Agricole Italia or pursue a fuller BPM combination; management firmly closed the door ("this is not something we want to do"), framing Italy as a committed second home market.
3. **French net interest income durability.** BNP Paribas's analyst (FY call) pressed on whether the strong Q4 French retail NII could be extrapolated; management capped expectations at "high single-digit" growth for 2026, declining to extrapolate the +11% print.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🟢 Decade-long record of ~5% revenue growth and 15-point cost-ratio improvement, corroborated by the numbers.
🟢 Among the best capital and coverage ratios in Europe (Group CET1 17.4%, "among the most solid major European banks"); very low cost of risk (35bps CASA).
🟢 Reliable, mildly-conservative guidance; rising dividend.
🔴 **The cost-to-income ratio deteriorated in H2 2025** (53.9% H1 → 55.7% FY) — the efficiency story stalled, and the 2028 target (<55%) implies only modest further progress.
🔴 **A relentless acquisition appetite** — a dozen-plus deals across these calls. Each is small, but the cumulative integration and execution risk is real, and minority stakes (BPM 20%, Worldline 7%, Victory 26%) add P&L volatility and governance complexity without full control.
🔴 **Structural valuation discount that management cannot explain away** — the persistent gap to peers reflects the cooperative-controlled structure (the listed CASA is the minority float over a mutual parent) and limited share-buyback culture (management confirmed buybacks "are not part of our usual distribution strategy").

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The most telling subtle shift is the *deliberate simplification of disclosure* ahead of the plan — management openly said it was reworking the ROTE calculation "to bring it perfectly close to market standards" and would "make things easier for you with your Excel files." That is a company conscious of its valuation discount and trying to close the credibility-of-numbers gap with the market. The arrival of new CEO Gavalda for the full-year call brought no strategic break — continuity is the message. And the repeated emphasis on minority-stake-plus-partnership (BPM, Victory, Crelan) signals a capital-allocation philosophy of buying influence and distribution rather than control — capital-light in cash terms but P&L-noisy.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Classification: STABLE.** This is a well-run, diversified, conservatively-capitalized European universal bank compounding earnings in the low-single-digits with a reliable, rising dividend — not a turnaround, not a melting asset.

The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: **the persistent peer-and-structural valuation discount is the entire thesis, and it is structural, not a fear-print.** The cooperative-controlled float, the absence of buybacks, the minority-stake P&L noise, and the simple fact that European bank multiples de-rate together mean the cheapness is shared sector beta, not idiosyncratic mispricing waiting for a catalyst. The big re-rating event (the ACT 2028 plan) has already been delivered, and its targets are evolutionary.

Economic moat: a **narrow** moat built on a regulatory banking licence, vast French/Italian deposit franchise (scale-cost advantage in funding), and high customer switching costs in retail banking and insurance. Returns are stable-to-mildly-rising (ROTE ~13.5%, low cost of risk), so the moat is **stable**, not eroding. Secular threat to terminal value: **manageable** — payments/fintech disintermediation and digital-bank competition nibble at the edges (the very thing the German platform and Worldline JV respond to), but a deposit-funded universal bank with a top European insurer is not facing a terminal-decline force on the horizon of this analysis. The cheapness is a structural-discount-on-a-stable-base, which is neither a deep mispricing nor a value trap — it is simply a fairly-priced, low-multiple compounder whose discount needs an unlikely structural change (buybacks, a control simplification) to close.
