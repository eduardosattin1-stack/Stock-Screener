# Forensic Dossier — Métropole Télévision (Groupe M6), ticker MMT.PA

*A deep dive on management and the setup. One bundled earnings call is available — the 2025 full-year results presentation held 17 February 2026 — cross-referenced against the screener's multi-year financial trajectory (2021 through 2025). With a single transcript there is no quarter-over-quarter arc to grade, so the time-series sections lean on the financial trajectory and the single call's tone; where a claim cannot be tested across calls, that limit is stated plainly.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

M6 — France's most profitable private free-to-air (television you watch without a subscription) broadcaster — tells a clear, internally consistent story on the 2025 call: manage the structural decline of linear TV, defend the margin with cost discipline, and build a digital second leg in streaming. Chief executive David Larramendy opens with "two figures to remember": full-year earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortisation (EBITDA, a rough proxy for operating cash generation) of 214 million euros, and a current operating margin of 17%, which he frames as "relatively high amongst our peers."

The growth engine management wants you to watch is streaming: the M6+ platform grew 27% to 126 million euros and the company says it is "slightly ahead" of its own milestones toward a 200-million-euro target. The defensive engine is cost: a newly announced 80-million-euro savings plan over five years, leaning on artificial intelligence to "produce better with improved quality of production at a lower cost." The quieter — and most important — theme is regulation. Management spends real time arguing France's audiovisual rulebook is "obsolete," leading with "the 5-year rule that is blocking the consolidation of the French market" and comparing France unfavourably to the United States. That is the tell: the real upside in this stock is a future corporate event, not the operating business.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

Honest summary: a slow secular decline (revenue down a 2.6% compound rate over three years; earnings per share down an 18.5% compound rate over five years), partly cushioned by real cost discipline and a fast-growing but still-small streaming arm. The stock is cheap on cash flow (about 11 times free cash flow), carries net cash, but the earnings base is shrinking, not compounding. Note: the screener's trailing-twelve-month figures (free cash flow of 314 million euros, diluted earnings per share of 2.38) sit well above the fiscal-2025 annual figures and look distorted by one-offs (the World Cup cash timing swing — paying three-quarters of costs in 2025 but collecting 100% of receipts in 2026 — flatters trailing cash flow); the fiscal-year trajectory is the more honest read of earning power.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

A single call gives a point-in-time read, not a trajectory: **measured and defensive.** Management leads with margin and cost, not growth. The language around linear TV is about protecting EBITDA — "you need to have the right balance between defending audiences and defending our EBITDA." The genuinely confident tone is reserved for two narrow areas: streaming reach and the political fight to change the law. On the advertising outlook management explicitly refuses to guide — "I will not comment the forecast… we don't have enough visibility" — and concedes February-March trends were "not really far from the last quarter" (which was down about 7%). That is defensiveness, honestly stated.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

No prior calls are available to test guidance-versus-delivery directly, so reliability is inferred. The screener shows a beat in only one of the last four quarters (a 25% beat rate) and the latest quarter's earnings per share fell about 50% year over year, while consensus still embeds a forward earnings rebound of roughly +37% — an optimistic street setup against a weak recent record. The one medium-term promise management could point to — the M6+ streaming milestones — it claims to be "slightly ahead" of, and the 27% growth supports that. Against that, the "good year" framing sits awkwardly next to a 29% drop in net income. Reliability: **Medium.** Targets they control (streaming, cost) look credible; the things they cannot (the ad market, the law) they wisely decline to promise.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Two analysts pressed the same nerve: the 80-million-euro savings plan. CIC's Eric Ravary and ODDO's Jérôme Bodin both pushed for phasing and for whether the cuts come from internal or external (producer) costs — a sign the street doubts the savings are real and bankable. Management's answer was vague on timing ("a progressive ramp-up") and revealing on source: of roughly one billion euros of addressable cost, "50% of programming and 50% of other costs," with production savings coming "in collaboration with producers" rather than restructuring — negotiated, not contractual, and therefore uncertain. The other deflection was the 2026 advertising forecast, declined entirely. The unaddressed worry beneath both questions: can cost-cutting outrun the structural erosion of linear advertising?

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 Net income down roughly 29% in 2025 and earnings per share down 28% year over year, with the latest quarter off about 50% — profit is deteriorating faster than revenue.
🔴 Gross margin contracting and about two standard deviations below its peak; the core advertising business is in structural, not merely cyclical, decline (cord-cutting and audience fragmentation).
🔴 The most important value catalyst — French audiovisual deregulation and a renewed TF1 merger — is entirely outside management's control and depends on legislation that has not passed.
🔴 The cost-savings plan is unproven, back-end-loaded, and partly dependent on third-party producer negotiations.
🟢 Net cash of about 110 million euros (cash of 216 million against 106 million of debt) — a genuinely under-levered balance sheet and a real sum-of-the-parts add-back.
🟢 Streaming (M6+) growing 27% and reportedly ahead of internal milestones; audience ratings the best in 3-4 years, with relative advertising-share gains inside a falling market.
🟢 Strong absolute returns (13% ROIC) and real cost discipline (25.4 million euros of linear savings already taken in 2025).
🟢 Solvency sound (Altman-Z 3.7, Piotroski 6/9).

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The most important subtle shift is the elevation of regulation from background grievance to central strategy: management lists "6 points" of reform it is fighting for, leading with the five-year ownership rule "blocking the consolidation of the French market." This is management telling you the realistic upside is a sale or merger, not organic growth. Second, the heavy reliance on artificial intelligence to deliver production-cost savings is both an opportunity and a quiet admission that the old content model's economics no longer work at scale. Third, the World Cup accounting note — paying three-quarters of the competition's costs in 2025 but receiving 100% of receipts in 2026 — is a one-off swing that will flatter 2026 cash flow and should not be mistaken for a turnaround.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

Trajectory: **DETERIORATING** on the operating fundamentals, with the equity story hinging on an external corporate event rather than a business turnaround. The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: this is a structurally shrinking linear-television business that happens to be cheap and cash-rich, where the real upside is a regulatory-then-deal catalyst — the five-year ownership lock-up expires around 2032 under current law, with a pending Senate amendment that could pull it to roughly 2028, and Bertelsmann/RTL has publicly said it wants to revive the TF1 merger — but that catalyst is non-binding, third-party-dependent, and serially delayed. You are underwriting a melting ice cube with a lottery ticket attached.

Economic moat: **ERODING.** The moat is an intangible-brand-plus-regulatory-licence advantage (premium audience reach and a state broadcast licence). The evidence says it is narrowing, not widening: gross margin is contracting and sits about two standard deviations below its peak; ROIC, though still a respectable 13%, is earned on a revenue base falling about 2.6% a year; earnings per share have compounded down 18.5% a year over five years. A high-but-falling return on a shrinking base is an eroding moat.

Secular threat: **material.** The structural force is cord-cutting and the decline of linear-TV advertising — audiences and ad budgets migrating to streaming and to the United States platforms management itself names as the existential competitor. M6's streaming pivot is real but sub-scale and unproven as an offset. The cheapness is therefore partly a fear-print (the balance sheet and cash flow are real) and partly a structurally-shrinking base (the earnings the multiple is applied to keep falling) — a value-trap risk that caps how far cheapness alone should carry conviction.
