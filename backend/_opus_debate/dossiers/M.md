# Forensic Dossier — Macy's, Inc. (M)

*A deep dive on management and the setup, built from eight consecutive quarterly earnings calls (first quarter 2025 through first quarter 2026) cross-checked against the screener's hard financials.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story management tells has been remarkably consistent in *theme* and steadily more confident in *tone* across the last five public calls. From the very first transcript (first quarter 2025) to the most recent (first quarter 2026), every call is organized around the same "Bold New Chapter" three-pillar framework: (1) strengthen and reimagine the core Macy's nameplate, (2) accelerate and differentiate luxury (Bloomingdale's and Bluemercury), and (3) simplify and modernize end-to-end operations. There is no thesis pivot here — this is the same plan, executed with growing evidence behind it.

What *changed* is the proof. In the first quarter of 2025, comparable sales (sales at stores open at least a year, plus digital and marketplace) were still negative (down 1.2% company-wide, down 0.9% in the go-forward fleet), and earnings per share of $0.16 was framed as "above guidance" off a low bar. By the second quarter of 2025 the company reported "its strongest comparable sales in 12 quarters" (up 1.9%). By the third quarter, "strongest comp growth in 13 quarters." By the first quarter of 2026, management proclaimed "our best comparable sales in four years, with all nameplates and channels positive" (up 3%). So the arc is a genuine operational inflection from decline to modest growth — the "Reimagine" remodeled-store program (now 200 locations) is the recurring hero, posting positive comps in 8 of the last 9 quarters.

A growth driver that has *quietly grown louder* is Bloomingdale's, which accelerated from a 3.8% comp (first quarter 2025) to 5.7%, to 9.9% (fourth quarter 2025), to 10.2% (first quarter 2026, "its highest first quarter sales in its 154-year history"). A driver that was barely mentioned and stayed small: the Macy's Media Network (retail advertising) and credit-card revenue, which together remain only ~3-4% of revenue and were actually *down* in the latest quarter ("Macy's Media Network was down 5%... credit card... benefit of net credit losses was taken in Q1").

Conspicuously *absent* from every transcript: any management discussion of the real-estate monetization thesis that the activists (Barington Capital, Thor Equities) have built their campaign on. Management talks about closing underperforming stores and "streamlining our asset portfolio," but never frames the owned real estate as a separable, value-unlocking asset — the opposite of what the stock's deep-value bulls want to hear.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

| Management claim | What the financials show | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Best comparable sales in four years... enterprise-wide growth" (Q1 2026) | Reported revenue is still *shrinking*: −1.7% year-over-year, −3.9% three-year compound annual growth rate. Fiscal revenue fell from $25.40B (2022) to $22.62B (2026). | **Half-true.** Comps turned positive, but total revenue keeps falling because the store count keeps shrinking (64 closures in 2024, 14 more announced). The growth is *per-store*, masked by a contracting footprint. |
| "Strong free cash flow and strong free cash flow yield" (Q1 2026) | Trailing-twelve-month free cash flow of $1.50B against a ~$7.2B market value is a real ~21% free-cash yield; price-to-free-cash-flow is only 6.8x. Free cash flow grew +39% year-over-year. | **Supported.** This is the strongest leg of the bull case and it is real. |
| "Healthy balance sheet with no maturities out to 2030" (Q1 2026) | Net debt is $3.81B (total debt $5.11B minus $1.29B cash). Altman-Z solvency score 2.7 sits in the "grey zone," not the safe zone. | **Mostly true but softened.** No near-term wall is a genuine positive, but this is a *levered* balance sheet, not a fortress — the activist "net cash to fund a $3B buyback" framing is not how the books actually look. |
| Margins improving via "regular price sell-through" and SG&A leverage | Gross margin is 36.5% and the screener flags it as *contracting*; net margin is a thin 2.8%. Earnings per share have a −17.9% three-year compound decline and a −15.5% five-year decline despite the recent +12% rebound year. | **Diverges.** The margin story is better quarter-to-quarter, but the multi-year trend in profitability is still down, and gross margin is not expanding on a full-year basis. |
| "Returning cash to shareholders... resumed buybacks" | $50M of buybacks plus $50M dividend per quarter, ~$1.1B authorization remaining. | **True but modest.** This is a fraction of the $3B over three years the activists demanded; it is a confidence signal, not a capital-return transformation. |

The single most important divergence: management narrates a *growth* company while the income statement is a *melting-ice-cube-stabilizing* company. Comps are positive, but only because the denominator (the store fleet) is being culled. Earnings per share of $2.32 in fiscal 2026 is still far below the $4.55 of fiscal 2022.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Trajectory: Rising — steadily and credibly.**

- *First quarter 2025:* Defensive and hedged. The dominant topic was tariffs and a "consumer under pressure"; Spring repeatedly emphasized "controlling what we can control" and a 20-40 basis-point tariff margin hit. Tone: cautious operator in a storm.
- *Second/third quarter 2025:* Shifting to offense. "Strongest comp in 12/13 quarters," record Net Promoter Scores, "we are taking share." Management began leading with wins rather than defending against headwinds.
- *Fourth quarter 2025 / first quarter 2026:* Confident. "Year of transformation," "best comparable sales in four years," Bloomingdale's "best first quarter in its 154-year history." The Chief Financial Officer volunteers the balance-sheet strength ("no maturities out to 2030") as a competitive weapon rather than answering defensively.

The language migrated from defensive (cost, tariffs, macro) toward offensive (newness, share gain, brand heat) — a healthy sign. Guidance specificity also improved: by first quarter 2026 they are giving precise segment-level revenue targets ($920M combined media-plus-credit). The one persistent defensive note is around big-ticket home/furniture (blamed on interest rates and mortgages) and plus-size apparel ("soft for some time").

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Reliability: High.** This is the most impressive single fact in the file. The screener records a beat rate of 7 of 7 quarters (100%), and the transcripts corroborate it call-by-call:
- Q1 2025: EPS $0.16 vs guide $0.12-$0.15 — beat.
- Q2 2025: EPS $0.41 vs guide $0.15-$0.20 — large beat.
- Q3 2025: EPS $0.09 vs guide of a *loss* of $0.15-$0.20 — large beat.
- Q4 2025: EPS $1.67 vs guide $1.35-$1.55 — beat.
- Full-year 2025: EPS $2.32 vs "most recent guidance of $2.00-$2.20" — beat.
- Q1 2026: EPS $0.13 "well above the high end of our guidance" — beat.

The magnitude of the third-quarter beat (a guided loss that turned into a $0.09 profit) and the fourth-quarter beat ($1.67 vs $1.55 high end) point to deliberate **sandbagging** — management is setting conservative bars and clearing them comfortably. That is a credibility builder, but it also means the "beats" are partly manufactured by low guidance, and investors should not extrapolate the beat *magnitude*. Notably, management flagged its *own* miss honestly: 2025 results were "above guidance despite... lower-than-expected asset sale gains," and the fourth-quarter asset-sale gain came in at $3M versus $15-20M expected — they did not bury the real-estate monetization shortfall.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

- **Tariffs and 2026 margin:** Analysts (Carney/Barclays, Kloppenburg/JJK) pushed repeatedly on tariff pass-through and 2026 mitigation. Management consistently deflected with "it's a little early to be forecasting tariffs in 2026" (Q2 2025) and only by Q1 2026 quantified a 20-40 basis-point gross-margin hit. The deflection turned out to be defensible — the hit has been contained so far.
- **Gross margin direction:** Jay Sole (UBS) pinned management in Q1 2026 on the implied Q2 gross-margin decline of 60-90 basis points; the Chief Financial Officer conceded margin would be "fairly consistent excluding tariffs" — i.e., the headline contraction is real and tariff-driven. This is the live worry the numbers confirm.
- **The activist real-estate question is the dog that doesn't bark.** Across eight quarters, *no analyst on these calls and no management remark engages the Barington/Thor real-estate-spinoff or Bloomingdale's-separation thesis.* For a stock whose deep-value narrative rests on that very catalyst, the total silence on the earnings calls is the most important pressure point — it tells you management has no intention of pursuing it and the sell-side has largely moved on from it.
- **Furniture / big-ticket weakness:** A recurring soft spot management attributes to rates and mortgages — an honest, externally-driven explanation, but a persistent drag.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🟢 Genuine operating inflection: comps positive across all three nameplates, Reimagine stores positive 8 of 9 quarters, record Net Promoter Scores.
🟢 Perfect guidance-beat record (7/7), with honest disclosure of the asset-sale shortfall — high management credibility.
🟢 Real, large free cash flow ($1.50B trailing) at a low 6.8x price-to-free-cash-flow; no debt maturities until 2030.
🟢 Bloomingdale's is a legitimately strong, share-gaining luxury franchise (10%+ comps) inside a struggling department-store shell.

🔴 Total revenue still *declining* (−1.7% year-over-year) — the "growth" is per-store, propped up by closing stores.
🔴 Earnings per share down −15.5% over five years; gross margin contracting on a full-year basis; net margin a thin 2.8%.
🔴 **Insider behavior is bearish:** zero open-market buys against 26 open-market sales (479,741 shares). The people who know the business best are net sellers as the stock sits at 99% of its 52-week range.
🔴 The load-bearing deep-value catalyst (real-estate unlock) is *decelerating, not unlocking* — monetization proceeds fell to $107M from $283M; asset-sale gains collapsed to $3M. Management never discusses real estate as separable value.
🔴 The stock has already re-rated: +28% above its 200-day average, 99% up its 52-week range, and the analyst consensus target ($19.60) sits ~24% *below* the current price — Wall Street thinks it has run too far.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

- A subtle but telling terminology shift: management quietly redefined and "simplified" its non-GAAP disclosures effective fiscal 2026 (announced February 18), folding everything into "go-forward" comparable-sales metrics. Re-cut disclosures during a turnaround deserve a skeptical eye — the new lens flatters the surviving fleet by excluding the closing stores from the comparison base.
- A change of voices: the Chief Financial Officer seat changed (Adrian Mitchell departed; Tom Edwards took over by Q2 2025). The new Chief Financial Officer leans harder on balance-sheet and free-cash-flow language ("robust cash flow, no debt maturities") — a deliberate repositioning of Macy's as a cash-returner rather than a grower.
- New initiative seeded quietly: "Ask Macy's," an AI shopping assistant (Q1 2026). Early-stage, no financial impact yet, but a marker that management is investing in digital rather than harvesting cash — which cuts against the activist "slash capex to 1.5-2% of sales" demand.
- Capital-allocation tell: resuming buybacks ($50M/quarter) while *also* defending capital expenditure and store investment is management drawing a clear line *against* the activist plan. They are choosing reinvestment over financial engineering.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Classification: STABILIZING (best read of the four options: STABLE, leaning toward strengthening operationally but with a structurally shrinking base).** The Bold New Chapter is working as an *operating* turnaround — comps, Net Promoter Scores, and Bloomingdale's are all genuinely better, and the guidance record is impeccable. But the enterprise is still a department store losing total revenue every year, defending a melting top line by culling stores and leaning on a strong-but-finite cash flow.

**The single most important thing the director must weigh before sizing:** the entire deep-value case rests on a real-estate / brand-separation catalyst that *management is actively refusing to pursue* and that is, on the company's own numbers, monetizing *more slowly* every year — while the stock has already rallied to the top of its range and trades above the sell-side target. You are being asked to pay up, at a 52-week high, for an unlock that the people in charge have publicly declined to deliver and that insiders are selling into.

**Economic moat and terminal-value test.** The moat type is *intangible-brand plus scale*, and it is **eroding**. Returns on invested capital of 8.0% are roughly at or below the cost of capital (the screener's earnings-power value margin of safety is −79%, the signal that the business does not durably earn its cost of capital), gross margin is contracting, and five-year earnings per share are down ~16%. That is the profile of a narrow, fading moat, not a wide one. The structural threat is **retail channel-shift** (the migration of spending from physical department stores to e-commerce and direct-to-consumer) — a genuine secular, not merely cyclical, headwind, even though Macy's is fighting it competently and Bloomingdale's is a real exception. The conclusion: the optical cheapness (6.8x free cash flow) is a *mix* of a legitimate cash-flow franchise and a structurally-shrinking store base. With the unlock catalyst stalled and the stock already re-rated, the cheapness reads closer to a value trap than a clean fear-print — the cash flow is real, but the terminal value is melting and the event that would force the gap to close is not coming.
