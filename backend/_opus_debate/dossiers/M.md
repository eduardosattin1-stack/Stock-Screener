# Forensic Dossier — Macy's, Inc. (M)

*A deep dive on management and the setup, built from five consecutive earnings calls (May 2025 through June 2026) cross-referenced against the screener's hard financials.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

Across these five quarters the story is remarkably consistent — and that consistency is itself the headline. From the first quarter of 2025 onward, every call is organized around the same three-pillar "Bold New Chapter" framework: (1) strengthen and reimagine the Macy's nameplate, (2) accelerate and differentiate luxury (Bloomingdale's and Bluemercury), and (3) simplify and modernize operations. CEO Tony Spring does not pivot the thesis; he compounds the same one quarter after quarter.

What *does* evolve is the proof. In May 2025 the company was defending a soft print — Macy's-nameplate comparable sales (sales at stores open at least a year, plus licensed and marketplace) were still negative (-1.2% total, -0.9% go-forward), and management leaned heavily on the "Reimagine 125" pilot stores as the one bright spot. By the fourth quarter (reported March 2026) and the first quarter of fiscal 2026 (reported June 2026), the tone shifts from "the pilot is working" to "we have inflected": total company comps turned positive (+1.9% in Q2, +3.2% in Q3, +3.0% in Q1-2026 — described as the best first-quarter comp in four years), the pilot base was expanded from 125 to 200 stores, and Bloomingdale's accelerated to a double-digit +10.2% comp in its best first quarter in its 154-year history. The growth driver that gets quietly *added* over time is luxury: Bloomingdale's and Bluemercury go from a supporting mention to the clear engine. The driver that gets quietly *managed down* is the full-line Macy's store fleet itself — 64 closures in 2024, 14 announced for 2025 — folded into the upbeat "more focused and profitable store base" language.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

The central tension: management narrates a credible *operational* turnaround, but the *financial trajectory* shows a business that is still shrinking and that does not yet earn its cost of capital.

| Management claim | The hard numbers | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Enterprise-wide growth… best comps in four years" (Q1-2026) | Revenue: 25.40B (2022) → 25.45B → 23.87B → 23.01B → 22.62B (2026). 3-yr revenue CAGR **-3.9%**; latest year **-1.7%**. | **Diverges.** Positive *comps* coexist with *falling total revenue* — the gap is store closures. The base is still shrinking. |
| "Strong free cash flow… healthy balance sheet, no maturities to 2030" | TTM free cash flow **$1.50B**; P/FCF **6.8x**; but **net debt $3.81B** (total debt $5.11B less $1.29B cash) — a liability, not net cash. | **Mostly holds, with a correction.** Cash generation is real and the maturity wall is genuinely pushed out; but management's "no debt" framing on the calls overstates a still-levered balance sheet. |
| "Returned $101M in buybacks… confidence in the business" | Buybacks resumed 2025; ~$926M program completed; ROE 18.9% but **ROIC only 8.0%**; EPV margin-of-safety **-79%**. | **Mixed.** Capital return is happening, but return on invested capital near 8% against a retail cost of capital means the earnings-power value (EPV — the value of current profits with no growth) is far below the price. |
| Margins improving via NPS / full-price sell-through | Gross margin **36.5% and contracting**; net margin **2.8%**; tariff drag 20-40bps guided. | **Diverges.** The qualitative margin story is not yet visible in the gross-margin line, which is flat-to-down. |
| EPS recovering | EPS: 4.55 (2022) → 0.16 (2024 trough) → 2.07 → 2.32 (2026); 3-yr EPS CAGR **-17.9%**. TTM diluted EPS 2.41, latest quarter +64% YoY. | **Genuinely improving off a deep trough**, but still roughly half the 2022 level. The recovery is real but partial. |

The honest read: the operating turnaround is *real* but *modest*, and it is being delivered against a *secularly shrinking* top line. The narrative is richer than the trajectory justifies on revenue and gross margin; it is fairly matched on cash flow and EPS recovery.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Rising.** The confidence build is steady and earned-by-results, not bluster.
- May 2025 (defensive): heavy tariff hedging — "we estimate a combined tariff impact… of roughly 20 to 40 basis points," "the impact on demand is less clear," consumer "remains under pressure."
- Sept / Dec 2025 (turning offensive): "strongest comparable sales in 12 quarters," then "13 quarters"; "results were meaningfully better than expected."
- March / June 2026 (confident): "best comparable sales in four years with all nameplates and channels positive," and Bloomingdale's "best first quarter… in its 154-year history."

The shift from defensive (tariffs, cautious consumer) to offensive (share gains, brand additions, new store openings) is unmistakable. Guidance specificity also improved — they consistently beat their own ranges, suggesting deliberate conservatism.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**High.** This is the strongest single fact in the company's favor. The screener records a **7-of-7 quarterly earnings-beat rate (100%)**, and the transcripts corroborate it call by call: Q1-2025 adjusted EPS $0.16 vs $0.12-0.15 guide; Q2 $0.41 vs $0.15-0.20; Q3 $0.09 vs a *guided loss* of $0.15-0.20; Q4 $1.67 vs $1.35-1.55; full-year FY25 $2.32 vs $2.00-2.20; Q1-2026 $0.13 above the high end. The pattern is textbook sandbagging — guide low, beat, repeat. Reliability score: **High.** The caveat is that beating consistently *lowered* guidance is easier than beating *raised* guidance, so credit it, but don't mistake it for accelerating fundamentals.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Analysts circle three things management keeps soft-pedaling:
- **2026 tariff impact** — repeatedly deflected ("it's a little early to be forecasting tariffs in 2026," Q2-2025; same again Q4). This is a real unquantified margin risk.
- **Where growth comes from once closures stop flattering comps** — the "recapture" of closed-store customers is asked directly (Jay Sole, Q3); management answers with a "15-to-20-minute drive horizon" retention story that is plausible but unprovable.
- **Marketing spend vs traffic** (Kloppenburg, Q1-2026) — the implicit worry that the turnaround needs more marketing dollars to sustain. Management reframes it as "what you talk about" rather than "what you spend," i.e. resists raising the budget.

Notably, *no analyst on these calls pushes on the real-estate value or the failed activist takeover* — the calls are run as a pure operating story, with the real-estate optionality living entirely outside the transcript.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 Total revenue still declining (-1.7% latest, -3.9% 3-yr) — the comp recovery is partly an artifact of closing weak stores.
🔴 Gross margin contracting (36.5%) against a "premiumization" narrative.
🔴 Insider behavior: open-market activity is **0 buys vs 26 sells (479,741 shares)** — the people who know the business best are net sellers into the rally.
🔴 Stock at **99% of its 52-week range, +28% vs its 200-day average** — the easy money on the re-rating has been made.
🔴 ROIC ~8% and EPV margin-of-safety **-79%** — the durable earnings base is worth far less than today's price.

🟢 Flawless beat record (7/7) and visible sequential comp acceleration.
🟢 Genuine free cash flow ($1.5B TTM, ~7x P/FCF) and a maturity wall pushed to 2030.
🟢 Luxury (Bloomingdale's +10.2%, Bluemercury 19 straight quarters of growth) is a structurally better business than full-line Macy's and is accelerating.
🟢 Buybacks resumed and a dividend maintained — real capital return.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

- **CFO turnover, smoothly handled.** Adrian Mitchell (COO/CFO) is gone by the Sept-2025 call, replaced by Tom Edwards — no drama, continuity preserved, but a change worth noting.
- **The "no debt maturities to 2030" line is repeated almost verbatim** across calls — a deliberately planted reassurance, which itself tells you the balance sheet is the question management most wants to pre-empt.
- **"Go-forward" accounting redefinition** (Form 8-K, Feb 2026) — management formally shifted its non-GAAP comp and earnings definitions. Changing the scorecard mid-turnaround is not sinister here (it isolates the continuing fleet), but it does make multi-year comparability harder and should be watched.
- **Real-estate monetization is mentioned only obliquely** ("streamlining our asset portfolio," "lower-than-expected asset sale gains" in Q4) — the part of the story the deep-value bull cares most about is the part management discusses least.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Trajectory: STABLE, edging toward STRENGTHENING** on operations — but layered over a structurally shrinking base. The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: **this is an operating turnaround that is real but already substantially priced**, with the one genuinely asymmetric catalyst (the Arkhouse/Brigade real-estate-led buyout) already dead, and insiders selling into a stock at the top of its range.

**Moat:** NARROW and **ERODING**. Macy's has a residual intangible-brand asset (the Thanksgiving parade, Herald Square, the Bloomingdale's name) and real-estate ownership, but the full-line department-store model carries no switching costs and faces structural traffic loss. The evidence — falling revenue, contracting gross margin, ROIC stuck near 8% — is the signature of an eroding, not a widening, moat. The luxury sub-segments are healthier, but they are a minority of the whole.

**Secular threat:** **material**, via e-commerce / direct-to-consumer channel shift — the department-store format is in long-run secular decline (the same force that took Sears and JCPenney). It is not yet *terminal* for Macy's specifically, because the company has real cash flow, a real-estate floor, and a defensible luxury franchise — but the cheapness here is best understood as a *structurally-shrinking base*, not a clean fear-print. A low multiple on a melting top line is the classic value-trap silhouette.
