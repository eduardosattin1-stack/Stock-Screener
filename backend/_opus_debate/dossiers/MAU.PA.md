# Forensic Dossier — Etablissements Maurel & Prom (MAU.PA)

*Africa-focused independent oil and gas producer (exploration and production, or "E&P"), listed in Paris and controlled by Indonesia's state oil company. Prepared for the investment committee. No earnings-call transcript is bundled for this name; the analysis below is built from the company's own 2025 annual results (released 12 March 2026), its first-quarter 2026 activity report (15 April 2026), the ownership record, and the screener's financial trajectory. Where the transcript would normally supply management's words, the published results releases and investor materials stand in their place; nothing here is invented.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

Maurel & Prom is a mid-sized oil and gas producer whose economic engine is the Ezanga oil permit in Gabon, supplemented by gas in Tanzania, a non-operated stake in Angola, and a high-margin royalty-style interest in Venezuela. The story over the last several years has been one of deleveraging and cash generation rather than growth: the company paid down debt, swung to a net-cash balance sheet, and turned itself into a dividend-paying cash machine for its majority owner.

The most important fact about this company is who owns it. PIEP, a subsidiary of Indonesia's state oil major Pertamina, holds roughly 71% of the shares (a stake built through a 2016-2017 tender offer and topped up since). The free float — the portion of shares actually available to public investors — is therefore only about a quarter to a third of the company. This single fact governs almost everything in the investment case: there is no realistic path to an activist campaign, a hostile takeover, a forced sell-down, or a board-level strategic reset, because the parent already controls the company outright and runs it as the international upstream arm of Pertamina.

The recent narrative is cyclical, not structural. After three years of flattish revenue ($676M in 2022, $682M in 2023, $808M in 2024), the company's reported revenue fell about 28% in 2025 to $578M. That drop was not a demand collapse — it was a combination of a lower realized oil price through much of 2025 and a roughly 6% dip in Gabon oil output caused by problems on the oil export line in the second half of the year. By the first quarter of 2026 the picture had already reversed: production recovered (working-interest output up about 7% versus the prior quarter) and the realized oil price jumped about 41% to roughly $91 a barrel, lifted by the Iran/Strait-of-Hormuz energy shock that has driven oil and inflation higher across the current regime.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

Without earnings calls, tone must be read from the cadence of the results releases. The signal is **Stable**. The 2025 annual release leads with the cash position and a 15% dividend increase — the language of a controlled, mature, capital-returning business, not a growth story pitching a turnaround. The Q1 2026 release is matter-of-fact about the production recovery and the oil-price tailwind. There is no defensive pivot, no crisis framing, and no aggressive new growth narrative being introduced. The company communicates like what it is: the steady cash-generating subsidiary of a state oil parent.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

Maurel & Prom is not a heavy guidance company, and as a controlled subsidiary it does not court the sell-side with quarterly targets. The verifiable track record is operational and capital-allocation discipline: it deleveraged to net cash, it has paid and grown the dividend (now €0.38, ~$90M, up 15%, payable August 2026), and it delivered a 2% increase in total working-interest production in 2025 despite the Gabon export-line disruption. The Gabon export-line problem in the second half of 2025 is the one operational stumble, and it appears to be a logistics issue rather than a reserve or decline problem, given the Q1 2026 recovery. **Reliability: Medium-to-High** on capital allocation and cash, lower visibility on production guidance simply because little is given.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

The recurring overhangs for a name like this — none of which a controlled company is under pressure to resolve — are: (1) the persistent minority-shareholder discount, because PIEP/Pertamina controls ~71% and minority holders are passengers; (2) jurisdiction risk in Gabon (which had a 2023 coup) and Tanzania, where contracts, fiscal terms, and export logistics sit outside investor control; (3) the durability of the Venezuela royalty income, which is high-margin but politically fragile; and (4) capital allocation — whether the parent uses the cash for dividends to all holders (good for minorities) or for related-party transactions and Pertamina-strategic uses (a structural risk for minorities). The thinness of disclosure on related-party flows is the quiet worry.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 The ~71% PIEP/Pertamina control means minority shareholders have no governance leverage and face permanent related-party risk; the discount to "fair value" may be a permanent feature, not a closing gap.
🔴 The 2025 reported net income is inflated by accounting/tax items; cash earnings (EBITDA, FCF) fell with revenue — the optical 70.9% net margin and 264% margin of safety are traps for the unwary.
🔴 Jurisdiction concentration: the bulk of value is one Gabon oil permit plus politically exposed Tanzania and Venezuela income.
🔴 The thesis is fundamentally an oil-price bet — the Q1 2026 strength is the Hormuz price spike, which the current regime read flags as a live tail, not a durable level.

🟢 Low-cost production with healthy ~43% cash (EBITDA) margins and growing working-interest volumes (+2% in 2025, +7% quarter-on-quarter in Q1 2026).
🟢 A growing, well-covered dividend (€0.38, ~$90M, +15%), payable to all shareholders including minorities.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The most telling signal is the gap between the two valuation models the screener carries: the discounted-cash-flow model (which works off sustainable cash flows) puts fair value at ~7.7, essentially today's price, while the owner-earnings/Buffett model (which took the windfall net income at face value) says ~28. That ~3.6x spread is not "264% upside"; it is a measure of how much of the bull case depends on treating a one-off accounting profit as permanent. The disciplined read sides with the cash-flow model. A second quiet signal: the dividend was raised 15% even as revenue fell 28% — a controlled parent signaling it wants cash out of the subsidiary, which is fine for minorities only so long as it is paid pro-rata rather than extracted through related-party channels.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Classification: STABLE.** This is a well-run, debt-free, low-cost oil producer that returns cash — not a turnaround, not a melting asset, not a special situation. The single most important thing the director must weigh before sizing is this: there is no catalyst here and no governance lever to create one. With ~71% held by a state-oil parent, this is a permanent minority stake in an oil-price proxy. The "264% margin of safety" is a mirage built on a non-recurring 2025 profit; the real value is closer to the company's sustainable cash earnings plus its net cash, which lands much nearer the current price than the headline suggests.

On the economic moat: the moat type is a low-cost resource position (a cost-advantage on the Ezanga permit), which is **narrow** — real while the asset produces cheaply, but with no switching costs, no network, and no brand, and entirely hostage to the oil price and to single-jurisdiction operating risk. Returns are decent but not exceptional and not structurally rising (ROIC ~9%, below the optical ROE), so the moat is best read as narrow-and-stable, not widening. On secular risk: oil demand faces a long-horizon energy-transition headwind, but for a low-cost producer with a debt-free balance sheet and a multi-decade reserve life, that threat is **manageable** rather than terminal — the assets generate cash long before transition impairs terminal demand, and the bigger risk to this specific stock is the permanent control discount, not stranded-asset risk.
