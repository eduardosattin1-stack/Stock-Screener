# Forensic Dossier — Etablissements Maurel & Prom (MAU.PA)

*Prepared for the investment committee. No earnings-call transcript is bundled for this Paris-listed small-cap; management's words are reconstructed from the public results releases (2025 annual results dated 11–12 March 2026, the half-year 2025 release of 4 August 2025, the Q1 2026 activity update of 15 April 2026, and the Venezuela sanctions updates of February 2026). Every narrative claim is cross-checked against the screener's hard financials and the public figures. No quotes are invented.*

Maurel & Prom is a French-listed independent oil and gas producer (an "E&P", short for exploration and production — a company that finds and pumps oil and gas rather than refining or retailing it), controlled by Malaysia's state oil company PETRONAS (which owns roughly 71% of the shares through its subsidiary PIEP). Its producing assets sit in Gabon (its onshore oil core), Tanzania (gas), Angola, Colombia (newly acquired gas) and Venezuela (the swing factor in the whole story).

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story has shifted from "harvest a maturing portfolio" to "redeploy a fortress balance sheet into growth." Through 2023–2024 the company was a steady cash generator anchored by Gabon oil and Tanzania gas. The pivot came in late 2025: Maurel & Prom sold its entire 20.07% stake in Nigeria's Seplat Energy to Heirs Energies for about $496 million (half received in December 2025, half in early February 2026), which turned the balance sheet from modestly net-cash into strongly net-cash. Management then immediately recycled that cash into three new growth legs: a 61% operated stake in the Sinu-9 gas asset in Colombia (closed January 2026, about $229 million), a 40% interest in Block 3/24 offshore Angola (heads of terms September 2025), and a re-activated Venezuela position once U.S. sanctions relief arrived in February 2026. The chief executive framed 2026 as opening "a particularly exciting chapter." In short, the narrative widened from a single-country oil harvester into a five-country growth-and-cash-return story within roughly two quarters.

The central forensic point: the 2025 income statement is two stories. The ~72% profit surge is largely the Seplat sale; the operating business actually saw revenue and EBITDA decline on lower oil prices even as volumes nudged up. The bull case rests on volumes, reserves and net cash — not on the headline net income.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Rising.** Across 2025 into 2026 management's language moved from steady operational reporting to overtly offensive, growth-forward framing. The half-year 2025 release was workmanlike; by the 2025 annual release the chief executive was describing Venezuela's "confirmed potential," Colombia's "very confident" development prospects, and "significant ambitions" in external growth. The Q1 2026 update reinforced this with concrete wins — total working-interest production up 7% quarter-on-quarter to 37,444 barrels of oil equivalent per day, and a realized oil price of $90.8/bbl (up 41% on the prior quarter, helped by the Hormuz-driven oil spike). The shift is from defensive harvesting to confident redeployment.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Medium-to-High.** The company gave hard 2026 production guidance of 42,700 barrels of oil equivalent per day (M&P share), broken down by country, plus an explicit oil-price sensitivity table for operating cash flow ($190M at $50/bbl rising to $340M at $80/bbl). This is specific and falsifiable. The Q1 2026 print (37,444 boepd working-interest, up 10% on Q4 excluding Venezuela) tracks toward — though has not yet reached — the full-year ramp. The credibility risk is concentration on the back-half ramp: Gabon's Etekamba gas evacuation is meant to step up from 30 to 40 million cubic feet per day by Q2 2026 (production was only ~14 mmcfd in January 2026), and Venezuela's 10,000 bopd target depends on field re-activation under a fresh and politically fragile sanctions license. The guidance is honest but back-end-loaded and partly outside management's control.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

With thin sell-side coverage, the recurring pressure points are structural rather than from a transcript Q&A: (1) the durability of U.S. sanctions relief on Venezuela — a single executive-branch reversal could strip out the 10,000 bopd and the reserves uplift; (2) whether the heavy 2026 capex genuinely converts to the promised volume step-up or slips; and (3) the PETRONAS overhang — the ~71% controlling shareholder's intentions for the minority float are a perennial unanswered question, with the parent able in principle to move to a full buy-in or simply cap strategic optionality.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🟢 Balance sheet transformed to strong net cash (~$281M) — a genuine sum-of-the-parts add-back and rare for a small-cap E&P.
🟢 Volumes growing (+2% in 2025, guided +15% to 42,700 boepd in 2026) — a growing, not shrinking, production base.
🟢 Reserves jump in Venezuela (2P reserves from 80 to 148 million barrels) on the sanctions reopening — a real, if fragile, intrinsic-value uplift.
🟢 Rising, well-covered dividend (€0.38, +15%).
🔴 Headline profit flattered by a one-off disposal gain; recurring EBITDA and revenue declined on lower oil prices.
🔴 2026 free cash flow near break-even at $70/bbl before the dividend — the growth year consumes cash.
🔴 Venezuela exposure is binary on U.S. sanctions policy; GL 50A replaced a prior license and could be revoked.
🔴 The whole thesis is a price-taker on oil — the dominant shared macro factor in this regime — not idiosyncratic.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The quiet but important signal is deliberate geographic diversification away from single-asset dependence: the Seplat exit (a passive financial stake) funded three operated or near-operated positions (Colombia operatorship, Angola interest, Venezuela re-activation). Management is converting passive, hard-to-value holdings into controllable operating assets — a governance positive the headline "revenue down 29%" masks. The second signal is the "significant ambitions" language around external growth, which hints the cash pile may be earmarked for further mergers and acquisitions rather than a larger buyback — a use-of-cash question the market has not resolved, and one that interacts with the PETRONAS overhang.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Trajectory: STRENGTHENING.** Management took a windfall (the Seplat sale) and, rather than letting it sit, recycled it into a diversified, growing production base while raising the dividend — a textbook disciplined small-cap capital cycle. The single most important thing the director must weigh before sizing: **this is a leveraged bet on the oil price dressed as a deep-value special situation.** The cheapness is real on book value and net cash, but the load-bearing catalysts (the Venezuela reserves uplift, the Q2 Gabon ramp, the August dividend) are already announced and largely priced as "happened," and earnings power swings hard with crude. Size it as commodity-beta value, not as idiosyncratic catalyst alpha.

On the economic moat: Maurel & Prom has **no durable moat** — it is a price-taking commodity producer whose returns (return on invested capital ~9%, only modestly above its cost of capital, with falling EBITDA margins) reflect a competitive, undifferentiated business. Its edge is operational competence and the PETRONAS relationship, not pricing power. On secular threat: the long-horizon energy-transition risk (electrification structurally capping fossil-fuel demand) is real but **manageable** rather than terminal over the investable horizon — these are short-cycle producing assets with near-term cash payback, not stranded multi-decade mega-projects, and global oil demand is not yet in structural decline. The cheapness is best read as a fear-print on oil-price and geopolitical (Venezuela) risk, not a melting terminal base — but the absence of a moat caps how far quality can carry the value case.
