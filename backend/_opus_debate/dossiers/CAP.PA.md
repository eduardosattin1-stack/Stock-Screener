# Forensic Dossier — Capgemini SE (CAP.PA)

*A deep dive on management and the setup, built from eight consecutive quarterly calls (full-year 2024 reported February 2025 through full-year 2025 reported February 2026) cross-checked against the screener's hard financials.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

Capgemini is Europe's largest information-technology services and consulting firm — it sells the labor and software to help big companies move to the cloud, modernize core systems, and now deploy artificial intelligence (AI). Across these eight quarters the story travels a clear arc: **from "managing a downturn" to "buying our way into the next growth wave."**

At the start (full-year 2024, reported February 2025) the tone is defensive. Chief executive Aiman Ezzat opens by conceding "the market proved weaker than we anticipated," with revenue down 2% at constant currency, manufacturing and France the soft spots, and clients refusing to "increase their discretionary spend." The emphasis is on resilience — holding the operating margin at 13.3% despite a falling top line — and on a self-help program ("targeted actions") launched in the third quarter of 2024 to "unlock potential at every level."

Through 2025 the narrative shifts from defense to a gradual, quarter-by-quarter recovery. Each call repeats the same refrain — "performance continues to improve through the first half," growth "improved steadily" — culminating in a genuinely strong fourth quarter (constant-currency growth of 10.6%). But the honest reader sees that **most of that acceleration is bought, not earned**: management discloses about 6.5 points of the Q4 growth came from "scope" (acquisitions), "driven primarily by WNS and Cloud4C." Organic growth, the part that reflects the underlying business, finished 2025 barely positive.

The thesis expands twice along the way. First, a new growth pillar — **"Intelligent Operations,"** the AI-powered remaking of business-process outsourcing — is introduced and then made concrete with the July 2025 acquisition of WNS (a large Indian business-process company). Second, two genuinely new demand themes appear and grow louder each quarter: **European defense** ("about €1 billion of revenue ... up 12%") and **digital sovereignty** (clients wanting their data kept on home soil). Generative AI moves from ~4% of bookings (full-year 2024) to over 8% (Q3 2025) — a real, rising contributor.

Quietly dropped or de-emphasized: the early-2025 hand-wringing over tariffs and "Liberation Day" client caution simply fades as the year proves benign. And the "1-to-2 points of M&A" framing narrows to "around 1 point" of bolt-ons — right before the company does the opposite and swallows WNS, a transformational deal that adds ~6.5 points of scope.

The cross-check verdict: **the financials corroborate the margin and cash-flow story, but expose the growth story as substantially acquired rather than organic.** Return on invested capital (ROIC) of 9.0% sits roughly at or only modestly above the firm's cost of capital — adequate, not excellent, and the WNS deal (a lower-revenue-per-head business funded partly with debt) is unlikely to lift it in the near term.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Trajectory: Rising (off a low base).**

- *Full-year 2024 (Feb 2025) — defensive:* "the market proved weaker than we anticipated," "clients did not increase their discretionary spend." Cautious, explaining a miss.
- *Q1 2025 — cautiously better:* "slightly better than our initial expectation," but heavily hedged on tariffs — "we remain extremely cautious of any potential development."
- *H1 2025 — turning:* "Our performance continues to improve," "returned to positive constant currency growth in Q2." Management raises the low end of guidance but explicitly "retain[s] the cautious stance."
- *Q3 2025 — confident:* "Q3 was a strong quarter for the group better than expected ... improvement has been pretty broad-based." The most offensive (growth-oriented) language of the series.
- *FY 2025 (Feb 2026) — assertive:* "Capgemini delivered a solid set of results," revenue "above the upgraded guidance," plus a confident, almost combative defense against AI-disruption fears (the pointed Palantir rebuttal — "don't stay on the headline").

The shift from defensive ("efficiency, cost, macro") to offensive ("AI, Intelligent Operations, sovereignty, defense") language is real and consistent. Guidance became *more* specific over time, not less — a credibility positive.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Reliability: High.**

Management set a 2025 framework early (constant-currency revenue −2% to +2%, margin 13.3-13.5%, free cash flow ~€1.9B) and then *narrowed it upward* through the year (to −1% to +1%, then "above the upgraded guidance" at year-end) while hitting margin and cash flow on the nose. The screener's earnings-beat record (beat 6 of 7 quarters, 86%) corroborates a modest, deliverable-guidance culture. There is a mild **sandbagging pattern** — the repeated "between realistic and conservative" framing and the habit of guiding cautiously then beating — which is a positive sign for a value setup. The one honest blemish: they guided "around 1 point" of acquisitions and then did a transformational deal, so the *organic* picture has to be read carefully against the headline.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Recurring questions management circled rather than answered crisply:
- **How much AI actually deflates pricing.** Analysts (BNP, Jefferies) pressed repeatedly on whether generative AI lowers the cost of delivery and forces price give-backs. Ezzat's answers are real but evasive on magnitude — "some of that is being passed to customers ... it's not yet, again, large scale." This is the central bear question and management never quantifies it.
- **Organic vs. acquired growth.** Analysts kept separating the M&A contribution from underlying demand; management consistently steered back to the headline.
- **France and manufacturing/auto.** Pressed every quarter. Management called the France "trough" passed in Q1 2025, yet France was "still challenging at year-end." Auto is candidly conceded as a structural, not cyclical, drag — "we don't expect to see a rebound in auto."
- **The buyback's true share-count impact.** Jefferies pushed twice on how much of the €2B buyback offsets dilution versus shrinks the count; management would not commit ("it depends on the timing").

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 **Red flags**
- *Growth is bought, not earned.* ~6.5 points of Q4 2025 growth is acquired scope; organic growth is barely positive on a flat three-year revenue base.
- *Reported EPS fell* (−3.6% year-on-year on the screener's basis) even as "normalized" EPS rose — the adjustments are doing work.
- *Net debt of €6.43B* now sits on the balance sheet after WNS, in a higher-for-longer rate world; this is a liability that must be subtracted in any valuation, not a cushion.
- *The core secular question is unanswered:* AI may automate the very labor-arbitrage hours this business bills for. Management's confidence here is asserted, not demonstrated.

🟢 **Green flags**
- *Margin and cash flow are genuinely durable* — held flat through a two-year demand downturn.
- *Tone and underlying momentum are improving* quarter after quarter, broad-based across geographies and sectors by Q3 2025.
- *Two real new demand vectors* (European defense double-digit growth; sovereignty) that are structural tailwinds, not hype.
- *Honest, deliverable guidance* with a sandbagging bias and an 86% beat rate.
- *Shareholder returns* — a €2B buyback accelerating in 2026 plus ~35% dividend payout.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

- **The vocabulary migrated** from "cloud/digital" (2024) to "Agentic AI / Intelligent Operations / sovereignty / defense" (2025-26) — management is repositioning the narrative ahead of the financials, which is either prescient or defensive depending on whether the AI-displacement fear proves real.
- **"Revenue per head" candor.** Ezzat openly admits WNS *lowers* revenue per employee today and bets it rises "over the midterm" via AI leverage — an unusually frank acknowledgment that the deal dilutes a key efficiency metric first.
- **The Palantir rebuttal** (FY 2025) is the most revealing moment: management felt compelled to publicly argue that AI will *not* collapse large-enterprise transformation work. You only mount that defense when the market is pricing the opposite — which is exactly what the depressed multiple says.
- **"Fit for Growth"** is corporate language for a sizeable workforce reset (~€700M) driven explicitly by "evolution ... from AI" — a tacit admission that AI changes the headcount model.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Classification: STABLE, tilting toward STRENGTHENING.** This is a well-run, cash-generative franchise that has navigated a two-year demand trough with its margins and credibility intact and is now repositioning — via WNS and an AI-services pivot — toward the next cycle. Management is honest and delivers on what it controls (margin, cash, guidance).

**The single most important thing to weigh before sizing:** *Is the depressed multiple a fear-print or a value trap?* The whole investment case turns on whether generative AI is a tool that Capgemini sells (bullish — it lifts demand for transformation work) or a force that automates the billable labor-hours its model depends on (bearish — terminal-value erosion). The financials don't yet settle it: returns are merely adequate (ROIC 9.0%), organic growth is flat, and the recent growth is acquired.

**Moat:** intangible/relationship and scale-based — deep client entrenchment, vendor-consolidation winner status ("if you're big, you stay in and you win"), and a top-tier partner ecosystem. But it is a **narrow** moat with thin switching costs relative to true software lock-in, and the returns evidence (ROIC ~9%, stable-not-rising margins, flat organic growth) points to a moat that is holding, not widening. **Moat trend: stable**, with a credible erosion risk if AI deflates bill-rates faster than volume grows.

**Secular threat:** the labor-arbitrage / AI-automation-of-IT-services theme is **material** — a real, debatable risk to the terminal economics of billing labor-hours at a spread — but not yet *terminal*: the firm is positioning to sell AI transformation, defense and sovereignty are genuine offsetting tailwinds, and demand for large, complex enterprise change has so far proven sticky. The cheapness is therefore best read as a **fear-print with a real (but not fatal) secular overhang**, not a melting-base value trap — provided organic growth re-accelerates and AI proves additive rather than substitutive.
