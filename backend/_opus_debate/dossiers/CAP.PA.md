# Forensic Dossier — Capgemini SE (CAP.PA)

*A deep dive on management and the setup, built from eight consecutive quarterly calls (Feb 2025 full-year through Feb 2026 full-year) cross-checked against the screener's hard financials.*

Capgemini is Europe's largest information-technology (IT) services and consulting group — it sells the people, software and now AI tools that big companies hire to run and rebuild their technology. The shares trade near €90, against the screener's fair-value estimates in the €155-164 range, so the market is pricing in roughly a 40%+ discount to a typical intrinsic-value read. The question this dossier answers is whether that discount is a fear-print on a stable franchise or a fair price for a business whose growth has stalled and whose core skill — billing offshore labor by the hour — is exactly what generative AI threatens.

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story management told across these eight quarters is one long, slow turn from defense back toward offense — with the offense increasingly bought rather than grown.

At the **start of the window (Feb 2025, FY2024 results)** the tone was frankly apologetic. Chief executive Aiman Ezzat opened by conceding "the market proved weaker than we anticipated," with revenue down 2% at constant currency and "unexpectedly strong headwinds in the second half, notably in manufacturing and in France." The whole 2024 message was resilience: hold the 13.3% operating margin, protect free cash flow, wait out subdued discretionary client spend.

Through **2025 the narrative shifted to "gradual improvement quarter after quarter."** Each call (Q1 minus 0.4%, Q2 plus 0.7%, Q3 plus 2.9% at constant currency) leaned harder on the same proof point: the targeted "actions" launched in late 2024 were working, and growth was inflecting. Two new growth pillars were introduced and steadily amplified — first **defense and sovereignty** (about €1bn of European defense revenue in 2024, growing roughly 12%, pitched as a multi-year structural tailwind), and second, and far more important, **"Intelligent Operations" powered by the WNS acquisition.**

By the **end of the window (Feb 2026, FY2025 results)** the WNS deal had closed (October 2025) and become the centerpiece. Reported Q4 growth of 10.6% at constant currency sounds like a turnaround — but management itself flagged that about 6.5 points of that was acquisition scope (WNS plus Cloud4C), so the underlying organic figure was closer to 4%. The same call introduced the new defensive counterweight: a **"Fit for Growth" restructuring with a €700m envelope**, an admission that AI is forcing a faster reskilling and headcount realignment than the original story implied.

What was quietly dropped: the early-window confidence that organic growth alone would carry the recovery. What was added: a large debt-funded acquisition and a large restructuring charge, both of which now do the heavy lifting in the headline numbers.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Rising — but from a low base, and carefully hedged throughout.**

- **Feb 2025 (FY2024):** defensive and candid — "the market proved weaker than we anticipated." Confidence low.
- **Apr 2025 (Q1):** cautiously better — "slightly better than our initial expectation," but Ezzat refused to over-claim: "let's not consider something has gone much, much better."
- **Jul 2025 (H1):** firmer — "performance continues to improve," returned to positive Q2 growth, but kept "the cautious stance adopted at the beginning of the year."
- **Oct 2025 (Q3):** clearly more confident — "Q3 was a strong quarter for the group, better than expected," with North America accelerating to +7%.
- **Feb 2026 (FY2025):** confident on strategy (WNS, defense, sovereignty) but newly defensive on its own workforce — the €700m "Fit for Growth" restructuring is an admission that AI requires faster, costly change.

The telling shift is *what kind* of confidence. Early on it was defensive (efficiency, cost, macro). By the end it is offensive-by-acquisition (WNS, mega-deals, Agentic AI) layered over a fresh defensive move on headcount. Ezzat remains disciplined — he repeatedly batted down hype, notably dismissing a Palantir "SAP migration in two weeks" claim as a "significantly wrong" headline. That discipline reads as credible rather than evasive.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY — Medium-to-High

The clearest credibility marker is that management **raised guidance through 2025 and then beat the raised number.** They entered 2025 with a wide constant-currency range of minus 2% to plus 2%, narrowed it to minus 1% to plus 1% at H1, and finished at +3.4% (helped by scope). Operating margin (13.3-13.5% target) landed at 13.3%; organic FCF (~€1.9bn target) landed at €1.95bn. The screener corroborates a strong earnings-beat record (beat 6 of 7 quarters, ~86%).

The honest mark-down: a chunk of the 2025 "beat" came from M&A scope they could fold in, and they were transparent about it (consistently separating the ~6.5 points of Q4 scope). They do not appear to sandbag aggressively; the guidance is realistic. Reliability: **Medium-to-High.**

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Three themes recurred and management never fully put them to rest:

1. **Is AI deflating their own revenue?** Asked repeatedly whether gen-AI savings get passed to clients and shrink billings, Ezzat conceded "yes, we are customers expect bit by bit cost improvement," framing it as normal productivity. The honest reading is that AI is a two-edged sword they are managing, not a pure tailwind — and the Fit for Growth restructuring is the tell that they know it.
2. **France and manufacturing weakness.** Analysts pressed on France being "past the trough" while growth stayed around minus 5%. Management admitted "broad weakness in France" and "structural changes" in automotive, with auto a "drag" for "the next 2 or 3 quarters."
3. **How much of the buyback offsets dilution vs. reduces share count?** Management dodged the specifics ("difficult to say... depending on the timing"), a minor non-answer on capital allocation.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 **Organic growth is essentially flat** — the headline acceleration is bought scope (WNS/Cloud4C ~6.5 points of Q4 2025 growth).
🔴 **The core model is in AI's crosshairs** — billing offshore labor-hours at a spread is precisely what gen-AI and Agentic AI compress; the €700m restructuring is management conceding it must reshape faster than planned.
🔴 **Leverage stepped up** — net debt of €6.43B after a debt/cash-funded $3.3B WNS deal, against only 9% ROIC; the strategic payoff is a 2027 synergy promise.
🔴 **France and automotive remain structural drags** with no near-term rebound.

🟢 **Margins and cash flow are genuinely resilient** — 13.3% operating margin held flat through a flat-revenue, weak-demand stretch; ~€1.95bn organic FCF on target.
🟢 **Credible, non-promotional management** — raised-then-beat guidance, transparent about scope vs. organic, and willing to debunk AI hype rather than ride it.
🟢 **Two real new demand pillars** — European defense (double-digit growth, structural multi-year ramp) and Intelligent Operations (first €600m+ mega-deal already signed on a non-headcount commercial model).
🟢 **The valuation is genuinely low** — price-to-free-cash-flow around 6.7x for a profitable market leader is a fear-level multiple.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

- **The vocabulary migrated from "growth" to "transformation of operations."** Early calls sold discretionary-spend recovery; later calls sell "Intelligent Operations" and "outcome-based / non-FTE commercial models" — a deliberate repositioning away from selling headcount-hours, because headcount-hours is the part AI erodes. The €600m mega-deal "operating on a non-FTE based commercial model" is the clearest signal of where they are trying to take the business.
- **"Revenue per head" became a recurring management metric.** Ezzat volunteered that WNS lowers revenue-per-head today but that Agentic AI should raise it "over the midterm." This is management explicitly acknowledging the labor-arbitrage model is under pressure and betting AI can decouple revenue from headcount before competitors do.
- **Sovereignty emerged as a new, demand-creating theme** (sovereign cloud, "Bleu," Cloud4C) — a genuine European tailwind that is harder for US-based rivals (Accenture, Cognizant) to claim.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Classification: PIVOTING.** Capgemini is mid-pivot from a classic labor-arbitrage IT-services model toward AI-enabled, outcome-priced "Intelligent Operations," and it is using a large debt-funded acquisition (WNS) plus a €700m restructuring to force that pivot faster than organic evolution would allow.

**The single most important thing to weigh before sizing a position:** this is a value question wearing a transition costume. The franchise is cheap (≈6.7x free cash flow), profitable and run by credible managers — but the very engine that makes it cheap, offshore labor billed by the hour, is the textbook victim of generative-AI displacement. The director must decide whether management's pivot to non-headcount, AI-led operations outruns the deflation of its legacy bill-rates. If it does, this is a fear-print on a durable franchise; if it does not, the low multiple is a value trap on a structurally shrinking base.

**Moat:** intangible/scale-based (a top-tier global brand, deep client relationships, vendor-consolidation share gains — "if you're big, you stay in and you win"). But the returns evidence — 9% ROIC, flat not rising; stable not expanding gross margin — says this moat is **narrow and being pressured, not widening.** It is real (the firm wins vendor-consolidation deals and holds margin) but it is not a fortress, and the structural threat is live.

**Secular threat:** **material.** Generative-AI displacement of IT-services labor arbitrage is a genuine terminal-value risk, and management's own actions (Fit for Growth restructuring, the "revenue per head" framing, the push to non-FTE pricing) confirm they see it. It is not yet *terminal* — the firm is large enough to be a consolidator and is repositioning toward the AI side of the trade rather than only the victim side — but it caps how much credit the cheap multiple deserves.
