# Forensic Dossier — Amadeus IT Group (AMS.MC)

*Five consecutive earnings calls reviewed: full-year 2024 results (Feb 28, 2025), Q1 2025 (May 8, 2025), first-half 2025 (Aug 1, 2025), Q3 2025 (Nov 7, 2025), and full-year 2025 results plus the new midterm outlook (Feb 27, 2026). Amadeus is the Madrid-listed travel-technology backbone — the "toll road" that connects airlines, hotels, travel agencies and airports. It is not a semiconductor company despite the ticker. Financials are from our screener scan, with the latest-quarter figures from the trailing-twelve-month block.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story is remarkably consistent across these five calls — which is itself a finding. Amadeus is a steady, double-digit-growing infrastructure business, and management talks like the custodian of one. The opening recap (full-year 2024) led with "double digit growth and expanding profitability," 13% revenue growth, and a fresh €1.3 billion buyback aimed at mopping up shares issued during the pandemic. By the close of this window (full-year 2025), the headline had shifted only slightly in tone — to "we have reached a tipping point" on Nevio, its next-generation airline-retailing platform — but the underlying message was the same: grow the toll road, invest heavily, return the rest.

Three threads run through every call. First, **Nevio / Navitaire Stratos** — the modernization of how airlines sell tickets (moving from old fare-filing to "offers and orders"). This thread *strengthened* materially over the year: from "four customers" (Air France-KLM joining BA, Saudia, Finnair) in early 2025 to the February 2026 announcement that nine Lufthansa Group airlines plan to adopt Nevio, plus TUI and Volotea on the low-cost Stratos product, and management's claim that "25% of Altéa passengers boarded are engaged in a Nevio program." Second, **NDC aggregation** (a new airline-content standard) — steady, almost ritual progress, from ~70 signed agreements early to "over 75" by year-end, with the honest admission that adoption as a *percentage* stays flat because every new airline enlarges the denominator. Third, **AI** — the emphasis here grew loudest, evolving from "we've used AI for 20 years" boilerplate into a genuine strategic frame by Q4 2025: Amadeus wants to be "the orchestrator in an AI-enabled travel ecosystem… the essential infrastructure powering" AI travel assistants.

What was quietly *dropped*: nothing was abandoned, but the pandemic-recovery framing ("post-COVID re-engagement," buyback to undo crisis dilution) faded as the recovery normalized, replaced by a more mature "high single-digit revenue / low double-digit EPS" midterm algorithm announced in February 2026.

The one place the story is *richer than the numbers* is the moat/returns framing — a 9.9% return on capital does not scream "uncrossable moat."

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Stable-to-rising, with rising confidence specifically on Nevio.**

- **FY24 (Feb 2025):** Measured, delivery-focused. "We successfully deliver on our outlook and commitments." Offensive posture (investing for the future, cloud transformation).
- **Q1 2025 (May):** A notch more defensive on the macro — "despite the broad uncertainty in the environment" — and a tactical retreat to guiding *ex-foreign-exchange* because dollar/euro volatility was "beyond what we could foresee." Volumes "came in a bit below, but nevertheless quite good." This is the most hedged call in the set, and it coincided with the softest booking growth (+2.5%).
- **H1 2025 (Aug):** Continued macro caution (geopolitics, U.S. travel softness, a France controller strike, the Israel-Iran conflict) but a hopeful pivot: "hopefully we have seen the bottom of this deceleration."
- **Q3 2025 (Nov):** Tone clearly *lifted* — "a strong third quarter full of momentum, which drove revenue growth acceleration and margin expansion."
- **FY25 (Feb 2026):** The most confident call in the window. "We have reached a tipping point" on Nevio; a clean, specific three-part midterm algorithm; AI reframed as opportunity, not threat.

The arc is offensive → briefly defensive (mid-2025 macro wobble) → offensive again, ending on a high. Guidance got *more* specific over time (the February 2026 midterm targets are the most concrete commitment in the file).

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**High.** This is the strongest part of the dossier. Management set an ex-foreign-exchange 2025 outlook in May 2025 and, by February 2026, stated plainly: "we successfully delivered on our '25 outlook… achieving our 2025 guidance across all metrics." The screener corroborates a 6-of-7-quarter earnings-beat rate (86%). When they warned of weaker first-half free cash flow on working-capital and capital-expenditure timing (Q1 25), the second-half reacceleration they promised did materialize (full-year FCF landed at €1.3B). There is a mild *sandbagging* flavor — they repeatedly called their own three-year air-distribution compound-growth target "conservative" (Deutsche Bank pushed on exactly this in early 2025), and they tend to under-promise on currency by guiding constant-currency. Reliability: **High.**

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

The recurring analyst worries, and how management handled them:

- **Nevio deal pace.** The most persistent pressure point. Rothschild (Nov 2025): "we haven't seen a new customer for a while… are you comfortable with the current pace?" Management deflected to "engagement is high… nothing is done until it's really done." This was *answered* three months later by the Lufthansa/TUI/Volotea wins — so the worry was legitimate but resolved favorably. The risk: deal timing is genuinely lumpy and outside management's control ("it's a matter of priorities for the carriers").
- **Free-cash-flow seasonality.** JP Morgan pressed twice on first-half FCF being down year-over-year. Management's answer (working capital, tax timing, capex ramp) held up — full-year delivered.
- **Pricing durability.** Kepler Cheuvreux repeatedly asked whether the +5% revenue-per-booking growth is sustainable. Management was candid that the one-off boost from converting "full-content" to "relevant-content" agreements will fade and 2026 revenue-per-booking will "soften slightly." This is the honest admission of a *decelerating* pricing lever — worth weighting.
- **AI as threat vs. tool.** Bernstein (Feb 2026) pushed hard on whether AI coding tools should let R&D spend *plateau*. Management said no — capex stays low-double-digit (though declining as a share) — framing AI as "force multiplication," not cost-out. The subtext: the spending floor stays high.

No question turned into a later disaster within this window. The deflections were on timing, not substance.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 **Gross margin contracting** (27.8%, flagged "contracting") on adverse content mix (more low-cost-carrier / local bookings dilute unit economics) — the pricing tailwind is lapping.
🔴 **Return on invested capital below 10%** (9.9%) despite a premium "moat" narrative — heavy R&D and €2.68B net debt weigh on capital efficiency.
🔴 **Revenue-per-booking growth explicitly guided to soften** in 2026 (management's own words) — a known deceleration of a key lever.
🔴 **Volume is macro-hostage** — bookings and passengers-boarded swung with geopolitics (Mideast, U.S. softness, strikes) through 2025; management cannot control air traffic.

🟢 **Free-cash-flow margin of 32%** and trailing free cash flow of €1.74B — elite cash conversion.
🟢 **Guidance delivered in full** for 2025; 86% beat rate; a clear new midterm algorithm.
🟢 **Nevio reached genuine commercial scale** — Lufthansa Group (9 airlines), British Airways, Air France-KLM all engaged; 25% of Altéa passengers in a Nevio program; this is the durable growth engine working.
🟢 **AI positioned as ally** — "the essential infrastructure powering" AI travel assistants, with Google and Microsoft as named partners and the first travel "data mesh." Credible, not hand-wavy.
🟢 **Net leverage minimal** — 0.8–0.9x EBITDA; the €2.68B net debt is trivially serviced by €1.7B+ of annual free cash flow.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

- **A new CFO (Carol/Caroline Borg, started May 2025) brought a cleaner, more numbers-forward communication style** — she explicitly said she was "increasing transparency" on traffic assumptions and gave crisper guidance bridges. New voices that *add* disclosure are a green signal.
- **The terminology shifted from "GDS / distribution" toward "orchestrator of an AI-enabled travel ecosystem."** This is a deliberate repositioning ahead of the obvious bear question — whether AI agents disintermediate the middleman. Management is getting in front of it, framing Amadeus as the neutral data/infrastructure layer agents *need*, not the layer they replace. The Skylink acquisition (conversational-AI capability) reinforces this.
- **Capital allocation language is widening.** By Q3 2025 the CFO listed M&A as "a really key relevant part of our growth strategy" alongside buybacks and dividends, and promised a February budget update — a subtle signal that fresh capital-return or deal decisions are pending, not that a special situation is imminent.
- **The honest "disintermediation is real but we offset it" admission** (Q3 2025) on full-service carriers selling more direct — management neither denied the structural pressure nor panicked, which reads as credible.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Trajectory: STRENGTHENING.** Over these five calls the franchise got stronger on every axis that matters — Nevio crossed from pilot to scale, guidance was delivered in full, free cash flow compounded, and management got ahead of the AI question rather than ducking it. This is a high-quality, cash-generative infrastructure compounder run by a credible, slightly conservative team.

**The single most important thing the director must weigh before sizing:** this is a *quality-compounder-at-a-fair-price*, not a special situation. There is no dated catalyst, no activist, no asset sale, no forced selling. The entire return case rests on (a) the market continuing to re-rate a steady ~8% revenue / low-double-digit EPS grower toward its intrinsic value, and (b) the AI-agent disintermediation fear *not* proving terminal. The valuation is the edge (price-to-free-cash-flow ~10x, well below this asset's history and quality), but with no forcing function the convergence is slow and depends on macro travel demand staying healthy. Size it as a patient quality holding, not a catalyst trade.

**Economic moat: WIDE but the returns evidence is only NARROW-confirming — call it NARROW-to-WIDE, trend STABLE.** The moat type is genuine **switching-costs plus network/scale** — airlines, agencies and hotels are deeply integrated into Amadeus's core systems (25-year Ryanair relationship; "natively integrated… others cannot easily replicate"). That is real lock-in. *But* the hard returns evidence tempers the width: a 9.9% return on invested capital and a *contracting* gross margin are not the fingerprints of a widening, uncrossable moat — they are the fingerprints of a strong franchise spending heavily to defend and extend its position. The moat is durable, not expanding.

**Secular threat: MANAGEABLE, not terminal.** The relevant structural force is AI-driven disintermediation (the "AI-displacement" tail — could AI agents route around the travel middleman?). The evidence says *manageable*: bookings and passengers-boarded are still *growing*, management is positioning as the AI infrastructure layer rather than the disrupted incumbent, and the switching costs are deep enough that a wholesale bypass is a multi-year, not imminent, risk. This is a fear-print / mispricing, not a structurally shrinking base — the cheapness reflects macro-travel uncertainty and a generalized "is AI coming for the middleman" worry, against a business whose volumes are still rising. It is *not* a value trap, but the AI question is the one that would change the rating if the evidence turned.
