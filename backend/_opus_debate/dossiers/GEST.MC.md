# Forensic Dossier — Gestamp Automoción (GEST.MC)

*A deep dive on management and the setup, built from three consecutive earnings calls (nine-month 2025, full-year 2025, and first-quarter 2026) cross-checked against the company's actual financial trajectory.*

Gestamp is a Spanish Tier-1 auto supplier — it stamps, hot-forms and welds the metal body structure and chassis parts that sit underneath a car. Its fortunes track global vehicle production, not a consumer brand, so the story is fundamentally a cyclical-industrial one dressed up by a credible, disciplined management team.

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

Across the three calls the story shifts cleanly from "grow with the market" to "protect profitability and the balance sheet while the market does not grow." The Executive Chairman, Francisco Riberas, opens nine-month 2025 conceding "this year remained very challenging with adverse FX impact for us and also negative volumes in our core markets," and by full-year 2025 he reframes the whole year as "increasing profitability without growth." That phrase is the thesis of the franchise right now.

Three drivers carry the new narrative: (1) the Phoenix Plan, a multi-year restructuring of the loss-making North American operations toward an 8%-plus profit margin; (2) relentless cost and "flexibility" measures in Europe to defend margin as volumes fall; and (3) balance-sheet repair, with leverage taken to a post-IPO low. A growth driver that was quietly demoted is the old "outsourcing tailwind" — an analyst (BNP's Francisco Ruiz) explicitly asked whether the structural share-gain story "is already over," and management did not reaffirm it, pivoting instead to "focus in profitability rather just to be waiting for volumes to come back." A new growth driver introduced is China and India: rapid gains with domestic Chinese makers (Edscha hinges now "more than 30% of our sales are in China") and aggressive plant investment in India.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

| Management claim | The numbers say | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Increasing profitability" — auto margin up to 11.9% (FY25) | Reported net income fell from €188M (2024) to €152M (2025), EPS €0.33 → €0.27; gross margin in the scan is 14.9% and "contracting" | Margin claim is real at the EBITDA line, but it does NOT reach the bottom line — depreciation, amortization and FX interest erode it. The story is richer at EBITDA than the EPS the equity owner actually banks. |
| "Very sound free cash flow of €228M more than guided" (FY25) | Screener TTM free cash flow is €205M; full-year FCF growth +177.6% YoY | Corroborated. Cash conversion is the genuine bright spot and the most credible part of the narrative. |
| "Reducing our leverage ratio to 1.4x EBITDA, lowest since the IPO" | Net debt €1.82B at FY25, then back up to €1.97B by Q1 2026 (seasonal working-capital swing); total debt €3.19B vs €1.22B cash | True at year-end, but net debt is still ~€2B and rebuilds intra-year. Altman-Z of 1.2 sits in the statistical distress zone — leverage is improved, not resolved. |
| Revenue "almost flat at FX constant," underperformance "only China" | Reported revenue fell from €12.27B (2023) to €12.00B (2024) to €11.35B (2025), a clear downtrend; EPS five-year CAGR is −8.2% | The FX-and-China framing is fair, but on a reported, owner's-eye basis the top line and EPS have been shrinking for two years. The narrative is poorer than the constant-currency gloss. |
| "Order book reaches €47.5B" (five-year, per FY25 disclosure) | Revenue declining −5.4% YoY | A genuine forward asset, but order books in this industry are notoriously soft on price and timing; it has not arrested the revenue decline yet. |

The throughline: every margin and cash claim checks out at the operating line, yet net income and EPS keep falling. This is a company doing the right operational things into a falling tide.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Stable, and notably defensive throughout.** This is not a deteriorating-confidence story; it is a management team that has been consistently sober for a year. Nine-month 2025: "we are not able to control in the short term what is going on with the market and the FX." Full-year 2025: "we are assuming that the bad news are going to be there, and we are putting a lot of stress in the operations." First-quarter 2026: a small, genuine note of pride — "flat compared with Q1 2025 at FX constant and clearly outperforming the market" (Gestamp beat the market by 2.3% in the quarter). The language is uniformly operational and cost-led (flexibility, efficiency, Phoenix, pass-through) rather than growth-led. Guidance got more specific over time, not less — by Q1 2026 management is committing to precise full-year targets (auto EBITDA margin above 11.9%, North America above 10%). That specificity is a confidence tell.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**High.** The cleanest evidence: in full-year 2025 management states it "met all the 2025 upgraded targets" — margin, leverage and free cash flow — and the free-cash-flow figure (€228M) came in above its own guide. The Phoenix Plan hit its 8.1% North American margin target "in a market which has been much weaker than expected" (North American volumes came in ~6% below the plan's assumption, yet the margin target was still met). Delivering a margin commitment while the volume assumption underneath it missed badly is the strongest possible credibility signal. The one offset is the screener's quarterly earnings-beat rate of 43% (3 of 7), which reflects the difficulty of forecasting FX-driven EPS, not operational unreliability. Reliability on the levers management actually controls: High.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Three recurring pressure points: (1) **Capital allocation** — Ruiz pushed directly on "what are you going to do with the cash" at record-low leverage; management deflected ("not now our first priority… too early"). This is the single most important non-answer in the transcripts. (2) **The €34M of customer "settlements"** booked at EBITDA (with a €52M asset write-down behind them) tied to cancelled EV programs — Ruiz asked for the divisional split and whether more are coming in 2026/27; management declined to break it out, saying only "probably there will be a little bit more." These are one-off-flavored credits propping the EBITDA line, and they recur. (3) **Western Europe underperformance** — ODDO's Anthony Dick pressed on accelerating underperformance; management attributed it to program-specific timing and insisted "are we losing market share in Europe? The answer is no."

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 Reported revenue and EPS have fallen two years running (EPS €0.49 in 2023 → €0.27 in 2025); the equity owner's earnings are shrinking even as EBITDA margin rises.
🔴 Altman-Z of 1.2 is in the statistical distress zone, and net debt rebuilds to ~€2B intra-year despite the year-end low.
🔴 Recurring EV-program write-downs and customer-settlement credits (€34M EBITDA benefit / €52M write-down in Q4 2025; another €15M write-down in Q1 2026) inject one-offs into the "clean" margin story.
🔴 Capital-return question repeatedly deflected — no buyback, no special distribution, only a small ~€0.09/share ordinary dividend.
🟢 Free cash flow genuinely strong and de-leveraging real; leverage at a post-IPO low of 1.4x.
🟢 Phoenix Plan delivering its margin target despite a far weaker market — operational execution is excellent.
🟢 Real, if early, share gains with domestic Chinese makers and in India; the company is positioning for the next mix, not denying it.
🟢 Q1 2026 finally showed a market-beat (+2.3%) and scrap profitability inflecting back up.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The most telling shift is linguistic: the word "growth" has been almost fully replaced by "profitability," "flexibility" and "balance-sheet strength." When asked about the old outsourcing-led share-gain engine, management would not re-endorse it. A second signal is the framing of the customer settlements — they are described as customers "doing a clear recognition of what we have been doing for them," i.e. compensation for stranded EV-program investment. Read plainly: the EV transition is destroying program economics, and the cash-back negotiations are a managed wind-down, not a growth event. Third, on capital allocation the Chairman volunteered "there will be some consolidation… opportunities" — a hint that the deleveraged balance sheet is being kept dry for M&A in a consolidating supplier industry, not for shareholder returns.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Trajectory: STABLE.** This is a well-run, honestly-managed cyclical industrial executing a margin-and-balance-sheet defense into a flat-to-down end market. It is neither strengthening (the top line and EPS are still falling) nor deteriorating (cash flow and leverage are improving and guidance is being met).

The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: **this is a price-taking Tier-1 metal-bashing supplier whose owner's earnings (net income, EPS) have fallen for two straight years even as operating margin improved — so the cheapness (4.7x price-to-free-cash-flow) is a fair multiple on a structurally low-margin, capital-intensive, FX-and-OEM-cycle-exposed business, not an obvious mispricing.** The DCF and earnings-power-value models both put the margin of safety slightly negative; only the asset-based Graham figure is positive.

**Moat: NARROW.** The durable advantage is scale and customer switching costs (Gestamp is designed into multi-year vehicle platforms with high tooling lock-in), which is why it holds share. But returns are thin: return on invested capital of 6.1% is near or below the cost of capital, and gross margin is described as "contracting." A real but shallow moat. **Moat trend: STABLE** — returns are not collapsing, but they are not widening either.

**Secular threat: MANAGEABLE.** The relevant structural force is the powertrain shift to electric vehicles. Crucially, Gestamp makes the body-in-white and chassis structure — the metal that exists regardless of whether the car is gas or electric — so it is far less exposed than a drivetrain or exhaust supplier. The EV-program cancellations are hurting program-by-program economics today, but they do not impair the terminal need for stamped body structures. This is a cyclical/transition dip with one-off costs, not a melting terminal base. The cheapness is therefore best read as a fear-print on a low-margin cyclical, not a value trap on a dying business — but also not deep, asymmetric value, because the business is genuinely low-return.
