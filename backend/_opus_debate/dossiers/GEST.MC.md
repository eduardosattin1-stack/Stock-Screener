# Forensic Dossier — Gestamp Automoción (GEST.MC)

*A deep dive on management and the setup, prepared for the investment committee. Gestamp is a Spanish maker of the metal "body-in-white" structures, chassis parts and hinges that go into roughly one in five cars built worldwide — a Tier-1 auto supplier whose fortunes track global vehicle production, not any consumer brand. Three consecutive earnings calls are on the table: the nine-month 2025 update (November 2025), the full-year 2025 results (February 2026), and the first-quarter 2026 results (May 2026).*

## 1. Narrative Arc

The story management tells has narrowed sharply over these three quarters — and that narrowing is itself the most important signal. Two or three years ago Gestamp sold itself as a structural grower: a supplier winning share through "outsourcing" (carmakers handing more stamping work to specialists) and riding the shift to heavier electric-vehicle structures. By the November 2025 call that growth language is essentially gone. Executive Chairman Francisco Riberas opens with "this year remained very challenging," and the entire pitch has become "increasing profitability without growth." He repeats almost verbatim across all three calls: the focus is "profitability and balance-sheet strength," not volume.

The single thread that strengthens across the quarters is the balance sheet. In November 2025 leverage was 1.6x net-debt-to-earnings (before interest, tax, depreciation and amortization — EBITDA); by the February 2026 full-year call it had fallen to 1.4x, which management called "the lowest since the IPO" (the 2017 stock-market listing). The Phoenix Plan — a restructuring of the loss-making North American operations — is the other consistent win: North American profitability climbed from a 7% margin in 2024 to 8.1% in 2025 and is tracking toward a 10% target for 2026.

What quietly dropped out: the China growth story. In November 2025 management spent real airtime on "more than 45% growth" with Chinese carmakers; by Q1 2026 China is described as down ~10% on volume and the region is a "clear underperformance." India replaced China as the designated growth market. The EV-structure thesis also softened into a liability — 2025 and Q1 2026 both carried write-downs (EUR 52 million of asset write-downs in 2025, another EUR 15 million in Q1 2026) tied to customers slowing their EV roll-outs.

## 2. Claims vs Financials (forensic)

| Management claim | The numbers | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Profitability is improving" | Auto-business EBITDA margin rose 11.1% → 11.9% in 2025; Q1 2026 margin +52 bps year-on-year | **Holds** — margin gains are real and consistent |
| "Solid set of results" | Revenue −5.4% in 2025; net income fell 188M → 152M (−19%); EPS 0.33 → 0.27 | **Poorer than the words** — the bottom line is shrinking, not solid |
| "Lowest leverage since IPO" | Net debt fell to ~1.82B at year-end 2025… | **Holds at year-end, but watch the seasonality** |
| (same) | …yet net debt is back to **1.97B as of 31 March 2026** | **Important caveat** — the "all-time-low" figure is a December snapshot; debt swings up ~150M by Q1 on seasonal working capital. This is a meaningfully leveraged balance sheet, not a cash fortress |
| Free cash flow is strong | 2025 free cash flow EUR 249M reported (EUR 278M ex-restructuring), "more than guided"; Q1 2026 free cash flow was negative | **Holds, with seasonality** — cash generation is real but lumpy and back-half-weighted |
| Margin gains from operations | Q4 2025 EBITDA flattered by EUR 34M of one-off customer settlements; Q1 2026 net income flattered by a EUR 23M financing accounting gain | **Partly cosmetic** — a chunk of the recent "improvement" is non-recurring items, not clean operations |

The forensic punchline: this is a company defending profitability on a **falling revenue base**. Revenue peaked at 12.27B in 2023 and has fallen two years running to 11.35B in 2025. Earnings per share has compounded **down** about 8% a year over five years. Management's framing ("solid," "very good year") is consistently a half-step warmer than the trajectory deserves — not dishonest, but optimistic packaging of a business in slow decline. One genuine bright spot the numbers confirm: trailing-twelve-month diluted EPS has recovered to 0.31 (from the 0.27 fiscal-year figure) and the latest quarter's EPS jumped ~85% year-on-year, so the very recent earnings trend has turned up off a low base.

## 3. Tone & Confidence Trajectory

**Stable, and notably defensive throughout.** There is no offensive growth language left to lose — by November 2025 the tone had already settled into "manage what we can control: cost, flexibility, the balance sheet." Riberas is candid about the things outside his control: foreign-exchange (a ~EUR 334M revenue drag in nine-month 2025, and a stated EUR 1.5B revenue loss versus 2022 currency levels), weak volumes, scrap-metal prices. The confidence that remains is concentrated in two places — the Phoenix Plan and the leverage ratio — and on both, management has so far delivered what it promised. The language is consistent and unhurried across all three calls; there is no panic, but there is also no enthusiasm.

## 4. Guidance Credibility

**Medium-to-High, and this is the company's strongest suit.** The Phoenix Plan targets were set publicly and then hit: an 8% North American margin for 2025 was guided and delivered (8.1%) in a market that came in ~6% weaker on volume than assumed when the plan was launched. The 2025 free-cash-flow guide was beaten. Full-year 2026 guidance is concrete — auto-business EBITDA margin above 11.9% and North American margin above 10% — and Q1 2026 is tracking it. Against the screener's earnings-beat record of only 3 of the last 7 quarters (43%), the picture is mixed on headline beats but clean on the self-set operational milestones management actually controls. They under-promise on revenue (explicitly refusing to guide top line, "better to assume bad news") and deliver on margin and cash. That is a credible, if unexciting, posture.

## 5. Analyst Pressure Points

Three recurring pressure points, and management's handling of each:

1. **"What will you do with the cash now that leverage is at a record low?"** (BNP Paribas, February 2026). Riberas explicitly punted: "what to do in the future with that is something that is not now our first priority… today it's very early." He floated consolidation, opportunities, and "increase remuneration to shareholders" as possibilities — but committed to none. **This is the load-bearing question for any value thesis, and management refused to answer it.**

2. **Western-European underperformance** (ODDO, repeatedly). Management insists it is not losing share — blaming specific programs (a UK program in 2025) and offsetting with Eastern-European growth. Plausible, but the underperformance has persisted across multiple quarters.

3. **The EUR 34M one-off customer settlements** — analysts pressed on whether more are coming. Riberas declined to give detail but signalled "a little bit more in 2026," confirming these flatter near-term margins.

## 6. Red Flags / Green Flags

🔴 Revenue has fallen two consecutive years; EPS down ~8%/year over five years — the base is shrinking.
🔴 Altman-Z score of 1.2 sits in the statistical "distress" zone (a solvency screen; below ~1.8 is the warning band) — driven by thin margins and ~2B of net debt against a low-margin business.
🔴 Recent profit improvement is partly cosmetic (EUR 34M + EUR 23M of one-offs across Q4 2025 / Q1 2026).
🔴 EV-program write-downs in two straight periods show the EV-structure growth bet is misfiring.
🔴 The capital-return catalyst was explicitly deferred by management — "not now our first priority."

🟢 Phoenix Plan delivering on every milestone — North America margin 7% → 8.1% → tracking 10%.
🟢 Deleveraging is real (1.4x year-end, lowest since IPO) and free cash flow is positive and beat guidance.
🟢 Eastern Europe is a genuine bright spot — 15.2% EBITDA margin, the strongest region, growing on Turkey ramp-ups.
🟢 Scrap prices turned up in Q1 2026 after a punishing 2025, a tailwind for the recycling arm.
🟢 Q1 2026 organic sales outperformed the market by 2.3% — the first clear outperformance in the set.

## 7. Hidden Signals

The most telling shift is geographic: China moved from hero to problem child in two quarters, and the company is repositioning toward India and Eastern Europe — lower-cost, higher-margin footprints. Riberas now openly describes "year-after-year downsizing of our operations in Western Europe." Read between the lines and this is a company quietly shrinking its high-cost legacy base to defend margins — a rational but defensive posture that caps the growth ceiling. Second signal: the repeated, almost ritual insistence on "constructive customer negotiations" and tooling-payment collection as a profit lever. When a supplier's margin story leans on year-end settlements and collecting tooling money, the underlying operating leverage is thin. Third, and most important for governance: the founder family (the two Riberas brothers plus their holding company) controls roughly 70-75% of the shares. That single fact means no outside investor can force a sale, a break-up, or a large capital return — the cash-return decision rests entirely with a family that just said it is in no hurry.

## 8. Capital-Allocation Verdict (for the director)

**Classification: STABLE — a competent, family-controlled cyclical defending profitability on a slowly shrinking revenue base, deleveraging steadily but with no committed catalyst to release the value.**

The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: **this is a deep-value multiple (4.7x price-to-free-cash-flow) whose re-rating depends on a capital-return decision that a 70%+ controlling family has explicitly declined to make.** Without an activist (impossible here, given control) or a committed buyback/special dividend, the cheapness can persist indefinitely. The deleveraging is genuine and the operations are well-run, but a low multiple on a low-margin, FX-battered, slowly-declining auto supplier with distress-zone solvency metrics is exactly the profile that stays cheap.

On the economic moat: Gestamp has a **narrow** moat at best — scale and engineering know-how in hot-stamping and EV chassis give it some cost and switching-cost advantage with carmakers, but a 6.1% return on invested capital (below its likely cost of capital) and a gross margin contracting to 14.9% show the moat is not generating excess returns. This is an **eroding** position: returns and margins on the auto business are being defended by cost-cutting, not widened by pricing power. On secular threat: the structural risk is the long electric-vehicle transition reshaping vehicle architecture plus the rise of Chinese carmakers who increasingly localize their own supply — a **material** (not yet terminal) threat. Gestamp is adapting (EV chassis, door rings, Chinese-OEM wins), but the EV write-downs show the transition is a cost as much as an opportunity. The cheapness is best read as a **fear-print blended with a genuinely impaired growth profile** — not a clean mispricing, and not yet a terminal value trap, but closer to a value trap than to hidden upside given the controlled share register.
