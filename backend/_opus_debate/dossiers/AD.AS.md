# Forensic Dossier — Ahold Delhaize (AD.AS)

*A deep dive on management and the setup, built from eight consecutive quarterly earnings calls (February 2025 through February 2026) cross-checked against the screener's hard financials.*

Ahold Delhaize is a pan-Atlantic grocery operator — it runs Albert Heijn and bol in the Netherlands, Delhaize in Belgium, Stop & Shop, Food Lion, Hannaford and Giant in the eastern United States, and Profi/Mega Image in Romania. It is a low-margin, high-volume, capital-intensive food retailer. The investment question is narrow: at roughly four times free cash flow, is the market pricing a structurally shrinking business, or is it simply ignoring a steady, cash-generative compounder?

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story has been remarkably consistent — almost monotonous — across all eight quarters, which for a defensive grocer is itself a positive signal. The spine is management's "Growing Together" strategy, unveiled in mid-2024 and repeated nearly verbatim every quarter since: grow sales faster than the industry at roughly a 4% compound annual rate, hold a leading underlying operating margin of about 4%, and convert that into steady earnings and free-cash-flow growth that funds dividends and buybacks.

What evolved within that frame is the emphasis. Early in 2025 the focus was on the heavy lifting — the Stop & Shop turnaround in the US (store closures, a $1 billion four-year price-investment program), the Belgium "Future Plan" franchise conversion, and integrating the Profi acquisition in Romania. By late 2025 and into the February 2026 call, the tone shifted toward harvesting: e-commerce reached full profitability "on a fully allocated basis" (a milestone management flagged as arriving earlier than expected), own-brand penetration climbed toward 39.8% group-wide (close to 50% in Europe), and retail media (advertising income) was repeatedly upgraded to a "growth machine."

Two growth drivers were introduced and amplified over time: retail media / advertising income, and artificial-intelligence-driven productivity (forecasting, store-first fulfillment, the Albert Heijn app, bol's "Spot & Shop"). Nothing material was quietly dropped — the Stop & Shop turnaround is still openly a multi-year project ("done in year four"), which is honest rather than evasive.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

| Management claim | The hard numbers | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "~4% sales CAGR" (Growing Together) | Revenue €86.98B (2022) → €92.35B (2025); 3-yr CAGR +2.0%, latest year +3.4%; FY2025 net sales +5.9% (Profi/M&A-aided) | **Partly supported.** Organic comparable growth (~3.2% ex-gas in 2025) is real but the headline 4% leans on acquisitions and currency. |
| "~4% underlying operating margin, leading" | Reported underlying margin 4.0% in 2025; screener net margin 2.4%, gross 26.5% (stable) | **Supported.** The 4% is an *underlying operating* margin (pre-unusuals); the thin 2.4% net margin is normal for grocery and consistent. |
| "Strong free cash flow funding returns" | TTM free cash flow €5.27B; FY2025 FCF strong; 2026 guide ≥€2.3B; P/FCF ~4.2x | **Strongly supported** — and the crux of the value case. (Note the wide gap between the ~€5.3B TTM figure and the €2.3B forward guide; the guide is the conservative, post-capex run-rate to trust.) |
| EPS "mid-to-high single digit growth" | Diluted underlying EPS +7.8% in 2025; reported EPS €2.5 latest, +32% year-on-year off a depressed 2024 base; 5-yr EPS CAGR +3.6% | **Supported.** The +32% optical jump flatters a 2023–24 trough; the durable rate is the high-single-digit underlying figure. |
| "Online now profitable, fully allocated" | Margin stable, no separate disclosure | **Plausible but unverifiable from the financials** — management stopped breaking it out, which removes the ability to check it independently. |
| Stop & Shop "improving trends, gaining share" | US impairments ran ~€200M+/yr; management insists this is "incidental," not a run-rate | **Watch item.** The repeated US store impairments are the one place the narrative is richer than the evidence. |

The headline takeaway: the financials *corroborate* the cash-generation and margin claims, which are the load-bearing planks of a deep-value thesis. The growth claims are real but modest and partly bought. Returns are pedestrian — return on equity 14.6%, return on invested capital 7.3% — acceptable for capital-intensive grocery but not evidence of a widening moat.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Stable-to-rising, with disciplined restraint.** Management's confidence is steady and, if anything, firmer by the February 2026 call. Frans Muller (CEO) repeatedly bats away requests for segment-level or quarterly margin guidance — "live with the group guidance on margin, Rob" (May 2025) — which reads as discipline, not evasion, because the *group* guidance is specific and gets reiterated. The language stays offensive (growth, investment, densify-and-grow) rather than retreating into pure cost-defense, even while acknowledging a "volatile" macro backdrop (tariffs, egg prices, SNAP-benefit delays, Serbian price controls, Romanian VAT). CFO Jolanda Poots-Bijl is consistently the more conservative voice ("we take out pain rather early than later" on impairments), which lends credibility.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**High.** The screener shows an earnings-beat rate of 6 of the last 7 quarters (86%). Across the transcript window management set group targets (≈4% growth, ≈4% margin, free cash flow floors) and then delivered against them — FY2025 landed at 4.0% underlying margin, +7.8% underlying EPS, and a reaffirmed dividend (+6% to €1.24) and €1 billion buyback. The Q1 2026 update reiterated full-year guidance (free cash flow of at least €2.3 billion, ~4% margin, mid-to-high-single-digit EPS growth) despite a seasonally negative Q1 free-cash-flow figure that management correctly pre-flagged as timing. There is a mild sandbagging pattern (the conservative €2.3B floor against ~€5.3B trailing). Reliability: **High.**

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Analysts pushed hardest on three things, and management's handling tells you where the soft spots are:
- **US margin trajectory and Stop & Shop** — the most-asked topic, and the one management most consistently refuses to quantify quarter-by-quarter. The price-investment program ($1B over four years) is a margin headwind they ask the market to take on faith.
- **US store impairments** (UBS, August 2025) — a "EUR 200 million plus per year run rate" that management firmly disputes as incidental (Stop & Shop closures, Fresh Direct, fulfillment-center exits). If impairments persist beyond those one-offs, it would undercut the "store-first model is working" claim.
- **Profi (Romania)** — analysts flagged cut full-year sales guidance for Profi (November 2025), which management attributed to Romanian VAT and food-voucher timing, not structural weakness.

None of these has yet "turned into bad news," but the US price-investment margin drag is the live tension.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🟢 Reaffirmed and growing capital returns — €1B buyback plus a 40–50% payout dividend (+6% in 2025).
🟢 E-commerce reaching full-allocated profitability earlier than planned (February 2026) — removes a long-standing margin drag.
🟢 Consistent guidance delivery (86% beat rate) and a strong Piotroski financial-health score of 8/9.
🟢 Retail-media / advertising income emerging as a genuine high-margin growth lever.
🔴 Recurring US store impairments (~€200M/yr) that management insists are one-offs — needs to actually fade.
🔴 Online profitability de-disclosed ("we look at it holistically now") — convenient timing removes a check.
🔴 Returns are merely adequate (return on invested capital 7.3%) and the $1B US price-investment program is a self-imposed multi-year margin drag.
🟡 Growth is acquisition-assisted (Profi, Louis Delhaize), so organic momentum is slower than the headline.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The vocabulary migrated from "turnaround" language (Stop & Shop "interventions," Belgium "Future Plan") toward "scale and harvest" language (retail media "growth machine," "build once, scale across brands," AI productivity). New executive voices — a new Chief Technology Officer (Jan Brecht) and a US Chief Commercial and Digital Officer (Keith) — signal a deliberate tilt toward technology-and-data monetization rather than store operations. Management's framing of bol versus Chinese marketplaces (Temu/SHEIN) leaning on "European regulation on quality" is a subtle moat-defense argument for the Dutch e-commerce asset. Capital allocation is unflashy and consistent: organic capex (~€2.7B/yr), bolt-on M&A in adjacent geographies, and a steady buyback-plus-dividend — no leverage adventures, no transformational deal.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Trajectory: STABLE.** This is a well-run, predictable, cash-generative defensive grocer executing a credible plan and returning cash steadily. It is neither strengthening dramatically nor deteriorating.

**The single most important thing to weigh before sizing:** there is no discrete catalyst here. The capital-return program is real but *ongoing and self-renewing* — a €1 billion buyback already roughly a third executed and a 40–50% payout dividend — not a one-time event that forces a re-rating. The thesis is "buy a stable cash machine at four times free cash flow and collect the yield while the discount slowly closes," which depends on patience, not an event.

**Moat:** narrow but real — regional scale economics and density (number-one or number-two in ~80% of its US trade areas; ~50% own-brand penetration in Europe) plus the Dutch e-commerce franchise (bol). It is a scale-cost and local-density moat, not a switching-cost one. The evidence says **stable**, not widening: return on invested capital at 7.3% is steady but not rising, and gross margin is flat at 26.5%.

**Secular threat: manageable.** Grocery is the most defensible corner of retail against e-commerce channel-shift — perishables, local fulfillment, and thin margins protect the incumbent, and Ahold is itself the online share-gainer (online +12.9% in 2025) rather than the victim. This is not a melting terminal-value situation; the cheapness is a fear-print on a low-growth, low-margin business, not a value trap.
