# Forensic Dossier — Advanced Micro Devices (AMD)

*Six consecutive earnings calls, Q1 2025 (May 2025) through Q1 2026 (May 2026), cross-referenced against the screener's hard financials.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story management tells has not pivoted — it has compounded in one direction with unusual consistency. Across all six calls the spine is the same: AMD is taking processor share from Intel in server CPUs while building a credible number-two position in AI accelerators against Nvidia. What changed is the *weight* the company puts on each leg and the *scale* of the ambition.

In the first call of the run (Q1 2025), the framing was still balanced — "strength in our core businesses and expanding data center and AI momentum," with the AI accelerator (Instinct) business described in qualitative terms ("a significant double-digit percentage" growth) and hedged by a fresh $700M China export-control headwind. By the most recent call (Q1 2026), the language is categorical: "Data center is now the primary driver of our revenue and earnings growth," and "These results mark a clear inflection in our growth trajectory and a structural shift in our business." The company moved from "we have momentum" to "the business has structurally changed."

Three drivers were introduced and steadily amplified, none dropped:
- **Server CPU (EPYC).** Quietly the most reliable engine. Management raised the server CPU market growth estimate from "approximately 18% annually" (their own November 2025 Analyst Day) to "greater than 35% annually... over $120 billion by 2030" just two quarters later (Q1 2026) — a striking upward revision of their own recent guidance, justified by agentic-AI driving CPU demand alongside GPUs.
- **Data-center AI (Instinct).** Went from "ramping MI325/MI350" to the headline event: the OpenAI 6-gigawatt deal (announced Q3 2025) and the Meta 6-gigawatt deal (announced Q1 2026), plus a stated path to "tens of billions of dollars in annual Data Center AI revenue in 2027."
- **The full-stack platform.** "Helios" (rack-scale MI400/MI450 + Venice EPYC + Pensando networking) was introduced mid-run and became the central 2026 catalyst.

The one segment quietly walked *down* was gaming/semi-custom: in the Q4 2025 call management guided 2026 semi-custom revenue "down significantly double-digit" as the console cycle ages — an honest negative tucked into an otherwise relentlessly positive narrative.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

The remarkable feature of this name is that the narrative is, if anything, *poorer* than the numbers — management under-described how violent the financial inflection actually was.

| Management claim | The hard numbers | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Record revenue... clear inflection" (Q1 2026) | FY revenue 25.79B (2024) → 34.64B (2025), +34% YoY; latest quarter +38% | Matches and then some — the acceleration is real |
| "Earnings grew more than 40%" / margin expansion "sixth straight quarter" (mid-run) | Net income 1.64B (2024) → 4.33B (2025), +165% YoY EPS; gross margin 49.5% and expanding | Under-stated if anything — net income more than doubled |
| "Free cash flow more than tripled to a record $2.6 billion" (Q1 2026) | TTM free cash flow 8.57B; FCF +180% YoY; FCF margin 19.4% | Confirmed — FCF growth is the single most impressive line |
| "Strong balance sheet, returning cash to shareholders" | Net cash 8.48B (cash 12.35B vs debt 3.87B), recomputed at 2026-03-28 | Confirmed — genuinely net cash, a real balance-sheet cushion |
| Gross margin ~50% corporate | 49.5% gross — but note Nvidia runs 75%+ | True, but the peer gap is the whole bear case (see §6) |

Where the story is *richer* than the trajectory justifies is forward, not backward: the "tens of billions in 2027" AI revenue and "greater than 80%" data-center growth target are claims about a future that has not happened. The historical execution earns them a hearing; the math has not been delivered yet. Note also the multi-year EPS CAGR is only +0.8% over five years — because 2022-2024 were a deep trough (EPS fell from 2.57 in 2021 to 0.53 in 2023). The "inflection" is real, but it is also partly a recovery off a cyclical bottom, which the valuation (price ~127x free cash flow) does not treat as cyclical at all.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Trajectory: Rising, and notably so.** This is one of the cleaner confidence ramps in the set.

- Q1 2025: confident but hedged — "despite the evolving dynamics related to tariffs and the regulatory environment," "uncertain macroeconomic backdrop." Lisa Su leads with execution but acknowledges the China export-control overhang openly.
- Q2 2025: absorbs an $800M inventory write-down (China MI308 export ban) and still reports record EPYC/Ryzen — tone stays offensive, frames the write-down as a one-time regulatory hit, not a demand problem.
- Q3 2025–Q4 2025: tone turns emphatic. "Outstanding quarter with record revenue and profitability." The OpenAI deal is announced with unusual structural detail (warrants tied to share-price milestones).
- Q1 2026: the most assertive of the run — "structural shift," and the willingness to publicly *raise its own Analyst-Day TAM number* two quarters later signals real internal conviction, not boilerplate.

The language is consistently offensive (growth, investment, capacity expansion) rather than defensive (cost, efficiency, macro caution). The one defensive thread — OpEx discipline — is repeatedly probed by analysts and management consistently frames rising operating expense as demand-driven, not margin-eroding. Specificity on guidance *increased* over the run (precise Q2 server-CPU "more than 70% year-over-year," dated H2 2026 Helios ramp).

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Reliability: High.** The screener shows a beat in 6 of the last 7 quarters (86%), and the transcript corroborates a pattern of guiding and then beating: Q1 2026 management notes "we did beat on the revenue side versus our guidance," and the Q2 2026 guide of ~$11.2B (≈46% YoY) was raised from the prior trajectory. There is mild evidence of *sandbagging the unknowable* — China MI308 revenue was deliberately excluded from the Q4 guide ("we did not include any MI308 revenue") so any license approval is upside, not a miss risk.

The one place to watch: operating expense has "continued to blow past the targets" (analyst Stacy Rasgon's words, Q1 2026), and management's answer is essentially "revenue blew past too, so the spend is justified." That is true while revenue is accelerating; it becomes a credibility problem the moment revenue decelerates and the cost base does not. So far they have delivered, so reliability stays High — but the OpEx forecasting miss is a real, repeated tell.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Analysts circled the same three concerns every quarter, and management's answers were direct on two and softer on one:
- **Instinct gross margin.** Repeatedly pressed (C.J. Muse, Ross Seymore) on whether AI-GPU margins can reach corporate average. Management's answer is consistently a deflection-with-promise: "at this stage we really focus on driving the top-line revenue growth... over time... we'll have a lot of opportunities to improve gross margin." This is the single most-deflected topic — AI accelerators are being sold at below-corporate margins to win the platform war, and management will not quantify when that reverses.
- **China MI308.** Pressed every quarter. Management has been honest that it is "a pretty dynamic situation," took the $800M write-down cleanly, and refuses to bank revenue it cannot see.
- **OpEx.** As above — analysts are visibly skeptical that operating-expense growth is forecastable; management leans on the revenue beat to justify it.

No topic that management deflected has yet turned into bad news — the deflections are about *timing and margin shape*, not about whether the business is working.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🟢 Free cash flow tripled year-over-year to a record and FCF margin reached ~19% — cash generation, not just accounting earnings.
🟢 Genuine net-cash balance sheet (8.48B net cash) — no refinancing risk, real optionality for buybacks/M&A.
🟢 33+ consecutive quarters of server-CPU share gains against Intel — a structural, multi-year share-take grounded in a real product (Turin/EPYC) and TCO advantage.
🟢 Two announced 6-gigawatt customer commitments (OpenAI, Meta) giving multi-year demand visibility — rare for a second-source supplier.
🟢 Management raised its own TAM estimate two quarters after Analyst Day — internal conviction, not scripted optimism.

🔴 Insider behavior is one-sided: zero open-market buys against 72 open-market sells (464,529 shares) — the people with the most information are net sellers, not buyers, at this price.
🔴 Valuation is the dominant red flag: price ~127x free cash flow, every intrinsic-value model (discounted cash flow, earnings power, Graham, owner-earnings) shows a margin of safety of -100% — i.e. they all say the stock is worth a fraction of the price. Buffett-style fair value 90.84 vs price 521.58, an 83% overvaluation on that lens. The stock is ~93% above its 200-day average and at the top of its 52-week range.
🔴 The analyst consensus price target (468.57) is *below* the current price — Wall Street's own targets imply roughly -10% downside, a rare situation where the sell-side has stopped chasing.
🔴 Instinct gross margin is structurally below corporate and management will not say when it converges — the AI revenue is real but lower-quality than Nvidia's.
🔴 The five-year EPS CAGR is just +0.8% — the "growth" is partly a recovery off a 2022-2023 trough, which a 127x multiple does not price as cyclical.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

- **The OpenAI warrant structure** (Q3 2025) is the most telling subtlety: AMD granted OpenAI warrants that vest as AMD's share price rises. Management framed it as "aligned incentives," but it is also a tacit admission that AMD had to *pay for* the marquee customer with equity dilution — a structure a clear market leader would not need. Lisa Su called it "unique," and signaled more such creative equity-for-demand deals could follow.
- **"Optimizing dollars more so than percentages"** — management's standing answer on GPU margin. Plain translation: they are prioritizing absolute gross-profit dollars and market share over margin percentage, deliberately running AI accelerators below corporate margin to entrench the platform. Defensible strategically; it caps the quality of the earnings the market is capitalizing at 127x.
- **SG&A outpacing R&D** (Q1 2026, flagged by Blayne Curtis) — in a supposedly supply-constrained, sold-out market you would not expect to need a heavy sales push. Management says it is building a go-to-market machine for enterprise/commercial and that R&D will outgrow SG&A going forward. Worth watching: a sold-out product that still needs rising selling expense is a small dissonance in the bull narrative.
- **GPU useful-life extension** (Q3 2025, Antoine Chkaiban) — management confirmed "early indications" hyperscalers are sweating older GPUs longer. Bullish for inference demand on MI300; mildly bearish for the upgrade-cycle cadence the whole sector is priced on.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Classification: STRENGTHENING.** This is a genuinely improving franchise — accelerating revenue, more-than-doubling net income, tripling free cash flow, a net-cash balance sheet, and a multi-year demand book from the two largest AI buyers in the world. Management's credibility is high and corroborated by the financials. There is nothing wrong with the *business*.

**The single most important thing the director must weigh before sizing:** this is a quality-and-execution story trading at a price that has already capitalized the execution — and then some. The argument is entirely about valuation and timing, not about whether AMD is winning. Every intrinsic model says -100% margin of safety; the sell-side's own price target sits below the stock; insiders are selling, not buying; and the load-bearing 2027 "tens of billions" AI number is a forward claim, not a delivered result. The risk is not that AMD fails — it is that you pay 127x free cash flow for a number-two AI supplier with structurally lower margins than the number one, and the multiple compresses even if the company executes.

**Economic moat: NARROW.** The moat is real but second-tier. The durable piece is the x86 server-CPU franchise — switching costs (software/socket compatibility) plus a scale-cost advantage from years of TSMC-node leadership over Intel, evidenced by 33 straight quarters of share gains. The AI-accelerator business has a *narrowing-but-not-yet-wide* moat resting on the ROCm software stack, which is improving but remains behind Nvidia's CUDA ecosystem; AMD competes there on memory bandwidth, total-cost-of-ownership, and customers' desire for a second source — not on entrenched lock-in.

**Moat trend: WIDENING.** Return on invested capital (9.5%) and gross margin (49.5%) are both rising, not falling; the ROCm ecosystem, the OpenAI/Meta design-ins, and the EPYC share gains are all moat-deepening. This is the opposite of an eroding moat — it is a narrow moat getting wider.

**Secular threat: none (AMD is a beneficiary, not a victim).** The dominant secular force in this name — the generative-AI capital-expenditure wave — is a tailwind for AMD, not a terminal-value threat. The relevant risk is *cyclical and valuation-driven* (an AI-capex digestion air-pocket, a multiple de-rate), not structural impairment of the earnings base. There is no secular-decline theme that impairs AMD's terminal value; the cheapness question does not even arise because the stock is expensive, not cheap.
