# Forensic Dossier — Alliance Resource Partners (ARLP)

*Coal mining and royalty master limited partnership (MLP). Five consecutive quarterly calls reviewed, from the first quarter of 2025 through the first quarter of 2026, cross-checked against the screener's hard financials.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story management tells has tilted, quarter by quarter, from "weather a soft coal market" to "ride a once-in-a-decade demand revival for coal-fired power." In early 2025 the tone was defensive and cost-focused: higher-priced contracts from the 2022 energy crisis were rolling off, volumes were soft, and the message was that "a material improvement in full year cost" would "roughly offset lower realized pricing" (first quarter 2025). By mid-2025 the language turned offensive. Chief Executive Joe Craft called the second-quarter-2025 setup "the most encouraging outlook we've seen for the domestic market since the beginning of 2023," and the order book started filling fast — the 2026 committed-and-priced position jumped from 61% (first quarter) to 80% (second quarter) to 93%-plus (fourth quarter). The new growth engine introduced over these calls is electricity demand from data centers and the artificial-intelligence build-out, plus a more coal-friendly regulatory posture (the administration's two-year relief on the federal mercury rule, and Craft's repeated claim that "all six of the EPA rules" would be revised). A second, quieter growth leg is the oil-and-gas royalty business, which set record volumes through 2025 and into 2026 and now carries an outsized share of profit.

Two threads were quietly dropped or downgraded. First, the "energy-transition" investments Craft once touted (the electric-motor venture Infinitum, the EV-charging name Francis Energy, a battery-materials stake) were narrowed — Craft said in early 2025 "we've narrowed our focus," and one battery investment was written down by $25 million in the second quarter of 2025. Second, the Appalachian Mettiki mine went from a routine operation to a closure: the company issued layoff notices in January 2026 and booked a $37.8 million impairment in the first quarter of 2026 after its single key customer would not commit to buy.

The cleanest divergence: management's market narrative is the most bullish it has been in years exactly as the income statement posts its weakest print of the cycle (Q1 2026 net income of $9.1 million, or $0.07 per unit, versus $74 million a year earlier). Some of that drop is non-cash (the Mettiki impairment and a Bitcoin mark-down), but coverage falling to 1.0x is cash-real.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

Trajectory: **Rising on demand, hedged on execution.** First quarter 2025 was measured ("generally in line with our expectations"). Confidence built through 2025 — "the most encouraging outlook... since 2023" (Q2 2025), and by Q3 2025 Craft asserted utilities "need every coal plant open to meet the demand for data centers." The conviction on the demand backdrop is genuine and specific (contracted-tonnage figures, multi-year contracts pricing above the top of guidance). But the operational language stayed defensive and granular — longwall moves, geology at Tunnel Ridge, the Mettiki shutdown, weather-delayed river shipments in Q1 2026. So the trajectory is two-track: rising confidence about coal's place in the power stack, paired with steady, almost apologetic explanations for why this quarter's volumes or costs slipped.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

Reliability: **Medium.** Management hit its volume and cost guidance through 2025 and tightened ranges credibly; the contracting cadence they promised (filling the 2026 book) materialized quarter by quarter, which is a real mark in their favor. Against that, the screener shows an earnings-beat rate of only 3 of 7 quarters (43%) and a three-year EPS decline of about 18% a year — they guide operations well but have not protected the bottom line through the price down-cycle. The Mettiki closure also followed quarters of "challenging conditions" commentary that, in hindsight, was an under-flagged structural problem rather than a passing one.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Analysts pressed three areas repeatedly. First, capital return — an investor asked directly about buybacks and a distribution increase (Q1 2026); Craft declined, saying coverage must first return to 1.2x-1.4x. Second, the Bitcoin holdings (now ~618 coins) drew repeated "why hold this?" questions; the answer is a conviction bet on the price, not an operating rationale, and it injects earnings volatility. Third, the durability of the demand story — analysts probed whether higher pricing is a new normal; management says yes but concedes it is "totally dependent on energy demand and... what gas prices do." The topic that turned into bad news was Appalachian operations (Tunnel Ridge geology, then the Mettiki customer default and closure) — flagged as transient for several quarters before becoming a write-down.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 Distribution coverage fell to 1.0x in Q1 2026, after the payout was already cut from $0.70 to $0.60; further pressure is plausible.
🔴 The Mettiki closure and $37.8 million impairment confirm single-customer concentration risk in Appalachia.
🔴 Insider conviction is negative on the open market: zero open-market buys versus four sells (100,000 units) — the opposite of a value-buyer signal.
🔴 Related-party governance: in the June 2026 AllDale royalty acquisition, Craft-affiliated parties bought $100 million of the same interests alongside ARLP's $206 million — recurring conflicts in an insider-controlled MLP.
🔴 Bitcoin on the balance sheet adds non-operating earnings swings (a $11.6 million mark-down hit Q1 2026).
🟢 The oil-and-gas royalty leg is genuinely growing — record volumes and the AllDale deal materially scale the highest-margin, lowest-capital business.
🟢 The 2026 order book is over 95% committed and priced, giving real near-term revenue visibility.
🟢 Costs fell meaningfully through 2025 (segment cost to ~$40/ton in Q4 2025).
🟢 Leverage is modest for the sector (net debt about 0.7x EBITDA; solvency score 2.9, the best among coal peers).

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The most telling shift is where capital is flowing: away from the energy-transition venture bets Craft once championed and into oil-and-gas mineral royalties (the AllDale deal) plus opportunistic ideas like supplying coal to utility-owned plants and "data center infrastructure." Read plainly, management is hedging the coal franchise's terminal value by buying a cash-royalty stream that does not depend on mining its own coal. A second signal is the careful framing around capital return — the refusal to raise the distribution or buy back units until coverage recovers tells you management privately rates the cash-flow outlook as tighter than the demand rhetoric suggests. Third, the persistent Bitcoin advocacy (citing the CLARITY Act and a Fed nominee's comments) is a leadership idiosyncrasy that, while small, signals a board comfortable with non-core speculation.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

Classification: **PIVOTING.** This is a well-run, low-leverage coal MLP that is quietly redirecting capital toward a royalty stream because management itself appears to doubt that thermal coal's terminal value will hold. The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: the bull case rests almost entirely on a third-party, non-binding demand driver — that data-center and artificial-intelligence load will halt or reverse coal-plant retirements. That is a real tailwind today, but it is not contracted to ARLP, it is shared across the entire coal cohort (sector beta, not single-name edge), and it sits on top of a base that the broad energy outlook (IEA, World Bank, IEEFA) still expects to shrink over the long run. Pay for the cash and the royalty optionality, not for a coal-demand renaissance.

Economic moat: a **cost-and-logistics scale advantage** in the Illinois Basin (low-cost mines, on-river transport, long-term utility relationships) — a real but narrow moat, and the evidence says it is eroding: return on capital is still healthy (~18%) but earnings power has fallen hard (EPS from $4.81 to $1.91 trailing), gross margin is contracting, and the company is closing mines. The secular threat is the energy transition — electrification and renewables structurally cap long-run thermal-coal demand. AI-power demand softens the slope but does not change the direction. This is a cyclically cheap stock on a structurally shrinking base: closer to a value trap than a fear-print mispricing, which caps how much the cheapness should be credited.
