# Forensic Dossier — Banca Monte dei Paschi di Siena (BMPS.MI)

*Two consecutive earnings calls reviewed: the third-quarter and nine-month 2025 results (November 7, 2025) and the fourth-quarter and full-year 2025 preliminary results (February 10, 2026). The story across both is dominated by one event: Monte Paschi — the 553-year-old Italian bank that the state rescued and recapitalised — turning predator and swallowing Mediobanca, a far more prestigious investment-and-private bank.*

## 1. Narrative Arc

The narrative across these two calls is a single, unbroken victory lap, but the subject of the victory shifts.

In **November 2025**, the headline was completion: chief executive Luigi Lovaglio opened by calling the close of the Mediobanca tender offer "a landmark moment," noting that 86.3% of Mediobanca holders tendered their shares — "a clear endorsement of the industrial strength and the long-term value of this combination." The stand-alone bank was presented as healthy (nine-month net profit about €1.37 billion, up 17.5% excluding tax effects), but the real message was that the deal was done and the integration machine — "more than 20 ongoing work streams" — was running.

By **February 2026**, the story has matured from "we did the deal" to "look what we now are." Lovaglio framed the day as "a decisive turning point," presenting for the first time consolidated results that include Mediobanca: a combined group net profit of €3.0 billion before purchase-accounting effects, a stand-alone Monte Paschi profit of €2.75 billion (up 17.7%), and a dividend of €0.86 per share — "one of the highest dividend yields in European banking currently standing at 10%." Management repeatedly deferred the substance — synergies, integration-cost phasing, the target legal structure — to the Capital Markets Day on February 27, 2026.

The growth drivers introduced and emphasised throughout are consistent: fee income (especially wealth management), a strong commercial network, and the promise of cross-selling Mediobanca's high-end private banking, corporate-and-investment banking, and Compass consumer-finance platform. The quietly recurring sweetener is **excess capital** — a core Tier 1 ratio of roughly 16–17%, plus about €500 million a year of deferred-tax-asset write-ups for six years — repeatedly dangled as fuel for either expansion or shareholder returns.

## 2. Claims vs Financials (forensic)

The defining tension in this name is that the reported profitability is spectacular and almost entirely unrepeatable in its current form. Management's operating claims are largely true; the headline earnings power is an accounting artifact.

| Management claim | What the financials show | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Net profit at about €3 billion" combined group, €2.75bn stand-alone (Feb 2026) | 2025 net income 3.04bn on the scan; but management itself disclosed a **€961 million positive tax contribution** from writing up off-balance-sheet deferred tax assets (DTAs) | Richer than reality — roughly a third of the profit is a one-time tax write-up, not operating earnings |
| "Net operating profit … up by 6.4% year-on-year" (stand-alone, Feb 2026) | Gross operating profit up only ~1% for the full year; net interest income falling (down 1.3% quarter-on-quarter in Q3) | Believable but thin — the operating engine is roughly flat, propped by fees offsetting a shrinking rate tailwind |
| "Resilient revenues … sustained by fees" | Reported revenue is **down 27.8% year-on-year** on the scan, with a 3-year compound growth of just 6.6% | The "resilience" is in fee lines; the top line is shrinking, and the optics of a –28% revenue print sit awkwardly under the upbeat tone |
| "Best-in-class capital buffer," core Tier 1 ~16.2–16.9% | Confirmed strong; net NPE ratio down to 1.8%, cost of risk 40 basis points | Matches the numbers — capital and asset quality are genuine strengths |
| Net margin 64.1%, gross margin 91.6% | These are flattered by reserve releases, the falling cost of risk, and the tax write-up | A high-but-distorted figure — durable operating profitability is far lower than the headline margin implies |

Two hard contradictions deserve emphasis. First, the screener's **trailing-twelve-month free cash flow is negative €2.68 billion** and the recomputed balance sheet (March 31, 2026) shows **net debt of €12.89 billion** (total debt €45.3bn against €32.4bn cash) — for a bank these are largely funding-structure artifacts rather than distress signals, but they flatly contradict any "net cash" framing. Second, **Altman-Z of –0.44 and a Piotroski score of 4 out of 9** signal that the conventional solvency and quality screens still rate this balance sheet as fragile, which is consistent with a bank only a few years out of a state recapitalisation.

## 3. Tone & Confidence Trajectory

**Rising and assertive throughout — arguably to a fault.** Lovaglio's language is consistently offensive (growth, expansion, synergies, "new heights") rather than defensive. In November he said the combination is "accretive … financially, strategically and commercially." By February he was openly challenging the market: pressed by Bank of America's analyst on why the stock trades around €8 ex-dividend, he insisted the valuation is "absolutely underestimated" and that "the market will recognise the work that we are doing." This is a chief executive talking his own book hard. The confidence is corroborated by real operating delivery (fees up, costs flat, asset quality improving), so it does not read as empty bravado — but the repeated insistence that the shares are too cheap is a tell that the market does not yet share his conviction.

## 4. Guidance Credibility

**Medium-to-high.** The reliable pattern is that management raised guidance and beat it: in November they guided full-year pre-tax profit to "well exceed €1.6 billion," and by February the stand-alone pre-tax figure came in at €1.7 billion, "well above guidance." The screener corroborates an earnings-beat rate of 4 out of 5 quarters (80%). The credibility caveat is timing and substance: on the two most investor-relevant questions — the split of integration charges and the exact synergy phasing — management explicitly refused to answer ("this is a second-level details question … let us answer on the 27th of February"). Promising and then delivering on operating results is one thing; the load-bearing synergy and integration math was deferred to the Capital Markets Day, which has since occurred.

## 5. Analyst Pressure Points

Analysts circled three topics management would not fully address on these calls. **Integration charges** — Equita and KBW both pushed for the size and the year-by-year phasing and the Mediobanca-versus-MPS split; management gave only a gross figure (~€500–600 million, mostly 2026) and deferred the rest. **Excess capital and how it gets returned** — Intermonte asked directly whether the surplus comes back as dividends or buybacks; Lovaglio kept it deliberately open ("if it's through buyback or … extraordinary dividend, whatever, is something that we evaluate time to time"). **The Generali stake** — Mediobanca owns roughly 13% of insurer Generali, and analysts repeatedly probed both the regulatory capital treatment (the "Danish compromise," worth a potential +50 basis points) and what Monte Paschi intends to do with the holding; management stayed vague ("we are focused on Mediobanca"). One analyst (BNP Paribas Exane) even floated whether MPS might buy back its own shares via an equity derivative as rival BPER had done; Lovaglio declined.

## 6. Red Flags / Green Flags

🔴 The headline 2025 profit is inflated by a **€961 million deferred-tax-asset write-up** — a one-time, non-cash accounting event, not earnings power. Strip it out and the group is a roughly €2 billion-profit bank, not a €3 billion one.
🔴 **A –28% revenue print under an upbeat "resilient revenues" narrative** — the fee story is real, but the top line is shrinking and net interest income is rolling over as rates fall.
🔴 **The valuation case rests on management repeatedly telling the market it is wrong.** When the bull thesis is largely "the chief executive thinks the stock is too cheap," the asymmetry is weaker than it looks.
🔴 **Integration and synergy specifics were withheld** on both calls, leaving the central value-creation claim unverifiable until the Capital Markets Day.
🟢 **Genuine commercial momentum**: wealth-management gross inflows of about €17 billion for 2025 (up 17%), retail mortgages of €6 billion (more than doubling), and a 46% cost-to-income ratio that held flat through a labour-contract renewal.
🟢 **Improving asset quality and a fortress capital position**: cost of risk down to 40 basis points, net non-performing exposures at 1.8%, core Tier 1 around 16%.
🟢 **A credible, high cash return**: a €0.86 dividend (about €2.6 billion total, ~10% yield) backed by real capital generation.

## 7. Hidden Signals

The most telling shift is the move from defence to ambition. A bank that the Italian state had to rescue is now talking about **expanding Compass consumer finance and private banking abroad** — "opening a new market and new potential revenue generation." That is the language of an acquirer that believes it has graduated. A second signal is the careful framing of the deferred-tax write-up as recurring "support to the core Tier 1 of around €500 million per year for the next six years" — management is coaching analysts to treat a tax benefit as a capital annuity. Third, the deal is described as a **"single point of entry"** for resolution and funding (MREL) purposes, which is the structural justification for keeping Mediobanca as a separate legal entity focused on corporate-and-investment and high-end private banking — a hint that "full integration" (and thus full synergies) may be slower and more legally complex than the upbeat tone suggests.

## 8. Capital-Allocation Verdict (for the director)

**Trajectory: PIVOTING.** This is a state-rescued domestic retail bank transforming itself, via a large and politically charged acquisition, into a diversified universal bank with wealth, corporate-and-investment, and consumer-finance arms. The pivot is real and, so far, competently executed.

**The single most important thing to weigh before sizing:** the load-bearing catalyst — the Capital Markets Day and the consolidated business plan — has already happened (February 27, 2026), and the re-rating it could trigger is therefore largely spent rather than ahead of you. What remains is execution of a multi-year integration whose biggest synergy benefits do not arrive until the second half of 2028. You would be buying the *integration grind*, not the *deal pop*, and you would be doing so while a third of the trailing profit is a non-repeatable tax write-up.

**Moat:** a regulatory-license and scale-cost moat, the ordinary franchise of a top-three Italian bank with sticky retail deposits and a dense branch network — but a **NARROW** one. Return on invested capital of 2.4% and return on equity of 12.6% are unremarkable; the franchise is real but not a fortress, and Italian banking is a low-growth, deposit-competitive, politically-exposed market.

**Moat trend:** **STABLE**. The Mediobanca combination genuinely broadens the franchise (a third of revenue now comes from asset gathering and wealth) and improves the business mix, which argues against erosion; but capital-light fee growth has not yet shown up as rising structural returns, so this is steadiness, not widening.

**Secular threat: manageable.** The relevant structural force is fintech and wallet disintermediation of payments and the long, slow digital erosion of branch banking — a real headwind for a branch-heavy Italian retail bank, but a gradual, decade-scale pressure rather than a terminal-value cliff. The wealth-management and private-banking pivot is, if anything, a partial hedge against it. This is a cyclical-and-political risk profile (Italian sovereign spreads, rate cuts compressing net interest income, integration execution) far more than a secular-decline one. The cheapness, such as it is, is a fear-and-complexity discount on a recovering franchise — not a melting base.
