# Forensic Dossier — CarGurus, Inc. (CARG)

*Six consecutive earnings calls reviewed: Q1 2025 (May 2025) through Q1 2026 (May 2026). All financial figures from the screener block and the company's own reported results.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story management tells about CarGurus has changed shape in a meaningful, deliberate way across these six quarters — and the change is the single most important thing in this file.

CarGurus runs the most-visited online car-shopping marketplace in the United States: dealers pay a subscription to list their inventory and reach shoppers, and that core "Marketplace" business throws off very high-margin, recurring revenue. Around it sat a much messier, lower-margin wholesale-transactions business called CarOffer, which let dealers buy and sell cars to each other instantly.

In early 2025 (Q1 2025 call), the headline was "intelligent acceleration" — reigniting growth and expanding margins, with Marketplace revenue up 13% year over year and a still-undecided strategic review of CarOffer hanging over the picture. By the Q2 2025 call (August 2025), management made the decisive move: it announced it would **wind down the CarOffer transactions business entirely**, keeping only the underlying sourcing technology and data. From that point forward the narrative was reframed around two durable assets management repeats almost word-for-word every quarter: "scale and data intelligence." The pitch evolved from "we run a car-listings site" to "we are a marketplace, software, and data company" that sells dealers a growing suite of paid tools (PriceVantage, Shopper Signals, Performance Insights, Next Best Deal Rating) on top of the listings subscription.

Two consistent growth drivers were introduced and genuinely built upon: (1) the international business (Canada and the UK), which accelerated from 20% growth (Q1 2025) to 39% growth (Q1 2026); and (2) a la carte software products, of which PriceVantage (launched October 2025) is the flagship. One driver was quietly buried: CarOffer/wholesale as a transaction engine, now reduced to a data feed.

The arc is a **pivot, executed on purpose** — shedding a low-quality revenue line to refocus a high-margin core. That is the right frame for everything that follows, including the screener's alarming-looking three-year revenue decline.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

The central forensic puzzle here is that the headline trajectory looks ugly while the underlying business management describes sounds strong. Both can be true — and reconciling them is the whole game.

| Management claim | The financial evidence | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Marketplace revenue grew ~13–15% YoY every quarter" | Total reported revenue grew only +5.0% YoY, and the **3-year revenue CAGR is −17.2%** | **Both true.** The −17.2% is the CarOffer wholesale run-off masking a fast-growing core. Revenue *from continuing operations* grew 14% in full-year 2025 and 15% in Q1 2026. The screener's all-in revenue line is the wrong denominator for this business now. |
| "Expanding profitability / strong operating leverage" | Gross margin 89.0% (expanding); net margin 16.6%; FCF margin 30.8%; TTM free cash flow $281M | **Confirmed and impressive.** An 89% gross margin and ~30% free-cash-flow margin are genuinely software-grade economics. |
| "Disciplined capital return through buybacks" | Repurchased ~$350M in 2025, ~$721M since late 2022 (≈29% of shares retired); new $250M program, $75M left after Q1 2026 | **Confirmed.** Real, large, share-count-shrinking buybacks — the clearest pro-shareholder fact in the file. |
| EPS strength | EPS $1.55 latest, +675% YoY; but the +675% is off a 2024 trough ($0.20) distorted by CarOffer write-downs; 3-yr EPS CAGR only +0.9%; latest-quarter EPS −8% YoY | **Mixed — caution.** The +675% is a base-effect mirage. The honest read is the TTM diluted EPS of ~$1.52 and the −8% latest-quarter print. Earnings power is solid but **not** growing at the headline rate. |
| "We're getting more efficient with AI; investing behind momentum" | 2026 guidance: revenue +10–13%, but **adjusted-EBITDA margin guided to compress 1.5–2.5 points** | **Tension.** After years of "expanding margins," management is now guiding margins *down* in 2026 to fund investment. This is the first defensive note in the file. |
| ROIC / returns | ROE 19.6%, ROIC 10.7% | **Adequate, not elite.** A 10.7% return on invested capital is only modestly above the cost of capital — respectable for the core, but it does not scream a wide, fortress moat. |

The story is **richer than the top-line trajectory justifies** (the −17.2% CAGR is a red herring) but **poorer than the +675% EPS headline implies** (that is a trough-base illusion). The truth sits in the middle: a high-margin, cash-generative marketplace growing low-teens, now spending some of its margin to defend and extend its lead.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Overall: Stable-to-slightly-declining, with a clear shift from offense toward investment-justification.**

- **Q1 2025 (offensive):** "the year of transformative innovation"; "reigniting growth, and expanding margins." Pure momentum language.
- **Q2 2025 (offensive but with a hard pivot):** "outstanding financial results," but paired with the sober admission that CarOffer had "structural limitations" and would be wound down — a candid, credibility-building moment.
- **Q3 2025 (confident, building the new framing):** introduces the "scale and data intelligence" flywheel and the claim that the addressable market expands by "roughly an additional $4 billion" of dealer software/data spend.
- **Q4/FY 2025 (proud but pivoting):** "2025 was a pivotal year." Investment intensity ranked explicitly: "number one is in product and tech; number two, international; number three, account management."
- **Q1 2026 (more defensive):** the new third value-creation driver is literally "disciplined in our capital deployment" — a notable insertion of balance-sheet discipline into what had been a pure-growth narrative, alongside guidance for margin compression.

Guidance specificity stayed high on the metrics they choose to give (Marketplace growth ranges, EBITDA ranges) but management repeatedly **declined to give exit rates, retention figures, or international EBITDA drag** ("we don't give actual retention data," "we don't break it out"). That selective opacity is consistent across all six quarters — not a new evasion, but a persistent one.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Reliability: High.** This is the strongest pillar of the file. The screener records a **7-for-7 (100%) earnings-beat rate**, and the qualitative record matches: in Q2, Q3, and Q4 2025 management reported coming in "above the midpoint" of its Marketplace and EBITDA guidance, and Q1 2026 EBITDA of $80.2M printed "above the high end of guidance." The double-digit Marketplace growth promised early in 2025 was delivered every quarter. There is a mild sandbagging pattern (guiding to a range and beating the top), which is benign and investor-friendly. The one thing to watch: management has now guided 2026 margins *lower*, so the "beat" bar for the coming year is a deliberately softer one.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Analysts circled three themes repeatedly, and management's answers grew thinner on the most important one:

1. **The AI/LLM disintermediation threat (the big one).** Asked directly (Q2 2025, Q1 2026) whether ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, and agentic search will route shoppers around the marketplace, management's answer is consistent but unproven: LLM traffic is "a very small percent of our traffic," it's "top of funnel," "not a substitute for a marketplace," and CarGurus is "embracing it" by putting its app *inside* ChatGPT. This is the load-bearing bet of the bear case, and management is asking investors to take the durability of the marketplace on faith.
2. **Margin trajectory / investment intensity.** Pushed every quarter on why margins won't keep expanding; the answer is always "we're investing behind momentum." In Q1 2026 that finally showed up as guided margin *compression* — i.e., the analysts' worry materialized.
3. **The shrinking cash balance and buyback runway.** In Q1 2026, Joe Spak (UBS) flagged that the cash balance was "the lowest since 2020" and that only $75M of buyback authorization remained. Management's answer leaned on an undrawn line of credit and refused to name a minimum-cash floor — a soft spot for a thesis that depends on buybacks as the re-rating engine.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🟢 **Real, sustained share-count reduction** — ~29% of shares retired since late 2022; the clearest value-accretive use of the high free cash flow.
🟢 **Genuinely accelerating international franchise** — Canada/UK from +20% to +39% YoY, taking share from a stumbling incumbent (the UK competitor's "fourth quarter initiatives" pushing dealers away).
🟢 **Software-grade unit economics** — 89% gross margin, ~30% FCF margin, 100% beat rate, Piotroski 7/9, Altman-Z 10.8 (no solvency risk whatsoever; net debt is a trivial $116M against ~$281M annual FCF).
🟢 **Disciplined exit of a bad business** — winding down CarOffer rather than throwing more capital at it.

🔴 **Margins now guided to compress** in 2026 after years of expansion — the first reversal of the operating-leverage story.
🔴 **The AI/agentic-search secular threat is real and unhedgeable by management's own framing** — its entire defense rests on "shoppers still need a trusted destination," which is exactly the kind of workflow-habit moat that generative AI is built to erode.
🔴 **Insider behavior is bearish:** the screener's grant-inflated buy ratio is misleading; the open-market truth is **1 insider buy (~31k shares) versus 34 sells (~198k shares)**. Insiders are net sellers, not buyers.
🔴 **Latest-quarter EPS down 8% YoY and the cash balance at a multi-year low** — the buyback has been funded partly by drawing the balance sheet down, which is not infinitely repeatable.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The most telling shift is **vocabulary**. Across 2025 management systematically re-labeled the company from a "marketplace" to a "marketplace, software, and data company," and quantified a new "$4 billion" software/data total addressable market on top of the existing "$3.5 billion" marketplace one. This is the classic incumbent move when the core market matures: reframe toward an adjacent, larger pool. It may be entirely legitimate (the dealer-data products show real adoption — Next Best Deal Rating at ~20,000 dealers, 60% of paying dealers using the insights suite), but the *timing* — a TAM-expansion narrative arriving exactly as AI threatens the core — deserves a skeptic's eye.

A second signal: the move to report as **"one segment"** (international is no longer broken out, CarOffer is discontinued). Management cited simplicity, but it also reduces the disclosure analysts can use to check the international-drag and segment-margin claims — the "we don't break it out" answers all trace to this.

A third, more positive signal: the capital-allocation language tightened into an explicit three-bucket framework (reinvest / M&A powder / buybacks) and a stated willingness to "get aggressive" on buybacks only "at prices that we think are more compelling." That is a value-disciplined operator's language, not an empire-builder's.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Trajectory classification: PIVOTING.** CarGurus is a high-quality, cash-rich marketplace deliberately reshaping itself — out of low-margin wholesale, into dealer software/data and international share gains, while shrinking its share count aggressively. The fundamentals are sound (no solvency risk, software-grade margins, a reliable beat record).

**The single most important thing the director must weigh before sizing:** this is not a special situation with a hard catalyst — it is a *quality-versus-secular-threat* judgment. The buyback is real but routine and nearly exhausted ($75M left of $250M, no new program announced); there is no activist, no asset sale, no dated event to force a re-rating. So the question is purely: does the marketplace's scale-and-data moat survive the migration of car-shopping discovery into generative-AI environments? Management's entire defense is that AI traffic is "top of funnel" and "not a substitute." If that holds, the stock is a reasonably-priced compounder at ~11x free cash flow. If it doesn't, the optical cheapness is a value trap on a slowly-melting base.

**Moat assessment:** a **narrow** network-effect / scale moat (two-sided liquidity between shoppers and dealers, reinforced by proprietary first-party demand data). It is genuine but not fortress-grade — the 10.7% ROIC is only modestly above the cost of capital, and gross margin, while very high, is not the thing under threat; *traffic acquisition* is. The moat is best read as **stable today but facing a credible structural erosion vector**, not actively widening.

**Secular threat:** **material** (not yet terminal). Generative-AI / agentic-search displacement of the top-of-funnel discovery that feeds the marketplace is the precise risk; management is responding intelligently (app inside ChatGPT, its own Discover tool) but the outcome is genuinely uncertain. The cheapness is therefore a **partial fear-print on a real but bounded secular worry** — closer to fairly-priced-for-the-risk than to either a clean mispricing or an obvious trap.
