# Forensic Dossier — Greif, Inc. (GEF)

*An industrial-packaging maker (steel and plastic drums, intermediate bulk containers, paperboard tubes and cores, caps and closures) that spent fiscal 2024–2026 selling off its commodity businesses, paying down debt, and reshaping itself into a higher-margin, lower-cyclicality company.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

Across the six earnings calls in this record — from the first quarter of fiscal 2025 (February 2025) through the first quarter of fiscal 2026 (January 2026) — the Greif story changed shape in a clean, deliberate way. It moved from "we can grind out results in a bad industrial economy" to "we have surgically reshaped the portfolio, and now we are a structurally better business waiting for volumes to return."

The story has three chapters. **Chapter one (early 2025): the cost-and-commitments framing.** Management opened by reaffirming the targets it had laid out at its December 2024 Investor Day — $1 billion of adjusted EBITDA (a profit measure that strips out interest, taxes, and depreciation) and $500 million of free cash flow by fiscal 2027 — resting on three "levers": known pricing items, an eventual industrial-volume recovery, and a $100 million cost-cutting program. **Chapter two (through 2025): the portfolio surgery.** What began as a timberland sale ("Soterra," announced February 2025) snowballed into the centerpiece of the entire thesis: the August 31, 2025 sale of the containerboard business to Packaging Corporation of America for roughly $1.8 billion, then the October 1, 2025 close of the Soterra sale for $462 million. Net proceeds of about $1.75 billion went straight to debt. **Chapter three (late 2025 into 2026): the "transformed company" framing.** By the January 2026 call the language had shifted entirely — leverage below 1x, a completed $150 million buyback, a fresh $300 million repurchase authorization, and a cost program raised twice (from $100 million to $120 million of run-rate savings by 2027). The closing line — "Greif is a company you can invest in with confidence" — captures the new posture.

A growth driver that was quietly de-emphasized: the original $1 billion EBITDA bridge for 2027. Management stopped reiterating it explicitly (an analyst flagged this directly: "I didn't see the $1 billion reiterated"), retreating to a "we still think there's a path" hedge that leans heavily on an industrial recovery that has not arrived.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

| Management claim | What the financials show | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Our strategy is working… margins expanding" | Adjusted EBITDA margin rose from ~12.3% (Q1 FY26) toward 15.4% (Q2 FY25); gross margin steady ~22% | **Matches.** Margin progress is real and cost-driven. |
| "$1 billion EBITDA / $500M FCF by 2027" | FY2026 low-end guide is now $610M EBITDA (cut from $630M in June 2026), $315M FCF — far below the 2027 target with one year to bridge | **Diverges sharply.** The headline target requires a volume recovery that keeps not arriving. |
| "Leverage historically low, below 1x" | Confirmed: net debt fell ~$1.77B; leverage ratio 3.3x → 1.1x after the two sales | **Matches and is the strongest, most verifiable claim in the file.** |
| "Strong free cash flow generation" | Reported FY2025 ~50% conversion; but the screener's trailing-twelve-month free cash flow is **negative ~$120M** and the latest-quarter EPS fell 73% year-over-year | **Mixed / poorer than the narrative.** "Adjusted" cash flow flatters a noisier GAAP reality distorted by divestiture timing and working capital. |
| EPS "+223% year-over-year" (fiscal 2025: $15.0) | Driven largely by the **$216M one-time gain on the Soterra sale** — not recurring earnings power | **Story is richer than the trajectory.** The eye-catching EPS jump is a sale gain, not operating leverage. |
| Revenue trajectory | Revenue fell from $6.35B (2022) to $4.29B (2025) — a 3-year decline of roughly 12% per year | **The shrink is by design (divestitures) but it is still a shrinking top line.** |

The single most important forensic point: **the dramatic 2025 earnings and balance-sheet improvement is the product of selling businesses, not of the core getting bigger.** The continuing business is smaller, still volume-challenged, and just had its 2026 profit guide cut.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Trajectory: Stable-to-Rising on execution confidence, but persistently Defensive on demand.** Management's confidence in what it controls (cost, capital allocation, the balance sheet) clearly rose — "each day, our conviction grows" (Q2 FY25), "we are not waiting for the macroeconomic environment to improve" (Q3 FY25). But the demand language never turned offensive: "customer sentiment remains cautious" (Q3 FY25), "still muted industrial backdrop" (Q1 FY26), North America "the most bearish" throughout. The tone is that of a management team executing well inside a recession it cannot end — proud of the self-help, careful never to call the volume bottom.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Reliability: Medium-to-High on the controllables, Low on the long-range target.** Greif has a genuine record of setting a conservative low-end guide and then nudging it up — an analyst praised exactly this ("done a really good job of kind of hitting the low-end guidance and even moving it up"). The cost program was raised twice, ahead of plan. The screener shows a 67% beat rate (four of six quarters). That is credible, sandbag-flavored guidance.

The credibility gap sits with the 2027 $1 billion EBITDA aspiration. With the FY2026 low-end now $610M and the target $1B, the bridge depends on a $140M+ volume snap-back that has been "coming" for three years. Management itself softened from "confident in meeting or exceeding" to "we still think there's a path." And crucially — **after this transcript record closed, the June 2026 (Q2 FY26) call CUT the 2026 low-end guide from $630M to $610M**, blaming Middle East conflict disruption and downgrading volume assumptions to down mid-single digits. That is a real, recent miss against the very reaffirmation made in January 2026.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Analysts circled three things repeatedly. **Volume timing** — every call, some version of "when does volume come back?", consistently deflected to "look at the same path as the past two years." **The $1 billion target** — pushed on whether it was still intact; management retreated to a path-not-promise answer. **Capital allocation / M&A** — analysts probed whether the freshly cleaned balance sheet means a big deal; management repeatedly said organic growth first, only disciplined tuck-ins ("we ran away from the church… the bride was pretty ugly"). The recurring unaddressed worry: the core's organic volume has no visible catalyst, so the entire forward case rests on a macro turn management openly cannot forecast.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🟢 Balance-sheet transformation is real and verified — 3.3x to 1.1x leverage, ~$1.77B debt repaid.
🟢 Cost program is ahead of schedule and twice-raised ($100M → $120M run-rate by 2027).
🟢 Disciplined capital allocation — buybacks at "value" prices, no empire-building M&A, dividend intent.
🟢 Real margin expansion through a demand trough (a genuine operating-quality signal).
🔴 The flagship 2027 $1B EBITDA target is quietly slipping and was de-emphasized in prose.
🔴 The headline +223% EPS is a one-time sale gain, not earnings power — easy to misread as a growth surge.
🔴 Trailing free cash flow is negative; "adjusted" figures flatter a messy GAAP picture.
🔴 Open-market insider activity is net selling (11 buys / 22k shares vs 16 sells / 72k shares) — not the conviction a deep-value setup wants.
🔴 The stock sits at ~89% of its 52-week range with an analyst consensus target essentially equal to the price — the re-rating the thesis needed has already happened.
🔴 Post-record (June 2026): 2026 guidance CUT — the volume recovery moved further away, not closer.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The terminology migrated from regional reporting to "solutions" segments, then to renaming Integrated Solutions as "Innovative Closure Solutions" — a deliberate repositioning toward the higher-margin caps-and-closures story (30%+ gross margin) and away from commodity drums. Management also began foregrounding new technology (the proprietary "SIOC"/IonKraft barrier coating, "the only ones that have that") — an attempt to seed an organic-growth narrative to replace the now-spent divestiture story. A subtle governance tell: an analyst asked about simplifying the Class A / Class B dual-share structure; management "continues to dialogue" but signaled nothing near-term — a latent, unforced potential catalyst left on the table.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Classification: PIVOTING (successfully executed) — now transitioning to STABLE.** Greif did exactly what it said: it sold the commodity businesses, crushed its leverage, and is returning cash. This is a well-run, shareholder-friendly management team.

The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: **the value-creating events that made this story compelling have already happened and are already in the price.** Containerboard sold, Soterra sold, debt repaid, first buyback done — all fired. What remains is a smaller, cleaner, still-volume-starved industrial cyclical trading at ~89% of its range with no upside to the analyst target and a just-cut 2026 guide. The optical method-based "margin of safety" is inflated by a one-time sale gain in the earnings figure; on continuing operations the business is fairly-to-fully valued.

**Moat:** Greif has a **narrow** moat — regional scale, local-to-local supply, switching costs in spec'd industrial containers, and #1/#2 positions in fiber drums and uncoated recycled board. But returns are modest and not rising: return on invested capital is ~7.5%, gross margin is steady-not-expanding at ~22%, and the top line is shrinking. That is a narrow, **stable** moat, not a widening one. **Secular threat: manageable.** Industrial packaging faces cyclical demand and sustainability pressure, but no terminal structural force is melting the base — drums and IBCs remain essential to chemicals, ag, and food. The cheapness here is therefore neither a deep mispricing nor a value trap; it is a fairly-priced, well-run cyclical whose special-situation catalysts have already paid out.
