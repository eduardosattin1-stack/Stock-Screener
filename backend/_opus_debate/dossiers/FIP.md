# FTAI Infrastructure Inc. (FIP) — Forensic Dossier (Event-Driven Special Situation)

*Interrogator pass, Claude Opus 4.8. Judged as an event-driven special situation: the question is whether the load-bearing catalyst is real, dated, and binding, and whether a verified downside floor backstops the price — not whether this is a good business.*

## The event, in one paragraph

FTAI Infrastructure is a holding company that owns short-line freight railroads, two East Coast bulk/liquids terminals (Jefferson in Texas and the still-being-built Repauno in New Jersey), and — until now — a 485-megawatt gas power plant on the Ohio River called Long Ridge. On April 30, 2026 the company signed a binding agreement to sell Long Ridge to a subsidiary of MARA Holdings for roughly $1.52 billion. The deal does two things at once: it lifts about $1.16 billion of debt that sat against the power plant straight off the books, and it hands FIP roughly $300 million of cash to pay down debt at the parent level. The stated plan is to use this to finish a multi-year pivot into a pure-play short-line railroad. The catalyst we are trading is the **closing of that sale**, which management guides to the third quarter of 2026, with a contractual backstop ("outside date") of November 30, 2026 before either side can walk. This is a real, dated, signed corporate transaction — not a rumor, not an activist wish, and not a "forced" divestiture. The Catalyst-Watch label of "forced seller" is, on the verified facts, a mischaracterization: FIP is a willing seller deleveraging by choice.

## 1. The catalyst — is it real, dated, and binding?

**Real and binding: yes.** There is a signed purchase agreement (announced April 30, 2026; disclosed in the Q1 2026 8-K and 10-Q) to sell Long Ridge to a MARA Holdings subsidiary for approximately $1.52 billion. The consideration mechanics are concrete: about $1.16 billion of Long Ridge-level debt comes off the balance sheet at close, and net cash of roughly $300 million flows to FIP to repay parent debt.

**Dated: yes, with a firm window.** Management guides the close to the third quarter of 2026. The agreement carries an outside date of November 30, 2026, with a possible extension only for outstanding regulatory approvals. So the hard window is "close by Q3, contractual backstop end-November." The Catalyst-Watch date of September 30, 2026 sits squarely inside the guided window and is a reasonable midpoint — but note the real contractual deadline is the November 30 outside date, two months later, which is the date that actually binds.

**The one genuine open item is regulatory approval.** The deal is "subject to customary closing conditions and regulatory consents." For a 485-megawatt merchant power plant plus working interests in natural-gas wells being sold to a bitcoin-mining/data-center operator (MARA), the realistic approvals are antitrust clearance (Hart-Scott-Rodino) and, more pointedly, Federal Energy Regulatory Commission sign-off on the change of control of a power-generation asset (a Federal Power Act Section 203 filing). FERC 203 approvals for a straightforward asset transfer to a non-utility buyer are routine and typically clear within the guided window, but they are the gating step and the most likely source of any slip. There is no financing contingency disclosed on the buyer side that I can verify, which removes one common deal-break risk; MARA is the counterparty most exposed to financing/crypto-cycle stress, and that is the residual counterparty risk to watch.

## 2. The downside floor — what actually backstops the price if the event fails?

This is the heart of the special-situation question, and here the setup is unusually clean because **the floor does not depend on the catalyst at all.** Even if the Long Ridge sale broke tomorrow, FIP would still own three operating, cash-generating infrastructure businesses:

- **Railroad (Transtar + Wheeling & Lake Erie):** about $40.2 million of adjusted EBITDA in the first quarter of 2026 alone (roughly a $160 million annual run-rate), up about 31% year over year. This is the crown jewel and the segment the whole company is being rebuilt around. Short-line rail trades at premium multiples (mid-teens EV/EBITDA and up) because of its toll-road-like, hard-to-replicate franchise.
- **Jefferson Terminal:** about $14.4 million of adjusted EBITDA in the quarter (roughly $55-60 million annualized), with management pointing to incremental contract wins.
- **Repauno:** still a small loss (about negative $2.3 million in the quarter) because it is mid-construction, but with phase-two operations targeted for late 2026 and an $80 million EBITDA contribution flagged for 2027 — i.e. optionality, not a current drag worth capitalizing.

The rail segment alone, at even a conservative 9-10x its roughly $160 million run-rate EBITDA, supports an enterprise value in the $1.4-1.6 billion range; at the premium 13-15x that comparable short-lines command, it is closer to $2.1-2.4 billion. Against roughly 118 million shares and a current price of about $4.95 (a market capitalization of only about $585 million), the rail franchise plus Jefferson comfortably backstops the equity even before the power sale — **provided the leverage is cleaned out, which is precisely what the Long Ridge sale accomplishes.** That is the key dependency: the floor is real but it is a *post-deleveraging* floor. With the Long Ridge debt still on the books (total debt was about $3.81 billion at quarter-end against only about $38 million of cash), the balance sheet is the genuine risk, and a deal-break would leave the parent more constrained. The Catalyst-Watch floor of $3.90 lines up exactly with the 52-week low and represents the "deal slips, leverage worry returns, sentiment resets" scenario — a credible, evidence-grounded floor rather than a round number.

## 3. Management credibility on hitting the date

FTAI Infrastructure is externally managed and shares DNA with the Fortress/FTAI complex, whose operators have a long track record of executing asset sales and corporate carve-outs (FIP itself was spun out of FTAI Aviation in 2022). They have already executed one large rail acquisition (the roughly $1.05 billion Transtar/W&LE build-out) and are now executing the matching divestiture. The strategic logic is coherent and self-consistent across recent quarters: buy and integrate rail, sell the capital-intensive power asset, delever, become a pure-play. That consistency raises confidence they will push to close inside the window. The credibility caveat is the recent earnings quality: Q1 2026 carried a large net loss (about negative $127 million, an EPS miss versus a roughly negative $0.35 consensus) and total adjusted EBITDA fell year over year ($70.6 million versus $155.2 million) — though management notes a 25-day planned power-plant outage masked roughly $10 million and that the four core segments still produced about $79 million. The headline loss is noisy (non-cash and one-time items around the transaction), not a sign the deal is wobbling.

## 4. Red flags / green flags

🔴 **Leverage is the standing risk.** Total debt of about $3.81 billion against about $38 million of cash is heavy; the entire thesis leans on the sale closing to fix it. A break re-arms this risk.
🔴 **Regulatory gate (FERC/antitrust) is the one true unknown** and the likeliest slip vector; a slip past November 30 would require an extension and would test patience.
🔴 **Counterparty is a crypto-cycle-exposed buyer (MARA);** a severe digital-asset drawdown is the tail that could pressure the buyer.
🔴 **A small dividend ($0.03/quarter)** and a history of dilution at this kind of holding company — watch for any equity issuance, though the deal is designed to *reduce* the need for it.
🟢 **The catalyst is signed and dated, not aspirational** — the single most important fact here.
🟢 **The downside floor is operating-asset-backed** (rail + terminal cash flows), not a cash-shell or a CVR game.
🟢 **The deal mechanically deleverages** — $1.16 billion of debt removed plus $300 million of paydown — which is exactly what the equity needs and what unlocks the rail re-rate.
🟢 **Strategic coherence and a credible operating sponsor** with a track record of closing transactions.

## 5. What the director must weigh before sizing

This is a clean, defined-risk event with asymmetric payoff: a signed sale closing inside a hard window, a live price (about $4.95) well below a realistic target (the $9-10 area implied by a deleveraged pure-play rail re-rate and consistent with the $10-11 analyst targets), and a floor (about $3.90) that is backed by operating cash flows rather than hope. The single thing to weigh is **regulatory timing**: the asymmetry is excellent if the deal closes by Q3-Q4 2026 as guided; the main way to be wrong is a FERC/antitrust slip that pushes resolution out and lets the leverage overhang reassert itself — which is a timing risk, not a thesis-break, because the operating floor survives a delay.

Moat and secular framing are largely not applicable to a defined-risk event of this kind — short-line rail is in fact a genuinely wide-moat, durable franchise (the opposite of a melting terminal value), and there is no structural secular force impairing it. The "value-conviction" question here is simply the margin of safety to the verified operating-asset floor, and that margin is real.
