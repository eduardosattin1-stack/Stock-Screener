# Forensic Dossier — Prosus N.V. (PRX.AS)

*A deep dive on management and the setup, built from six consecutive Prosus earnings calls (November 2023 through November 2025) cross-checked against the screener's hard financials.*

Prosus is the Amsterdam-listed consumer-internet holding company carved out of South Africa's Naspers. Its single most valuable asset is a roughly 23% stake in Tencent, the Chinese internet giant; around that it owns a collection of operating businesses — iFood (Brazilian food delivery), OLX (online classifieds), Swiggy and PayU (India), eMAG (eastern-European e-commerce), and, since late 2025, Just Eat Takeaway. The investment question is not really "is the operating business good." It is "will the gap between Prosus's stock price and the value of what it owns ever close?" Read the dossier with that frame.

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story changes character almost exactly at the handover from interim chief executive Ervin Tu to permanent chief executive Fabricio Bloisi in mid-2024, and the shift is the most important thing in these transcripts.

**The Tu era (late 2023 – mid 2024): apology and repair.** The early calls are defensive and confessional. Tu opens the November 2023 call by noting Prosus has "come a long way since our peak losses just 12 months ago." He is blunt about mistakes — that they "paid 2021 prices for businesses that were not worth those valuations" in EdTech, "right in front of the disruptive wave of GenAI," and that OLX Autos and Delivery Hero "have not panned out the way they were intended." The recurring themes are getting e-commerce to its first-ever profit, eliminating the Naspers/Prosus cross-holding, and running an "open-ended share repurchase program" to shrink the share count while the discount is wide. This is a clean-up narrative.

**The Bloisi era (late 2024 – now): offense, ecosystem, and a profit ramp.** Bloisi arrives "from a traffic jam in Sao Paulo" and the tone flips to growth, culture, and ambition. The new organizing idea is the "ecosystem" — sharing technology, customers, and best practice across the portfolio companies (he points to Despegar already drawing about 5% of revenue from the iFood ecosystem and targets 10-15%). By November 2025 the ambition is explicit and large: build "at least $100 billion outside of Tencent" and "one of the best tech companies in the world," with "a couple of billion dollars of profit in the next few years." Two growth drivers were introduced and kept: artificial-intelligence-driven operating efficiency (the in-house assistant "Toqan," large commerce models for OLX) and a string of acquisitions to feed the ecosystem (Despegar, La Centrale, and the big one — taking Just Eat Takeaway private in November 2025).

What was quietly dropped: the language of contrition. The 20%-plus return-on-investment target that Tu repeated is mentioned less; the EdTech post-mortem fades. The constant across both eras is the buyback and the unaddressed elephant — the discount to net asset value.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

The financials here are unusually treacherous because Prosus's reported net income is dominated by non-operating items — gains booked when it sells down Tencent or revalues stakes — not by the cash its businesses earn. Read the headline numbers with that warning.

| Management claim | What the numbers show | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "E-commerce is profitable for the first time ever" (Jun 2024), now ramping to "$1.1-1.2 billion in adjusted EBITDA this year ex-JET" (Nov 2025) | Revenue is genuinely growing — +12.9% year over year on the latest scan, gross margin 42.5% and expanding; trailing-twelve-month free cash flow about $3.27 billion. The operating turn is real and visible in the cash line. | **Matches — the operating story is corroborated.** |
| "Net income / profit is exploding" (the optics) | Reported net margin of ~200% and net income of $12.4 billion on $6.2 billion of revenue is arithmetically impossible from operations — it is overwhelmingly stake-sale gains and Tencent equity accounting. Return on invested capital is **-1.2%**: as a standalone operator Prosus does not yet earn its cost of capital. | **Story is far richer than operations justify — the profit headline is an accounting artifact, not earnings power.** |
| "Our buyback is compounding value per share" | This one is real and large: about $42 billion repurchased since 2022, roughly 30% of the free float, with management citing ~18% accretion to net-asset-value per share. Net cash of about $4.4 billion (cash ~$22.2 billion against ~$17.8 billion of debt) funds optionality. | **Matches — the per-share math genuinely works while the stock trades below asset value.** |
| "We will close the discount through execution" | The stock sits about 23% below its 200-day average and near the bottom of its 52-week range; the discount to net asset value is still ~35%. Two years of "consistent execution" rhetoric has not yet moved it. | **Diverges — the central promise is the one the tape has refused to validate.** |

The honest read: the businesses are turning, the cash generation is real, the capital allocation is shareholder-friendly — and none of it has yet closed the only gap that matters for the share price.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Trajectory: Rising — sharply, at the CEO change.** Tu (Nov 2023) is measured and defensive: "I am very happy with our operating performance, but I am not happy with our returns, which... have been unsatisfactory." By the Bloisi calls the register is unmistakably offensive: "execution, execution, execution" (Nov 2025), "I intend to reduce 10x more our corporate costs" (Jun 2025), and the repeated, almost evangelical "we are just getting started." The shift from defensive language (efficiency, fixing, discipline) to offensive language (growth, ecosystem, $100 billion ambition) is the clearest tonal signal in the file. The risk that comes with it: Bloisi is, by his own admission, "naturally optimistic," and the calls increasingly lean on culture and vision rather than the cold capital-return math that Tu foregrounded.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Reliability: Medium-to-High on operations, Low on the discount.** Bloisi made a specific, checkable promise — "I promised you $400 million and delivered $443 million" (Jun 2025) — and a year-ahead claim that Prosus would earn "more profit than the dividends" and reach "multiple billion dollars of profit," which the trajectory now supports (the EBITDA guide stepped from $443 million to a $1.1-1.2 billion range within eighteen months). On the operating profit ramp, management has delivered and modestly over-delivered. The screener's earnings-beat rate of 3 of 6 quarters (50%) reflects the noise in the stake-driven bottom line more than the operating cadence. Where credibility is weakest is the standing claim, repeated every call since 2023, that consistent execution will compress the discount — that has simply not happened, and management openly concedes "it will not disappear overnight."

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

One question dominates every call and management never fully answers it: **what will you do with the cash, and will you return the excess?** HSBC's Chris Johnen pressed it directly (Dec 2024) — the cash pile had grown "from $10 billion, $12 billion, $14 billion now to $17.5 billion" and "could be put to better use than just the positive carry trade." Management's answer is consistently philosophical deflection: Bloisi's "I'm not here in $15 billion for three years, I'm here for five months," and Tu's "that's not the world we're in today." The second pressure point, newer and sharper, is **generative-AI risk to classifieds** — BNP's Will Packer flagged (Nov 2025) that classifieds shares "sold off sharply" after OpenAI's Developer Day and Rightmove's AI profit warning, asking directly whether AI threatens OLX. Management's answer (OLX is expanding margins *because* of AI, not despite it) is plausible but is exactly the kind of claim that later turns into bad news if the disruptor thesis proves right. The third recurring worry is the **Just Eat Takeaway turnaround** — management openly admits "Jet is not performing well" and could share no numbers, only culture and confidence.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 **The discount is structural, not a mispricing management can talk away.** Two full years of execution and $42 billion of buybacks have not closed a gap that persists for hard reasons — the South African parent overhang, Chinese-stock and capital-control risk embedded in the Tencent stake, and capital-allocation uncertainty.

🔴 **Capital-allocation discipline is asserted, not yet proven on the acquisitions.** The pivot from "return the cash" to "deploy it into the ecosystem" (Despegar, La Centrale, the ~$4 billion Just Eat Takeaway take-private) reverses the very discipline Tu preached; the Just Eat turnaround is unproven and the EdTech / OLX Autos / Delivery Hero history shows this team can overpay.

🔴 **The bottom line is opaque by construction** — stake-sale gains masquerade as profit, and return on invested capital is negative.

🟢 **The buyback genuinely compounds value per share while the stock is below asset value** — this is the single best feature of the setup, and it is real and ongoing (~$94.5 million repurchased in a single mid-June 2026 week).

🟢 **The operating turn is real:** first-ever e-commerce profit, gross margin expanding, iFood's core delivery business at a ~28% operating margin, OLX growing profits ~61% on ~18% revenue growth.

🟢 **A fortress balance sheet** — net cash of about $4.4 billion plus roughly $10-11 billion of deployable firepower — means no solvency or refinancing risk (Altman-Z 4.6).

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The most telling subtle shift is in **how management talks about Tencent versus the rest of the portfolio.** Bloisi (Nov 2025) all but pre-announces the funding mix of the buyback: "selling less Tencent and more other companies is good capital allocation because we believe much more in the growth of Tencent." Read plainly, that is management signaling it will harvest the *operating* businesses to fund repurchases and keep the Tencent stake — a quiet admission that, AI ambitions notwithstanding, Tencent remains the crown jewel and the e-commerce assets are partly a source of recyclable cash. A second signal: the vocabulary moved from "emerging markets" to "growth markets" (Jun 2024) and then to a tight three-region focus — Latin America, India, Europe — with "95% of the energy and capital" there; the sprawl is being deliberately pruned. Third, the arrival of new voices (CFO Nico Marais replacing Basil Sgourdos, more EBITDA/EBITDA-reconciliation disclosure) signals a deliberate push to make Prosus look more like a conventional operating company that analysts can model, and less like an opaque holding vehicle — itself an attempt to attack the discount through transparency.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Trajectory: PIVOTING** — from a defensive holding-company clean-up under Tu to an offensive operating-company-plus-ecosystem build under Bloisi, with the buyback as the constant.

The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: **this is a discount-compression bet on a holding company whose value is dominated by a single Chinese asset, where the discount has stayed wide for structural reasons that management's execution has not yet touched.** You are paid to wait by an accretive buyback, but the re-rating catalyst is soft — there is no contractual event that forces the gap to close. Size it as a patient, asymmetric value position, not as an event-driven special situation.

On the **economic moat**: Prosus itself has essentially *no* franchise moat — it is a capital allocator, and its return on invested capital is negative. The moat lives inside the underlying assets: Tencent's network and platform moat (wide but outside Prosus's control), and narrower network/scale moats at iFood and OLX. The blended picture is a **narrow** moat, and the trend is **stable-to-improving** as the operating businesses scale into profitability. On **secular threat**: the genuine structural question is generative-AI disruption of online classifieds (the OpenAI/Rightmove tell that analysts flagged) — a *material* but not terminal risk, since food delivery and payments are less exposed and Tencent is, if anything, an AI beneficiary. The cheapness is therefore best read as a fear-and-structure discount on a genuinely valuable asset base, not a melting-ice-cube value trap — with the caveat that the discount may simply be a permanent feature of the structure.
