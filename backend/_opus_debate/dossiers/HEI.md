# HEICO Corporation (HEI) — Forensic Dossier

*A deep dive on management and the setup, built from six consecutive earnings calls (May 2025 through May 2026) cross-checked against the actual financials.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

HEICO is a family-run aerospace and defense aftermarket company. Its core business is making and distributing FAA-approved replacement parts (called PMA parts — Parts Manufacturer Approval, meaning a part HEICO is licensed to make as a cheaper alternative to the original-equipment maker's version) and repairing aircraft components. It runs two segments: the Flight Support Group (the aftermarket parts and repair engine, about two-thirds of revenue) and the Electronic Technologies Group (defense, space and niche electronics, about a third).

Across these six quarters the story did not shift — it intensified. Every single call opened with the word "record." In mid-2025 the emphasis was on the digestion and over-delivery of the Wencor acquisition (a large aftermarket-parts deal closed in 2023) and on a steady cadence of bolt-on acquisitions funded entirely by internal cash. By the most recent call (second quarter of fiscal 2026, reported 2026-05-28), the narrative had broadened to three simultaneous tailwinds: a commercial-aviation aftermarket boom ("more planes in the sky"), a multi-year defense restocking cycle ("replace depleted stocks," explicit references to the proposed ~$1.5 trillion defense budget and "Golden Dome" missile-defense content), and a space ramp. Management also began monetizing the recent Middle East oil shock as a *positive* — higher fuel costs push airlines toward HEICO's cheaper PMA parts ("the simple one-word answer to that is yes," Eric Mendelson, Q2 2026, on new customers approaching them).

Nothing material was quietly dropped. The only softening note: management flagged a roughly $15–20 million defense "pull-forward" of sales into Q2 2026 and a modest Middle-East-related slowdown, but framed both as trivial against record demand.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

The defining feature of this name is that the narrative is, if anything, *more conservative* than the numbers — the reverse of most companies.

| Management claim | The actual evidence | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Record results, quarter after quarter, year after year" | Revenue 1.87B (FY21) → 4.49B (FY25), a 3-year compound growth rate of ~26.6%; EPS 2.21 → 4.90, ~22% per year; trailing-twelve-month diluted EPS now 5.60 with the latest quarter up 48% year-on-year | **Fully corroborated.** Among the most credible growth records in the dataset. |
| "Real cash, not GAAP earnings" (the recurring family mantra) | Free cash flow grew ~40% year-on-year, ~25.5% over three years; trailing free cash flow 926M; FCF margin ~19% | **Corroborated.** Cash conversion is genuine, not accounting-driven. |
| "We delever quickly to fund the next deal" | Net-debt-to-EBITDA fell from 2.06x (Oct 2024) to 1.60x (Oct 2025) even after ~$630M of deals, then rose to ~1.74–1.79x on more acquisitions — exactly the "lever up, delever, repeat" rhythm described | **Corroborated.** Net debt is ~2.38B (a liability), but coverage is strong; the solvency score (Altman-Z) is a very safe 8.6. |
| "We don't do a profit grab; margins come from operating leverage" | Gross margin ~39.8% (the scan flags it as contracting), but operating leverage is real: G&A fell as a percent of revenue, and Q2 2026 operating income rose 41% on 25% sales growth | **Mostly corroborated.** Margin expansion is volume-and-mix driven, as claimed; the gross-margin softness is mix, not pricing erosion. |
| Returns justify the premium | Here the story is *poorer* than the multiple implies: return on equity ~14%, return on invested capital ~10%. These are good, not spectacular — the heavy goodwill from serial M&A weighs on capital returns | **Partial divergence.** The quality is in growth and cash, not in headline capital returns. A 10% ROIC does not, by itself, support a ~56x price-to-free-cash-flow multiple. |

The single most important forensic point: the business genuinely delivers, but the **price** has run far ahead of even this excellent record. Fair-value estimates cluster well below the market price — the company's own discounted-cash-flow model implies a fraction of today's price, and every value method in our screener shows a negative margin of safety. The stock trades at roughly a 9% *premium* to even a generous Buffett-style fair value.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Trajectory: Rising, and unusually so.** Confidence climbs call over call.
- Q2 2025: "We are filled with deep optimism" (Victor Mendelson).
- Q3 2025: "robust double-digit organic growth … we remain very optimistic."
- Q4 2025 / Q1 2026: the calls turn into a tribute to the late founder Larry Mendelson, with the sons reaffirming the "do the right thing" culture and a "36-year 23% compound annual growth rate in share price."
- Q2 2026: "firing on all engines … business is very strong for us virtually across the board."

The language is relentlessly offensive (growth, investment, market share), never defensive. Guidance remains deliberately qualitative — HEICO famously gives little formal guidance — but the directional confidence only strengthened. The one humble note is the repeated insistence that they take no one-time charges and under-reserve nothing, a credibility signal rather than a hedge.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Reliability: High.** HEICO does not issue precise numeric guidance, so the test is whether the qualitative promises ("strong organic growth," "accretive deals," "margin leverage") materialize. They do, every quarter. The financials show a 7-of-7 quarterly earnings-beat rate (100%). Organic growth ran 12–19% across the period; acquisitions were repeatedly described as accretive within a year and the consolidated numbers bear it out. There is mild, structural sandbagging — management consistently sounds measured and then prints records — but it is the honest kind. The CFO's candor on technical points (stock-comp amortization timing, the cash-neutral leadership-compensation-plan distributions that optically dented operating cash flow in Q1 2026) reinforces the high mark.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Analysts are not fighting management on execution — they are probing for the *ceiling*. Recurring themes:
- **Margin headroom:** persistent questions on how high ETG and FSG margins can go (Carlos Macau eventually nudged his FSG range up to "24% to 26%"). This is a bullish pressure point, not a worry.
- **Centralized purchasing / scale efficiencies:** Kristine Liwag (Morgan Stanley) pushed on whether HEICO could centralize its supply chain for savings; management declined ("I don't see that as being a direction … in the next 3 to 5 years"), accepting a known cost to protect its decentralized culture.
- **Pricing restraint:** analysts repeatedly noted HEICO sells PMA parts at 50–70% discounts to the original-equipment maker and asked why it doesn't raise prices; Eric Mendelson explicitly refused to "do a profit grab." This is left money on the table — a deliberate moat-investment, but a real near-term margin cap.
- **Dual-class structure:** the perennial Class A vs. common question. Management called Class A "a screaming value" and noted directors buy it, but signaled no change.

No topic that management deflected has yet turned into bad news. The genuine open question analysts circle is simply valuation, which management waves off ("we're not into … comparing ourselves to others hoping to get a higher multiple").

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🟢 100% earnings-beat rate; organic growth accelerating into Q2 2026 (Flight Support +19% organic).
🟢 Cash generation is real and growing faster than earnings; deleveraging discipline intact.
🟢 Founder-family alignment, decades-long culture, no history of one-time charges — a rare clean ledger.
🟢 A genuine, regulator-backed moat (FAA PMA approvals are slow and hard to win) with a multi-year defense and space demand tail.

🔴 **Valuation is the overwhelming red flag** — ~56x price-to-free-cash-flow, ~63x forward earnings; every intrinsic-value method shows a negative margin of safety. The stock sits 84% up its 52-week range, +10% above its 200-day average.
🔴 **Insider selling, properly measured:** on an open-market basis there were 21 small buys (~17,600 shares) against 13 sells of ~230,500 shares. The directors' "screaming value" purchases are real but tiny next to the net selling.
🔴 Gross margin flagged as contracting and margins sit ~1.45 standard deviations above their own historical peak — limited room to surprise upward on profitability.
🔴 A ~$15–20M defense "pull-forward" into Q2 2026 borrows from future quarters.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

- The **co-CEO succession is now complete and seamless.** Founder Larry Mendelson passed away; the Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 calls handle this with composure and no operational wobble — the strongest possible "key-man-risk-retired" signal.
- A subtle **shift toward "new space" and "defense tech" customers** (explicit mentions of supplying disruptors, Golden Dome content, Artemis II hardware). Management is positioning HEICO as a supplier to *both* incumbents and insurgents — a deliberate hedge against which side of the defense market wins.
- The CFO's repeated, unprompted explanations of why reported cash flow understated true cash (leadership-comp-plan distributions are investing inflows, not operating uses) signal a management team that polices its own optics honestly.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Classification: STRENGTHENING.** Organic growth, margins, cash flow, and deleveraging all improved across the window, and key-man risk was retired cleanly.

The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: **this is a verified, best-in-class compounder trading at a price that already discounts many years of that compounding.** The forensic quality is a 5; the entry price is the problem. There is no special-situation catalyst here — the "catalyst" is simply the continuation of a known, fully-priced growth story.

**Moat:** intangible/regulatory — FAA PMA approvals and a sole-source aftermarket position, the same architecture as TransDigm. It is genuinely **WIDE**, but the trend is best read as **STABLE** (durable, not visibly widening): gross margin is contracting and capital returns (ROIC ~10%) are diluted by acquisition goodwill. **Secular threat: none** — aftermarket aerospace parts are not in the path of AI displacement, fintech, cord-cutting, or energy transition; if anything, fleet aging and cost pressure on airlines *help* the PMA model. The cheapness question is moot because there is no cheapness — this is a great business at a demanding price, not a fear-print and not a value trap.
