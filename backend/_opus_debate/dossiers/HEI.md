# HEICO Corporation (HEI) — A Deep Dive on Management and the Setup

*Forensic dossier prepared for the investment committee. Six consecutive earnings calls reviewed (second quarter fiscal 2025 through second quarter fiscal 2026), cross-referenced against the screener's hard financials.*

---

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story barely moved across these six quarters — and that is itself the most important finding. HEICO is a serial compounder whose core message has been identical for, by management's own telling, 36 years: generate excess cash from decentralized operations, fund organic growth without debt, and redeploy the cash into accretive bolt-on acquisitions. Across every call the Mendelson family (co-CEOs Victor and Eric, with CFO Carlos Macau) repeats the same four pillars — aftermarket replacement parts (PMA, or Parts Manufacturer Approval, which are FAA-cleared substitutes for original-equipment parts), parts distribution, defense/space electronics, and disciplined M&A.

What *did* evolve is the intensity and the breadth of the demand picture. In the second quarter of fiscal 2025 the tone was "very pleased / record results." By the fourth quarter of fiscal 2025 it had escalated to "16% organic growth — strong would be an understatement." By the second quarter of fiscal 2026 (May 2026) Victor declared the company "firing on all engines… business is very strong for us virtually across the board." Three growth drivers were progressively layered in and amplified, not dropped: (1) the Wencor integration ("the gift that keeps on giving"), now cross-selling and exceeding plan; (2) a multi-year defense restocking tail tied to allied rearmament and budget expansion ("Golden Dome," missile defense, drones); and (3) space, framed selectively ("a lot of profitless opportunity there… we've been pretty good at avoiding those").

One genuinely new arc element: founder Larry Mendelson, the long-time chairman, passed away — eulogized at length on the fourth-quarter fiscal 2025 call. The sons spent unusual airtime reaffirming that his "do the right thing" culture is fully embedded, an implicit succession-risk inoculation.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

The unusual verdict here: the narrative is, if anything, *poorer* than the numbers justify. Management consistently under-sells. The financials corroborate or exceed nearly every claim.

| Management claim | What they said | What the financials show | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sustained high growth | "record results" every quarter; 12–17% organic | Revenue 3-yr CAGR +26.6%; latest +16.3% YoY; trailing-twelve-month diluted EPS $5.60, latest-quarter EPS +48% | Corroborated, arguably understated |
| Cash generation is the real story ("real cash") | OCF = 130% of net income (third quarter fiscal 2025); cash flow up 43% (second quarter fiscal 2026) | Free cash flow +40.3% YoY, 3-yr CAGR +25.5%; FCF margin 19.2%; trailing free cash flow $926M | Corroborated |
| Deleveraging despite heavy M&A | net debt/EBITDA fell from 2.06x to as low as 1.60x | Net debt $2.38B today (recomputed); Altman-Z solvency 8.6 (very safe) | Corroborated |
| Margin leverage off a flat cost base | G&A down as a % of revenue; "busting at the seams" | Net margin 15.4%; operating income +41% on +25% sales (second quarter fiscal 2026) — clear operating leverage | Corroborated |
| Disciplined, accretive M&A | 5 deals in fiscal 2025, more in fiscal 2026; "accretive within a year" | Returns are the soft spot — ROIC just 10.0%, ROE 14.0%; goodwill-heavy roll-up dilutes returns on capital | Mixed — see below |

The one place the story is *richer* than the numbers: capital efficiency. A 10% return on invested capital is unremarkable for a business the market prices as an elite compounder. The roll-up model (buying ~5 companies a year, carrying heavy goodwill and acquisition amortization) keeps reported ROIC modest even as EPS compounds. The compounding is real; the *return on each incremental dollar of capital* is good, not extraordinary. Gross margin is also flagged "contracting" (39.8%) in the scan — consistent with Exxelia/Gables-style deals that dilute segment margins on amortization, which Carlos openly acknowledged.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Rising.** Confidence climbed steadily and is now near a multi-year peak.
- Second quarter fiscal 2025: "filled with deep optimism."
- Third quarter fiscal 2025: "humbled… we continue to win in the marketplace."
- Fourth quarter fiscal 2025 (post-Larry's death): emotional but resolute — "more optimistic than ever about this company's prospects."
- First quarter fiscal 2026: a rare didactic monologue on the "secret sauce" (never taking one-time charges, properly reserving) — confident enough to lecture.
- Second quarter fiscal 2026: peak — "firing on all engines," "over the moon."

Notably, the language stayed *offensive* (growth, investment, market-share gains) the entire stretch. There was no pivot to defensive cost/macro talk. Guidance specificity is deliberately low — HEICO famously gives no formal numeric guidance — but the qualitative posture grew more assertive, not more hedged. The only defensive note is honest, not evasive: management repeatedly flagged that Middle East / oil shocks create "short-term disruption" and that China briefly halted PMA purchases after the April tariffs (a message, "not… a long-term strategy").

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**High.** HEICO does not issue formal guidance, so the test is whether the qualitative promises matched delivery — and they did, repeatedly. The screener records a perfect beat rate (7 of 7 quarters, 100%). Every "we expect this acquisition to be accretive within the year" was followed by record consolidated results. The recurring "Wencor exceeding expectations" claim is borne out by Flight Support Group organic growth that ran 12–19% across the window. There is no sandbagging *pattern* in the cynical sense; rather, a culture that structurally under-promises (no guidance, conservative reserving) and over-delivers. Carlos's CapEx and stock-comp commentary (1.5–1.6% of revenue CapEx, amortization tapering in fiscal 2027) has been precise and consistent. Reliability: High.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

Analysts pushed hardest on three things, and management answered candidly rather than deflecting:
- **Margin ceiling.** Repeatedly asked how high Flight Support and Electronic Technologies margins can go. Carlos raised his Electronic Technologies range to 24–26% (second quarter fiscal 2026), up from prior quarters — a genuine, quantified upgrade, not a dodge.
- **Pricing-grab opportunity.** Kristine Liwag (Morgan Stanley) pressed on whether HEICO could reset its deep PMA discounts higher. Eric explicitly declined — "that's really not the HEICO way… I would not model that kind of activity." A real, if conservative, answer that caps a bull lever.
- **Pull-forward / China pre-buying.** Asked whether record numbers borrowed from the future. Management quantified a modest $15–20M defense pull-forward (second quarter fiscal 2026) and denied China stockpiling. Honest, specific.

The one structurally unaddressed worry: the dual-class share structure (Class A trades at a persistent discount; Gautam Khanna asked about collapsing it). Management said "status quo," acknowledging the Class A is "a screaming value" but declining any action — a minor governance overhang, not a thesis-killer.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🟢 Perfect delivery: 7/7 earnings beats; record results literally every quarter in the window.
🟢 Cash quality: operating cash flow routinely exceeds net income; free cash flow compounding +25%+; the "real cash, not GAAP" obsession is backed by the numbers.
🟢 Rapid deleveraging (2.06x → 1.60x) *while* spending $630M+ on deals — a rare combination.
🟢 Honest accounting culture, openly explained (proper inventory reserving, no one-time charges in 36 years).
🟢 Succession risk addressed head-on after the founder's death; the bench (working-supervisor model) is described as deep and scalable.

🔴 Valuation, not the business, is the flag: ~56x price-to-free-cash-flow (scan) / ~61x trailing earnings — every intrinsic-value method in the screener fails (DCF, earnings power value, owner-earnings, Graham all at −62% to −100% margin of safety). The stock prices several more years of flawless compounding.
🔴 Modest return on capital (ROIC ~10%) for a premium multiple — the roll-up math caps incremental returns.
🔴 Gross margin tagged "contracting"; acquisition amortization is a structural drag.
🔴 Insider open-market activity skews to selling: 21 buys (17,613 shares) vs 13 sells (230,496 shares) — directors buy small amounts of cheap Class A for optics; net dollar flow is sharply outward.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

- **Margin guidance crept up, quietly.** The Electronic Technologies margin range moving to 24–26% (from prior framing) is the most material new datapoint buried in the Q&A — a real upgrade management didn't headline.
- **Defense is the genuinely new wave.** Earlier calls led with commercial aftermarket; by fiscal 2026 the defense backlog ("multi-year tail," Golden Dome, allied restocking) is described as accelerating and showing up in orders — a mix shift toward a higher-visibility, higher-margin vertical.
- **Selective on space.** Management is unusually disciplined about avoiding "profitless" space opportunity — a tell that they will not chase the most hyped end-market for growth's sake.
- **No centralization, on purpose.** When pushed on supply-chain centralization savings, Carlos refused ("not worth it… disrupting our happy customer base") — the decentralized model is a deliberate moat choice, not inertia.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**STRENGTHENING.** Every operating and cash metric is accelerating, the M&A engine is compounding, leverage is falling, and management credibility is as high as it gets. This is a genuinely elite business run by aligned, honest operators.

**The single most important thing to weigh before sizing:** this is a quality verdict, not a price verdict. HEICO is firing on all cylinders *and the market knows it* — the stock just surged ~12% on the second-quarter beat, is up ~30% in a month, and trades at roughly 56x free cash flow / 61x earnings with a negative margin of safety on every intrinsic model. You are not being offered a mispriced compounder; you are being asked to pay a full, demanding multiple for confirmed excellence. The risk is not that the business disappoints — it is that the multiple compresses even if it keeps delivering.

**Economic moat.** WIDE and durable. The moat is twofold: a regulatory-license / intangible barrier (the FAA-approval engineering capability for PMA parts is hard-won and decades-deep, with entrenched OEM-platform positions) plus switching-cost stickiness in mission-critical aftermarket components. The evidence supports width: net margin 15.4%, free-cash margin 19%, expanding operating leverage, and 36 years of ~23% share-price compounding. The one caveat is that *return on invested capital* (~10%) is held down by the goodwill-heavy roll-up, so the moat shows up in growth and cash conversion more than in headline ROIC.

**Moat trend: STABLE-to-widening.** Margins are expanding (operating income +41% on +25% sales) and management is raising its margin outlook; the modest "contracting gross margin" is acquisition-amortization mix, not competitive erosion. The franchise is not being structurally impaired.

**Secular threat: manageable / none.** There is no AI-displacement, disintermediation, or channel-shift force eroding aerospace aftermarket terminal value; if anything, an aging global fleet, supply-chain scarcity, and allied rearmament are structural *tailwinds*. The cheapness question does not apply — HEICO is not cheap. This is a high-quality, fully-priced compounder, not a value trap and not a fear-print.
