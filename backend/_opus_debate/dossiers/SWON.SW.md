# Forensic Dossier — SoftwareOne Holding AG (SWON.SW)

*One earnings call available: full-year 2025 results, reported 2026-03-31. This is a single-quarter read, not a multi-quarter arc, so the trajectory work below leans more on the financial trajectory the screener supplies than on a sequence of calls. A note on identity: the peer-group file in our system tags SWON.SW as "Swisscom AG" — that is wrong. SWON.SW is SoftwareOne Holding AG, a Swiss-listed software and cloud reseller and services firm that, in mid-2025, merged with its Norwegian rival Crayon. Every number and quote below is SoftwareOne's.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The single call we have is dominated by one story: 2025 was "transformational" because SoftwareOne combined with Crayon to create what management calls "a global software and cloud leader," with roughly CHF 14 billion of combined gross sales, more than 70,000 clients across 70-plus countries, and about 13,000 employees. The reported income statement blends twelve months of SoftwareOne with six months of Crayon (the deal consolidated from July 1, 2025), which is why headline revenue jumped about 22.5% year over year to CHF 1.24 billion even though the underlying, like-for-like growth of the combined business was a modest 1.4%.

The arc within the year is a recovery story. Management is at pains to show that growth troughed early — hurt by Microsoft cutting the incentives it pays resellers on enterprise agreements — and then accelerated through the year, "back to positive territory" by the third quarter and reaching 11% revenue growth in the fourth. The drivers they lean into now are the transition from those lost enterprise-agreement incentives toward cloud subscription (CSP) and services-based incentives, a fast-growing channel (distribution) business led by Asia-Pacific, and a newly announced role as the first global authorized distributor for Google Cloud. AI is introduced as an emerging tailwind rather than a delivered number — they talk about partnering with the likes of Anthropic and OpenAI and about Microsoft price increases helping their cost-optimization pitch.

The central forensic tension is the chasm between a ~21% *adjusted* profitability story and a *reported* net margin of essentially zero. The cash flow statement rescues the picture — this business converts revenue to cash well — but the income statement has not produced reliable earnings in years (a five-year EPS decline of about 70% a year, zero earnings beats in the last seven quarters).

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

With only one call, this is a snapshot, not a trend. The tone is confident and forward-leaning: "2025 was transformational," "the actions we are taking are working," and on the worst region, North America (-12.6% in 2025), the CFO says he is "very confident" growth returns in 2026 and expects Q1 to beat Q4. Management is specific where it helps them (synergy run-rates, the Google Cloud distribution win, segment growth rates) and notably reassuring on the Microsoft incentive headwind that scared the market in 2024 — they now say they "do not see any headwind effect" from enterprise-agreement changes in 2026. The posture is offensive (growth, AI, channel expansion) rather than defensive, though the heavy reliance on the word "adjusted" is itself a quiet defensive tell.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

We cannot track a multi-call guide-versus-deliver record from one transcript, so credibility has to be inferred. Two things cut against management: a hard beat record of 0 of 7 quarters (per the screener) and a long history of erratic net income. Two things cut for them: the 1.4% like-for-like growth "ahead of our initial expectation of broadly flat," and adjustment costs that came in below the promised CHF 30M ceiling. The cleanest credibility signal is external and current: subsequent reporting shows the company *raised* its 2026 outlook (to mid-to-high single-digit constant-currency revenue growth and an adjusted EBITDA margin target above 23%, up from ~20.9%), with synergies tracking to the top of the range. On balance, reliability is Medium — the cash and synergy delivery is real, but the long record of weak reported earnings keeps this short of High.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

The Q&A clusters on exactly the right worries. Analysts pressed on (a) whether the Microsoft enterprise-agreement headwind is truly behind them — management says yes, even a possible tailwind in 2026; (b) further impairments after the CHF 8M LATAM write-down — the CFO says none are expected because they test at the regional level annually and would have taken them already; (c) exceptional/adjustment costs in 2026 — they reiterate the below-CHF-30M ceiling but explicitly carve out Crayon integration costs, which is the soft spot, since those carve-outs are where the reported-versus-adjusted gap lives; and (d) the timing of the North America turnaround, which is promised but not yet shown.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 Reported net margin of essentially zero (0.1%) against a ~21% adjusted figure — the adjustments are large and recurring-looking (integration, acquisition, amortization), so "adjusted" profitability flatters a business that has barely earned a net profit in five years.
🔴 A weak solvency score (Altman-Z around 0.6) and a low quality score (Piotroski 3 of 9) — though the Altman reading is distorted by the merger accounting and the genuine net-cash position.
🔴 North America fell 12.6% in 2025 on self-inflicted sales-execution problems; the recovery is a promise.
🔴 A history of zero earnings beats in seven quarters and a 70%-a-year five-year EPS decline.
🟢 Genuinely strong, cheap cash flow: trailing FCF about CHF 228M at roughly 5.2x, a 16.3% FCF margin, and FCF up sharply year over year.
🟢 A real net-cash balance sheet (about CHF 360M) — rare for a company this optically distressed.
🟢 Concrete, partly-delivered self-help: CHF 64M of synergies in hand against an CHF 80–100M target, and a margin guide raised to above 23%.
🟢 A credible strategic logic — scale in software/cloud distribution, the Google Cloud authorized-distributor win, and a real AI-advisory angle.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

The most important subtle signal is structural, not verbal: this company spent 2023–2024 in a wrenching takeover fight. Private-equity firm Bain Capital made repeated bids (down to about CHF 18.8 per share); the board rejected them; the founding shareholders, who control roughly 29%, ousted that board to reopen the door — and then, in March 2024, dissolved their group with Bain and walked away. The Crayon merger is what management did *instead* of selling. So the takeover catalyst that once animated this name is spent, and the company is now a self-help integration story, not a deal story. The second signal is linguistic: the relentless framing of profitability as "adjusted" and the explicit carve-out of Crayon costs from the adjustment ceiling — management is steering attention to cash and run-rates and away from a still-thin reported bottom line.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

Classify the trajectory as turning (PIVOTING): the business is mid-pivot from a lost Microsoft-incentive model and a failed sale process into a larger, merger-built, cash-generative reseller with a credible margin-expansion plan. The single most important thing to weigh before sizing is the reported-versus-adjusted earnings gap: you are underwriting cash flow and a self-help synergy plan, not a clean, growing GAAP earnings stream — so the thesis lives or dies on whether the CHF 80–100M of synergies and the >23% adjusted-margin target actually flow through to reported profit over the next several quarters, and on a North America turn that is promised but unproven.

On the economic moat: SoftwareOne is a reseller and services intermediary. Its edge is real but narrow — switching costs and procurement complexity (clients lean on it to navigate Microsoft, AWS, Google, Adobe and now multi-vendor and sovereign-cloud rules), plus newly enlarged scale. But the returns evidence is mixed: return on invested capital is about 10.7% (decent) while net margins are near zero and gross margin is contracting (44.1%, flagged contracting). That is a narrow moat, roughly stable, not a wide one. The genuine secular question is AI: large language models and agentic tools could compress the value of "good-enough" advisory and software-procurement work over time. Management actually leans into this as opportunity (guiding customers on which AI models to deploy), and a procurement/licensing intermediary is less directly displaceable than a knowledge-work shop — so the structural threat is manageable, not terminal, but it is the right thing to watch.
