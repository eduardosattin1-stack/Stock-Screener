# Allied Gold Corporation (AAUC) — Event Dossier

**Setup:** Cash takeover (merger arbitrage). This is a special situation, not a bet on
the mining business itself. Allied Gold has signed a binding agreement to be bought by
Zijin Gold International for **C$44.00 per share in cash**. The whole thesis is the gap
between today's price and that cash payout, and whether the last regulatory door swings
open before the deadline.

## 1. The event, in plain terms

Zijin Gold International (the Hong Kong-listed gold arm of China's Zijin Mining) agreed to
acquire all of Allied Gold's shares for **C$44.00 in cash** under a court-approved plan of
arrangement (the Canadian legal process for a friendly takeover). The deal is friendly —
Allied's board recommends it, and the company's shareholders have already voted it through
by a wide margin, with both leading proxy-advisory firms (Institutional Shareholder
Services and Glass Lewis, the firms that advise large funds how to vote) recommending a
yes vote.

What remains is **regulatory clearance**, not a vote. That is why our catalyst board tags
the driver as a foreign-regulator gate.

## 2. Is the catalyst real, dated, and binding?

Yes on all three.

- **Real and binding:** a signed arrangement agreement at a fixed C$44.00 cash price, with
  the shareholder vote already passed. There is no financing contingency of note — Zijin is
  a large, cash-generative miner and the parties have amended Allied's credit facility to
  carry the company to closing.
- **Dated:** the outside date — the contractual last day to close before either side can
  walk — was extended once, from May 29 to **July 29, 2026** (about six weeks out as of late
  June 2026). Any further extension needs both sides to agree. This is a firm, scheduled
  backstop, which is exactly what a hard-dated catalyst means.

## 3. How credible is the path to closing?

The approval stack is most of the way done:

- **Canada — DONE.** Investment Canada Act clearance has been received, which completes the
  Canadian side. This was the most-watched approval given a Chinese state-linked buyer of a
  Canadian-domiciled company, and it cleared.
- **Regional African competition — DONE.** Merger clearances obtained from the West African
  (ECOWAS) and East/Southern African (COMESA) competition authorities.
- **Host-country approvals — mostly done.** Allied's mines sit in Mali, Côte d'Ivoire,
  Ethiopia and Egypt. Management describes these host-country approvals as "obtained or at
  advanced stages." A handful of these foreign sign-offs, plus customary closing conditions,
  are the only remaining gate.

The single extension and the careful "advanced stages" language are the reason the market
still leaves a wide gap to the cash price — residual foreign-regulatory friction in
multiple emerging-market jurisdictions, with a Chinese acquirer, is the live risk.

## 4. The downside floor — what actually backstops the price if it fails

If the deal broke, Allied would re-rate back to a standalone gold producer. The bull case
for the floor is that gold has run hard in 2026 and Allied's own business plan has improved
since the deal was struck — the same reasons management walked away from an earlier,
cheaper Ambrosia private placement in 2025. The realistic floor is around the recent
unaffected trading level, roughly **US$23** on the New York line, which is also close to
where the US shares trade today. That is a thin cushion below the current price, so the
defined-risk floor here is "recovery value as a standalone miner in a high gold-price
environment," not a contractual minimum.

## 5. The numbers (US line, NYSE American)

- Live price: about **US$23.0–25.3** (the US-listed shares closed near US$23.01 on
  June 25, 2026; the catalyst board's working live is 25.26).
- Cash deal value: C$44.00, which converts to roughly **US$31.5** at prevailing exchange
  rates — the target.
- Floor: about **US$23**, the recent unaffected / standalone level.

The spread from the working live to the target is wide for a deal this far along — that
width is the asymmetry, and it exists because the last regulatory steps are foreign and
not yet fully in hand.

## 6. Red flags / things to watch

- **Foreign-regulator risk is the whole ballgame.** One extension has already happened; a
  second slip past July 29 is possible if a host-country approval lags. That would not break
  the deal but would stretch the timeline and the annualized return.
- **Chinese acquirer optics.** Although Canada has cleared, geopolitics around a
  state-linked Chinese buyer of African gold assets is a non-zero tail.
- **Thin floor.** The cushion below today's price is modest; the downside protection leans
  on a strong gold tape holding up, not on cash-in-hand.

## 7. Moat / secular — not applicable

This is a defined-risk arbitrage on a cash takeover. Business quality, moat width and
secular-decline themes do not carry the thesis; the cash price and the regulatory clock do.
