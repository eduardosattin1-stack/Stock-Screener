# Alten S.A. (ATE.PA) — Forensic Dossier

*A deep dive on management and the setup. Alten is a French engineering and research-and-development (R&D) consulting firm — it rents out engineers to design products for aerospace, defence, automotive, telecom and life-sciences clients. Roughly 51,000 engineers across 35-plus countries. No bundled transcript was supplied; this dossier is built from the company's published 2025 annual results (February 2026), its first-quarter 2026 activity release and call, management's investor presentation, and the screener's financial trajectory.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The story has flipped from "structural compounder" to "cyclical trough, awaiting a turn." For most of the last decade Alten told a simple, credible growth story: a global engineering-services champion compounding revenue at double digits through organic hiring plus steady bolt-on acquisitions, with operating margins on activity (the company's preferred profit measure — operating profit before share-based pay and one-offs, as a share of sales) parked around 10%. That story peaked in 2022, when revenue jumped to €3.78B and net income to €458M.

Since then the narrative has narrowed sharply. By the 2025 annual results the message was defensive: revenue of €4.10B was down 1.1% as reported and down 4.5% on an organic basis (stripping out acquisitions and currency), margin on activity slipped to about 8.5%, and net income collapsed to €107M. Management reframed the year as a cyclical air-pocket concentrated in a few end-markets — German automotive engineering, telecom and semiconductors — rather than a loss of competitive position, pointing to France, Iberia and Italy holding up as proof the franchise is intact.

The most recent communication, the first quarter of 2026, is a "trend-reversal-in-progress" narrative. Management now leads with aerospace and defence reaccelerating (defence up almost 10% year on year), banking and finance turning positive, and a stated hope that the worst end-markets stabilise by year-end. The driver quietly dropped is organic hiring momentum; the driver newly emphasised is acquisitions — a just-signed €60M-revenue, 470-consultant data/cloud deal plus "more than 5 deals underway." The engine has shifted from organic engineering growth to a wait-for-the-cycle-and-buy-the-dip posture.

## 2. CLAIMS vs FINANCIALS (forensic)

| Management claim | What the numbers show | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "Resilient franchise, just a cyclical dip" | Revenue −1.1% reported / −4.5% organic in 2025; revenue essentially flat at €4.1B for three years (2023 €4.07B, 2024 €4.14B, 2025 €4.10B) | Partly supported. The top line has stalled, not collapsed — consistent with a cycle, not a structural unwind. But three flat years is a long "dip." |
| Margins are merely "normalising" toward 10% | Margin on activity fell to ~8.5% (2025) from the ~10%+ peak; net margin just 2.6%; net income −43% year on year to €107M | Concerning. The profit fall is far steeper than the revenue fall — operating leverage runs in reverse because bench engineers (paid but not billing) are a fixed cost. |
| Cash generation stays robust | Free cash flow €239.9M (5.9% of revenue); net cash improved to €390M from €276M | Strongly supported. The most credible bullish fact: even in a bad year the business throws off real cash and the balance sheet got stronger. |
| Acquisitions will refuel growth | A large pending deal lifts the headcount math toward ~154,000 "altogether"; a €60M-revenue deal signed Q1 2026, consolidating in Q3 | Supported but unproven. Integration risk and the price paid are not disclosed. |
| 2026 EPS ~€8.5, 2027 ~€9.29 | 2025 reported EPS €3.03 (annual) vs a trailing-twelve-month €8.36 in the screener; the gap reflects share-count and one-off differences | Needs scrutiny. The €8.5 figure implies a sharp earnings rebound that depends entirely on the cyclical turn arriving and margins recovering. |

The honest read: the revenue story matches a cyclical-trough thesis, but the profit story is worse than a clean cycle would produce, and the EPS-rebound guidance is a hope, not yet a delivery.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Trajectory: Declining, with a tentative late-cycle bottoming tone.** Through 2022-2023 management spoke offensively about growth and hiring. By the 2025 results the language had turned defensive — margin discipline, cost control, "stabilisation." The Q1 2026 call was candid to the point of bluntness: the quarter was "in mixed conditions, not really good," German auto engineering "decreasing by 30% year-to-year," telecom in "a very strong decrease" of −18%, and on semiconductors a striking admission — "we are bad, and we do not have the same strategy to develop those accounts." That is unusually un-spun for a management team. The confidence that does appear is narrow and conditional: aerospace and defence growth is real, but the recovery call rests on "we are hoping" language and refuses precise full-year guidance ("we have no precise guidance for this time").

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Reliability: Medium.** The screener notes a roughly even beat rate (one of two quarters), and the multi-year record shows management hit its growth-and-margin framing in up-cycles but is now visibly behind its own ~10% margin aspiration. The candour on weak segments is a credibility positive — they are not hiding bad news. The forward risk: management has put concrete EPS numbers (€8.5 in 2026, €9.29 in 2027) and an "approximately 10%" margin target on the table while simultaneously guiding first-half organic growth to between 0% and −0.5%. Flat-to-down revenue with a sharp EPS rebound in the same year is an internally demanding combination that leans on margin recovery and acquired earnings, not organic volume.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

The recurring pressure points: (1) when German automotive and telecom actually bottom — management can only offer "by the end of the year"; (2) the margin path, where management declined a precise target beyond "improvement versus 8.6%"; (3) the shape and price of the large pending acquisition. The unaddressed worry that should concern a capital allocator is structural rather than cyclical: whether artificial-intelligence coding and design tools erode the billable-hours model over time. Management offered no commentary on AI's impact on the staffing model — a notable silence for an engineering-labour business in 2026.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 Net income down 43% on a revenue decline of barely 1% — severe reverse operating leverage from the bench-cost model.
🔴 Three consecutive flat-to-down revenue years; the "dip" is long enough to question whether the prior growth rate was cyclical, not structural.
🔴 Telecom −18%, semiconductors −8% with an admitted strategy gap, German auto engineering −30% — multiple end-markets weak at once.
🔴 No management engagement with the artificial-intelligence threat to billable engineering hours.
🟢 Free cash flow of €239.9M (5.9% of sales) and net cash rising to €390M — genuine financial strength in a bad year.
🟢 Aerospace and defence reaccelerating (defence +~10%), a durable multi-year tailwind given European rearmament.
🟢 Unusually candid management — they name the weak segments plainly rather than spinning them.
🟢 Disciplined, opportunistic acquirer adding capacity cheaply while the cycle is soft.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

Terminology has shifted from "growth" to "stabilisation" and "back to growth by the end of the year" — a tell the team is managing expectations down to a beatable bar. Capital allocation has rotated from organic hiring to mergers and acquisitions, with management volunteering an unusually full pipeline ("more than 5 deals underway"). That is a defensive use of the balance sheet: when you cannot grow the bench profitably, you buy revenue. The "several hundred engineers missing compared to end-2025 levels" remark hints management deliberately let the bench shrink to protect margin — sensible, but it caps how fast they can ride a recovery.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Trajectory: STABLE, late-cyclical.** This is a financially sound, well-run, cash-generative business in a genuine cyclical trough, not a balance-sheet or governance problem. The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: this is a *cyclical* call dressed as a *value* call. The cheapness (about 5.5x price-to-free-cash-flow) is real, and the net cash and free cash flow are real, but the re-rating requires the German-auto / telecom / semiconductor cycle to turn — a soft, undated, macro-dependent driver, not a hard corporate catalyst. There is no activist, no asset sale, no buyback program forcing the issue.

**Moat: NARROW.** Alten's edge is intangible — deep client relationships, a large reusable bench of specialised engineers, and scale in regulated verticals like aerospace and defence (long qualification cycles, security clearances). But returns are slipping: return on invested capital sits around 10-11% and gross margin (about 8%, contracting) reflects a labour pass-through model with limited pricing power. A return on capital that is decent but *falling*, with margins compressing, is a narrow moat under pressure, not a wide one.

**Moat trend: ERODING.** Margin on activity has fallen from roughly 10%+ to about 8.5%, and net income has more than halved over two years. Whether that reverses with the cycle is the whole debate.

**Secular threat: MATERIAL (not yet terminal).** The structural risk is labour-arbitrage deflation — artificial-intelligence tools that automate engineering and software tasks, decoupling client value from the billable headcount Alten rents out. Aerospace, defence and regulated R&D are the most defensible niches (safety-critical, certified, relationship-heavy), which is why this is "material" rather than "terminal." But the silence on AI and the eroding margin mean the cheapness is part fear-print, part early warning. A low multiple on a base that may be structurally (not just cyclically) shrinking is the value-trap risk a director must price.
