# Ball Corporation (BALL) — A Deep Dive on Management and the Setup

Ball is now a pure-play aluminum beverage-can maker. It sold its aerospace arm to BAE Systems for $5.6 billion in cash (closed February 2024), and what remains is a global packaging business in three pieces: cans for the Americas and Asia (about 51% of 2025 revenue), cans for Europe (about 32%), and metal food and household-products packaging in the Americas (about 17%). The story management tells is simple and, on the operating evidence, largely true: aluminum is steadily taking share from glass and plastic, Ball is growing volumes faster than the market in every region, and the company is funneling almost all of its cash into buying back its own stock. The question for capital is not whether the business is sound — it plainly is — but whether the current price already pays for that soundness.

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

Across the four quarters of 2025 the core story stayed remarkably consistent, which is itself a signal. In the first quarter (May 2025 call), then-CEO Dan Fisher framed the year around delivering "11% to 14% comparable diluted EPS growth" and "record adjusted free cash flow," leaning on the "resilient and defensive nature of our global business." By the second quarter (August 2025) the guidance band had been nudged up to "12% to 15%," and by the third and fourth quarters that 12–15% target held and was ultimately delivered as a reported 13% (a record $3.57 of comparable EPS for the full year).

The one genuine narrative shift is leadership, not strategy. The August 2025 call introduced an interim CFO (Dan Rabbitt) after the prior CFO departed; by the February 2026 year-end call a new CEO, Ron Lewis — a 20-year Coca-Cola supply-chain veteran and a former Ball can-buyer — had taken the chair, with Rabbitt confirmed as permanent CFO. Lewis's first act was to reaffirm continuity: "our strategy is intact and it is working." He also recast the long-term algorithm slightly more conservatively, to "10-plus percent annual comparable diluted EPS growth," versus the 11–15% bands management ran during 2025. Two growth drivers were quietly added late in the year: the Benepack acquisition of two European can plants (closed late January 2026), and a planned new facility in Oregon to serve a re-contracted Northwest customer in 2027 and beyond.

The forensic core: the operating business does what management says — volumes grow, the substrate shift is real. But the value-creation engine is financial engineering (buying back stock with cheap-looking but debt-laden capital), not high returns on capital. A 6.7% return on invested capital does not justify a premium multiple.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

Trajectory: Stable-to-Rising on operations, with a clean leadership handoff. Q1 2025 was hedged — "mindful of ongoing uncertainties related to tariffs and consumer pressures." By Q2 and Q3 the tone firmed as volumes beat: Q3's "we now expect… to exceed the top end of our long-term 1% to 3% volume growth range." The Q4 2025 call (Lewis's debut) was the most confident of the set — "we've never been stronger" operationally — but notably trimmed the forward algorithm to "10-plus percent," a small but honest tempering. Management consistently chose defensive framing for the macro (the can as the affordable, recession-resilient package) while staying offensive on volume — a coherent posture, not a contradiction.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

High. The 2025 EPS-growth band was raised mid-year (11–14% to 12–15%) and then delivered at 13% — the rare case of a company raising guidance and still beating it. The screener confirms a 7-for-7 quarterly earnings-beat record (100%). There is a mild sandbagging pattern: each quarter's volume guide was set conservatively and then exceeded, which is a credibility positive. The one caveat is the "adjusted free cash flow" framing, which presents a flattering cash number by excluding aerospace tax payments — credible on EPS, generous on cash.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

The recurring pressure point is margins and operating leverage. RBC's Arun Viswanathan pushed repeatedly on the "2-to-1" rule (operating profit should grow twice as fast as sales) and on North American mix headwinds — management conceded North America "underperformed" in the short term (Q2 2025) on unfavorable mix (energy and non-alcoholic cans are lower-priced than beer) and tariff-driven plant inefficiency in Mexico. The second pressure point is the stock price itself: an analyst directly asked Fisher about "the disconnect between what you think you can deliver and what the stock is reflecting" (Q3 2025), to which he answered only that the company believes it is "very cheap." The third is 2027 contract risk — management disclosed that one "fairly sizable" North American contract comes due in 2027, leaving that year only ~75% contracted versus ~90%+ for 2026.

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 Return on invested capital of 6.7% — at or below cost of capital; the premium multiple is not earned by returns.
🔴 "Adjusted" free cash flow masks a much lower all-in cash figure once aerospace tax payments are counted (trailing free cash flow $596 million vs the touted $956 million).
🔴 Net debt of roughly $7.08 billion (~2.75x EBITDA) funds the buyback; EPS growth leans on share-count reduction, not profit growth.
🔴 Insider behavior is net selling on the open market (1 small buy of 1,551 shares vs 2 sells of 21,378 shares) — no conviction signal from those who know best.
🔴 A sizable North American contract renews in 2027; 2027 is only ~75% contracted.
🟢 Volume growth beat guidance every quarter of 2025 (+4.1% full year), validating the aluminum substrate-shift thesis.
🟢 Clean, well-telegraphed leadership handoff to a credible industry insider (Lewis) with continuity of strategy.
🟢 Earnings beat in 7 of 7 quarters; guidance raised mid-2025 and still beaten.
🟢 Defensive end-market (beverages) with genuine recession resilience — Lewis: consumers "are going to spend their money on food and beverages… not travel."

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

Two terminology shifts matter. First, the forward EPS algorithm was quietly walked down from the 11–15% bands of 2025 to "10-plus percent" under the new CEO — a realistic reset that tells you the easy post-divestiture comparisons are behind them. Second, management repeatedly flagged a deliberate category re-mix in North America, moving "from kind of 40% to 30%" alcohol exposure toward energy and non-alcoholic — a margin-dilutive trade (those cans carry lower price points) made for volume durability. The new CEO's repeated emphasis on "EVA" (economic value added — profit above the cost of capital) as the "North Star" is telling given the 6.7% return on invested capital: it reads as a commitment to fix exactly the weakness the numbers expose.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

Trajectory: STABLE. This is a well-run, defensive, scale-advantaged packaging business executing a credible plan — but it is a low-return business whose per-share growth is manufactured primarily by debt-funded buybacks, trading at a full price (about 21.6x price-to-free-cash-flow, a slight premium to peers Crown Holdings at ~18–20x and Silgan at ~15x). The single most important thing the director must weigh before sizing: the headline growth is financial, not economic — at a 6.7% return on invested capital, every dollar reinvested barely clears its cost, so the stock works only if the multiple holds and the buyback continues, not because the underlying business compounds capital at attractive rates.

The moat is real but modest: a scale-and-cost advantage plus multi-year customer contracts (a switching-cost element), not a brand or network. Returns are the tell — a 6.7% return on invested capital with thin, stable ~15% gross margins is a narrow, durable-but-low-return moat, neither widening nor structurally eroding. There is no terminal secular threat here; if anything the secular wind (glass/plastic-to-aluminum substrate shift, sustainability tailwind) blows in Ball's favor. The cheapness question is therefore moot — the stock is not cheap. This is a fairly-priced quality-utility, not a mispriced bargain.
