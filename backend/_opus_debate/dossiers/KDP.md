# Keurig Dr Pepper (KDP) — Forensic Dossier (Event-Driven Special Situation)

*Prepared by the Interrogator. Reviewed as of June 29, 2026. The thesis on the table is not "is this a great business" — it is whether a corporate break-up unlocks more value than the live price reflects, on a firm enough timetable to bet on.*

## The setup in one paragraph

Keurig Dr Pepper is a roughly $45 billion North American beverage-and-coffee group (Dr Pepper, 7UP, Snapple, Canada Dry on the soda side; Keurig, Green Mountain on the coffee side). On April 1, 2026 it closed an $18.4 billion acquisition of JDE Peet's, the European coffee giant (Jacobs, L'OR, Peet's, Douwe Egberts), and announced that once the combined coffee business was assembled it would split the whole company into two separately listed firms: a North American refreshment-beverage company (about $11 billion in annual net sales) and the world's largest pure-play coffee company (about $16 billion in net sales). The investment case here is the classic "sum-of-the-parts" trade — the belief that two focused companies, valued separately and picked up by index funds that must own each new ticker, are worth more than the conglomerate trades for today. The catalyst that is supposed to force that re-rating is the spin itself plus the buying pressure when each new company enters the indexes.

## 1. Is the catalyst real, dated, and binding?

Real: yes. This is a board-approved, publicly committed plan, not a rumor. Management has named the two companies, assigned leadership, and is actively building the separation.

Dated: no — and this is the crux. The spin has no record date and no effective date. Worse, the goalposts have already moved. When the deal was announced, management guided to a separation in "late 2026," with teams targeting "operational readiness to separate by year-end 2026." As of the company's June 23, 2026 update, the target has slipped to "early 2027." A catalyst that was a few months out at the start of the year is now a year out, and the language remains soft — the separation is explicitly conditioned on reaching "appropriate leverage levels at each company" and "supportive market conditions." Those are management's words for "we will do this when the balance sheet and the market let us," which is the opposite of a hard, contractual date.

Binding: only loosely. A spin is a corporate decision the board can delay, restructure, or pause if conditions sour. There is no counterparty, no merger agreement with a drop-dead date, no regulator on a clock, no contractual payout. Unlike a merger arbitrage (where signed terms cap and floor the outcome) or a drug-approval date (where a regulator must rule by a deadline), nothing external forces KDP to complete this split on any particular day.

## 2. Credibility of management on hitting the timetable

Mixed, and trending the wrong way in the very near term. Two concrete red flags:

- The timeline has already slipped once (late-2026 to early-2027) inside the first quarter after the deal closed. Serial slippage is the signature of a soft catalyst.
- On June 23, 2026, the executive picked to run the future coffee company — Rafa Oliveira, the JDE Peet's veteran heading the coffee operating unit — announced he is leaving at the end of July 2026 for an outside chief-executive job. The board has had to open a fresh search for the Global Coffee Co. chief executive, led by the chairman. Losing the named leader of the larger of the two spin entities, months before the spin, is a governance and execution wobble, not a confidence signal. Existing CEO Tim Cofer is back-filling oversight of coffee during the transition and will run the beverage company after the split.

On the other hand, the strategic logic is sound and the financing is in place: the acquisition was funded with roughly $9 billion of new long-term debt, about $8.5 billion of equity capital (including a $4.5 billion convertible preferred from Apollo, KKR and T. Rowe Price), and the assumption of about $5 billion of JDE Peet's bonds. The plan is internally coherent. The issue is timing risk, not whether the company intends to do it.

## 3. The verified downside floor

This is where KDP differs from a typical "cheap-and-bad" special situation — there is a real business under the trade, which is what backstops the price if the spin drags. KDP is a profitable, dividend-paying consumer-staples company: about $45 billion market value, a price-to-earnings ratio around 25, a dividend yield of roughly 2.75 percent ($0.92 a year), and a 52-week range of about $24.88 to $35.94. Even if the spin is delayed a year or quietly restructured, you still own a cash-generative staples franchise paying you to wait. The realistic floor is the "no-catalyst, just-the-business" value — the lower part of the recent trading range, around the high-$20s, where the stock found support over the past year and where the dividend yield alone would draw buyers. The dossier-board floor of $28.5 is consistent with that: it is roughly the recent support zone, not a distressed liquidation price.

The caveat: the post-deal balance sheet is levered (combined net leverage around 4.5x earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization). A staples business can carry that, but it removes some of the cushion and is precisely why management is gating the spin on "appropriate leverage levels" — they need to pay debt down first. Higher leverage means the floor is firmer than a broken biotech but softer than a net-cash situation.

## 4. Red flags

- Undated, serially-slipping catalyst (late-2026 to early-2027 already).
- Spin explicitly conditioned on leverage and "market conditions" — discretionary, not binding.
- Departure of the designated coffee-company chief executive weeks before he was to be installed; open CEO search.
- Elevated leverage (~4.5x) post-acquisition narrows the margin of safety and is itself the gating constraint on the spin.

## 5. Green flags

- Genuine, board-committed sum-of-the-parts story with a credible value-unlock logic (the world's #1 pure-play coffee company plus a clean North American soda business).
- Real, profitable, dividend-paying business as the floor — you are not paying for a lottery ticket on a shell.
- Financing fully arranged; sophisticated capital partners (Apollo, KKR, T. Rowe) underwrote the preferred.
- The spread is modest but real: live price about $33 versus a sum-of-the-parts target near $38.

## 6. Capital-allocation verdict for the director

This is a sound break-up thesis attached to a soft, undated, recently-slipped catalyst. The asymmetry is fine but not dramatic — roughly $33 today against a high-$30s target and a high-$20s floor, a reward-to-risk a touch under two-to-one. The single most important thing to weigh before sizing: do not treat this like a dated, binding special situation. There is no hard date, the date has already moved once, and the leader of the larger spin-entity just walked. This belongs on the watch list, sized for patience, with the trigger being a concrete record/effective date for the spin and stabilized coffee-company leadership — not bought as if a clock were ticking.

On the standard quality and moat axes (mostly not the point here): the underlying franchise is a wide, stable consumer-brand moat with no terminal secular threat — soda and single-serve coffee are durable, slow-growth categories. But the trade is the event, not the moat, so those tags are secondary.
