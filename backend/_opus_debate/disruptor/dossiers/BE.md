# Bloom Energy (BE) — Forensic Dossier

*Interrogator dossier — six consecutive quarterly transcripts, Q1 2025 (2025-04-30) through Q1 2026 (2026-04-28), cross-referenced against the screener financial trajectory and TTM/latest-quarter overrides.*

## 1. NARRATIVE ARC

The Bloom story underwent a clean, evidence-backed metamorphosis over these six quarters — from a *defending-the-guidance* story into a *demand-can't-be-met* story, and the financials ratified it rather than lagging it.

- **Q1 2025 (defensive footing).** The framing was reassurance against macro/tariff fear. KR Sridhar spent the call insisting "We don't see any slowdown," reiterating 2025 guidance ($1.65–1.85B revenue, ~29% GM), and quantifying tariff drag as "up to 100 basis point impact on our gross margin for the year." The thesis was *on-site power is now accepted as a necessity* — "that briefcase is closed." Notably, this quarter also carried a **CFO departure** (Dan Berenbaum exiting May 1) with only an Acting Principal Financial Officer to replace him — a governance soft-spot at the exact moment the business inflected.
- **Q2 2025 (proof points arrive).** The narrative pivoted from defense to evidence. The **Oracle** purchase order ("power available to their first data center in 90 days"), the **AEP / AWS / Coralogix** Ohio deployment, and the **BBB tax-credit restoration** (flat 30% ITC) all landed. Management announced the doubling of factory capacity "from 1 gigawatt a year now to 2 gigawatts a year by the end of next year."
- **Q3 2025 (the "become the standard" thesis).** KR formalized a **"7 distinct AI ecosystem channels"** playbook (hyperscalers, electricity providers, gas providers, colocation, neoclouds, data-center developers, infrastructure owners), each anchored by a lighthouse customer. The **Brookfield $5B initial investment** ("preferred on-site provider for Brookfield's trillion-dollar infrastructure portfolio") was introduced. Oracle was delivered "in 55 days against a 90-day commitment." Trillion-dollar TAM language entered.
- **Q4 2025 (backlog inflection + 800V DC moat).** Product backlog **+140% YoY to ~$6B**, ~$14B service backlog, eight straight quarters of service profitability, service GM hitting 20%. The **800-volt DC** native-output argument ("Bloom, and only Bloom, natively produces 800 volts DC today… physics requires it") was introduced as a new technical moat. Geographic backlog shifted from >80% CA/Northeast to >80% lower-cost states — evidence of penetration beyond high-price niches. A **permanent CFO still not named.**
- **Q1 2026 (escape velocity).** The **Oracle Project Jupiter** announcement — "up to 2.45 gigawatt power block… 100% bloom," replacing planned gas turbines and diesel — reframed Bloom as a *gigawatt-scale islanded-microgrid* provider, not a kilowatt/megawatt distributed-generation vendor. Guidance **raised materially**. Capacity ceiling lifted again: "Our current manufacturing footprint will allow us to deliver 5 gigawatts of product annually." And the new permanent **CFO (Simon Edwards)** finally appeared. The tell of the quarter: "today, we are not order constrained and not capacity constrained. The pace of our revenue growth is decided by how fast our customers can build."

The arc is unusually coherent: every expansion of the story (channels → backlog → gigawatt deals → 5 GW capacity) was accompanied by a hard data point, not just rhetoric. Nothing material was *quietly dropped* — the one thing that disappeared (the CFO vacancy) was resolved, not buried.

## 3. TONE & CONFIDENCE TRAJECTORY

**Trajectory: RISING (decisively).**

- **Q1'25 — defensive/reassuring.** "We don't see that changing anytime soon"; heavy tariff/macro hedging; "we are keeping a close eye on this segment [retail]." Confidence present but on the back foot.
- **Q2'25 — offensive.** "tangible evidence"; "AI companies need power at AI speed"; named customers (Oracle, AEP, AWS, Quanta).
- **Q3'25 — expansive.** "once-in-a-generation opportunity… become the global standard"; "trillion-dollar market"; "still in the early innings."
- **Q4'25 — assertive/structural.** "Bloom is rapidly becoming the standard"; introduces 800V DC as a physics-mandated moat ("physics requires it").
- **Q1'26 — peak confidence.** "the headline numbers as strong as they are, are a lagging indicator"; "not order constrained and not capacity constrained"; closing line: *"If you bet against any one of the statements that I made, you can bet against Bloom. Otherwise, you've got a great ride with us."*

The shift is unambiguously **from defensive (efficiency/cost/macro/tariff) to offensive (growth/capacity/standard-setting)** language. Specificity of guidance *increased* (raised FY26 guide with explicit GM/OI/EPS ranges). The only caution: the CEO's language has become **promotional in register** ("genius of and," "lighting up the planet is a good day job," "great ride with us") — a tone that demands the financials keep ratifying it, because there is now zero margin of safety in the language.

## 4. GUIDANCE CREDIBILITY

**Reliability: HIGH.**

- FY25 original guide ($1.65–1.85B) → **actual $2.02B** = beat the top end.
- Q1'25 commentary explicitly said the quarter "was better than implied by the commentary we provided on our late February call."
- Q1'26 consensus EPS ~$0.13 → **actual $0.44** = 3.4x beat; revenue consensus crushed (+130% YoY).
- Screener corroborates **beat 6/7 quarters (86%)** and **consensus forward EPS growth +104%**.
- The pattern is **conservative-guide / over-deliver (sandbagging-positive)** — management has serially set ranges it then exceeds, and has been transparent about *why* the ranges are wide (project-timing lumpiness: "a project can fall a few days in front of or on the other side of December 31").

The single credibility caveat is structural, not behavioral: guidance is framed in **non-GAAP**, and the company is still **GAAP-unprofitable**. The beats are real on the metrics management chose to be judged on.

## 5. ANALYST PRESSURE POINTS

- **Long-term margin targets.** Q3'25, Mark Strouse (JPM) pushed for gross/operating margin targets under capacity expansion; KR **deflected** ("Wait till 90 days from now"). This was later *answered favorably* (34% GM guide), so the deflection was timing, not evasion.
- **Customer concentration / Oracle sizing.** Repeatedly probed (Q3'25 Michael Blum, Q4'25). KR **consistently refuses to size individual customers** ("I can't speak to any one customer"). Q1'26 he volunteered the mitigant: "well more than half of our current data center backlog comes from *other* hyperscalers, neo clouds and colocation providers" — i.e., Oracle is large but **not a majority** of DC backlog. **No explicit top-customer revenue-share % is disclosed** (see Customer Concentration note below).
- **Related-party revenue.** Q3'25, Ameet Thakkar (BMO) flagged **$288M of related-party revenue** in the 10-Q; CFO attributed it to Brookfield JV equity investments. This is a watch item — revenue recognized with entities Bloom co-invests in.
- **Mix shift to PPA / financing structures.** Q3'25 Colin Rusch (Oppenheimer) probed the shift from direct CapEx sales to PPA/related-party financing — a question about **revenue quality** that management answered but did not fully de-risk.
- **Warrant transactions with hyperscalers (Oracle).** Q4'25, Sherif Elmaghrabi (BTIG) — KR confirmed "not penny warrants… done at market pricing," but the agreement was "still… not executed."
- **Non-AI backlog mix.** Q4'25, Strouse asked for the geographic split of *non-AI* business; KR flatly **refused** ("we just don't do that. Give me another question.").

## 6. RED FLAGS / GREEN FLAGS

🔴 **CFO instability (Q1'25–Q4'25).** Dan Berenbaum exited May 2025; an Acting PFO ran finance for ~3 quarters during the most consequential inflection; permanent CFO (Simon Edwards) only seated by Q1'26. A long finance-leadership gap during a guidance-raising hyper-growth phase.

🔴 **Insider selling, zero open-market buys (screener override).** **0 open-market buys vs 48 sells (~1,029,109 shares).** No insider has bought a single share on the open market into a stock up ~228% YTD. The grant-inflated buy_ratio is noise; the real signal is one-directional selling.

🔴 **Related-party revenue ($288M in Q3'25 alone).** Revenue recognized with Brookfield JVs Bloom co-invests in — quality/circularity watch item.

🔴 **GAAP still loss-making; ROIC −6.7%; P/FCF ~1,094x.** Every intrinsic model (DCF/EPV/Graham/OwnerEarnings/IV15) returns **−100% margin of safety**. The price embeds a near-flawless multi-year execution path.

🔴 **Promotional CEO register.** "great ride with us," "lighting up the planet," "genius of and" — rhetoric has outrun GAAP economics; little margin of safety in the language.

🟢 **Gross-margin expansion on accelerating revenue** (26%→29%→30%→34% guide) — the single most important *moat lie-detector*, and it reads **pricing power**, not commoditization.

🟢 **Backlog inflection** (+140% YoY product to $6B; total $20–24B) with 100% service attach — durable, multi-year revenue visibility and a high-margin annuity tail (service GM 20%, profitable 8 straight quarters).

🟢 **Serial guidance beats** (FY25 above range; Q1'26 EPS 3.4x consensus) — credible, conservative management.

🟢 **Capacity ceiling repeatedly raised** (1 GW → 2 GW → 5 GW deliverable) while management insists capex intensity is low ("ROI for capacity expansion is a few months, not years") — capital-light scaling claim, if true.

🟢 **Third-party-verified demand durability** — hyperscaler capex +77% to $725B (2026), gas turbines sold out to 2028–2030. The theme is not a Bloom assertion; it is corroborated by the entire AI-infrastructure complex.

🟢 **Permanent CFO seated (Q1'26)** — the governance red flag resolved.

## 7. HIDDEN SIGNALS

- **Vocabulary shift from "value proposition vs the grid" → "value proposition for on-site power."** Q1'25 KR explicitly reframed the sales question: "unlike in the past where we had to ask what's our value proposition vis-à-vis the grid, today… what is our value proposition when it comes to customers absolutely needing on-site power?" This is a **TAM-unlock signal** — the buy decision is no longer *whether* to go on-site, only *which* on-site vendor.
- **From "generations" to "continuous improvement."** Q2'25 KR stopped describing the product in version steps: "we don't anymore talk about our product in terms of generations… it is getting healthier and healthier as the days go by." Reframes Bloom as a **semiconductor-style learning-curve** company (4.5 trillion field data points, digital twins) rather than an industrial-product company — a moat-deepening reframe.
- **Scale unit inflated quarter over quarter**: kW/MW distributed gen → 1 GW capacity → 2 GW → "5 gigawatts of product annually" → a single 2.45 GW Jupiter block. The **denomination of the business changed** within four quarters.
- **Competitive framing sharpened**: from "micro turbines / reciprocating engines" (Q1'25) to "their supply to current orders arrive only in 2029 or later" (Q1'26) — management is explicitly leaning on **competitor supply scarcity** (turbine sold-out books) as Bloom's wedge, which the web data confirms.
- **New CFO voice (Simon Edwards)** arrives without disrupting the cadence — and the guide was raised on his first call, a confidence signal.
- **The "lagging indicator" tell (Q1'26):** KR calling record headline numbers "a lagging indicator" is a deliberate signal that he believes the *forward* numbers are even larger — consistent with the serial-beat pattern.

## CUSTOMER CONCENTRATION (explicit)

Bloom does **not disclose a precise top-customer revenue share**, and management actively refuses to size individual accounts ("I can't speak to any one customer," Q3'25). However:
- **Oracle is the single largest named relationship** — Project Jupiter alone is "up to 2.45 GW… 100% Bloom," and the broader Oracle master agreement was expanded to **2.8 GW** (web-confirmed, April 2026), plus a warrant/strategic-partnership structure. This is a **material single-customer dependency.**
- Mitigant (management, Q1'26): "**well more than half of our current data center backlog comes from other hyperscalers, neoclouds and colocation providers**" — i.e., Oracle is large but **below 50% of DC backlog**, and DC is only part of total (C&I backlog grew >135% YoY independently).
- **$288M related-party revenue** (Q3'25, Brookfield JVs) is a separate concentration/quality vector.
- **Net assessment:** concentration is **elevated and rising** (one gigawatt-scale anchor customer plus a co-investing infrastructure partner), partially offset by a genuinely diversifying 7-channel backlog and a large C&I book. This is the chief idiosyncratic risk the director must weigh.

## 8. CAPITAL-ALLOCATION VERDICT (for the director)

**Classification: STRENGTHENING.**

Bloom has gone from defending guidance under tariff/macro fear (Q1'25) to *raising* guidance materially while declaring itself "not order constrained and not capacity constrained" (Q1'26), with the **gross-margin trajectory (26%→34%) confirming pricing power** rather than commoditization, **backlog inflecting to $20–24B**, and **third-party demand data corroborating** the theme. Management's guidance credibility is High (serial conservative beats), and the one governance red flag (a multi-quarter CFO vacancy) has been resolved.

**The single most important thing to weigh before sizing:** *valuation, not the business.* The operating story is genuinely strengthening and theme-load-bearing — but the stock is up ~228% YTD to a ~$72–83B market cap on a name that is **still GAAP-unprofitable (NI −$88M FY25, ROIC −6.7%)**, trading at **triple-digit forward P/E**, with **every intrinsic model at −100% MoS** and **zero open-market insider buying against ~1M shares sold**. The risk is not that the theme is fake — it is real and durable — but that *the price has already discounted the flawless execution of it*, leaving the position acutely exposed to (a) any quarter where project-timing lumpiness or a hyperscaler capex air-pocket interrupts the beat cadence, and (b) the Oracle single-customer dependency. Size for a great business at a dangerous price, not a cheap stock.
